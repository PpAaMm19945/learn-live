# Map 29: Byzantine North Africa (c. 533 AD – 698 AD)
## The Exarchate of Africa: The Last Christian Stronghold

---

## ERA & CHRONOLOGY

**Young Earth Dating:** c. 533 AD – 698 AD (Justinian's Reconquest to Islamic Conquest)  
**Biblical Framework:**
- **Post-Apostolic Era:** The mature North African church (Augustine's legacy) facing internal heresy (Donatism) and external threat (Vandals, then Islam).
- **Prophetic Context:** The "Iron mixed with Clay" (Daniel 2) phase of the Roman Empire—fragmented, brittle, yet persisting in the East (Byzantium).
- **Divine Judgment:** The collapse of Christian North Africa is often viewed through the lens of Revelation 2-3 (removal of lampstands) due to internal strife and loss of Gospel focus.

**Conventional Equivalent:** c. 533 AD – 698 AD (Early Byzantine Period)

**Key Chronological Markers:**
- **533-534 AD** — Vandalic War: Belisarius reconquers North Africa for Emperor Justinian.
- **534-640 AD** — Byzantine Rule (Exarchate of Africa): A period of fortification, church building, and Berber wars.
- **c. 550 AD** — "Three Chapters" Controversy: Theological rift between African bishops and Justinian.
- **642 AD** — Arab conquest of Egypt (isolating North Africa).
- **647 AD** — Battle of Sufetula: First major Arab raid into the Exarchate.
- **698 AD** — Fall of Carthage: Hassan ibn al-Nu'man captures the capital, ending Byzantine rule.

## BIBLICAL & CHURCH HISTORY FRAMEWORK

### The Theology of Empire & Persecution

**Romans 13:1-7 — Submission to Authority:**
> "Let every person be subject to the governing authorities..."
- **Application:** The African church struggled with this under the "heretical" Vandals (Arians) and then the "tyrannical" Byzantines (who imposed heavy taxes and theological compromise).
- **Conflict:** When the Emperor (Justinian) interfered in doctrine, African bishops resisted, citing Acts 5:29 ("We must obey God rather than men").

**Matthew 13:24-30 — The Wheat and the Tares:**
- **Donatist Controversy:** The Donatists wanted a "pure church" and separated from the "Catholic" (universal) church, which they viewed as compromised by traditors (those who handed over Bibles during persecution).
- **Augustine's Response:** The visible church contains both wheat and tares until the harvest. The validity of sacraments depends on Christ, not the priest's perfection.
- **Byzantine Era:** By 533 AD, Donatism was suppressed but still present, weakening the church's unity before the Islamic invasion.

**Revelation 2:5 — Removing the Lampstand:**
> "Repent and do the works you did at first. If not, I will come to you and remove your lampstand from its place."
- **Historical Lesson:** North Africa was the home of Tertullian, Cyprian, and Augustine—a theological powerhouse. Its total collapse under Islam raises solemn questions about spiritual vitality, internal division, and the failure to evangelize the indigenous Berber populations effectively.

## GEOGRAPHIC COVERAGE

### Map Boundaries
- **North:** Mediterranean Sea (Sardinia/Sicily visible)
- **South:** Sahara Desert fringe (Limes Tripolitanus, ~30°N)
- **East:** Gulf of Sidra (Libya, ~20°E)
- **West:** Mauretania (Morocco/Algeria border, ~5°W)

### Geographic Regions

**1. Africa Proconsularis (Zeugitana):**
- **Center:** Carthage. The fertile heartland, heavily urbanized and Romanized.
- **Economy:** The "Breadbasket of Rome" (grain exports continued to Constantinople).

**2. Byzacena:**
- **Location:** Central Tunisia.
- **Site:** Sufetula (Sbeitla). A region of olive groves and fortified towns.

**3. Numidia:**
- **Location:** Eastern Algeria.
- **Site:** Timgad, Hippo Regius. Mountainous, heavily fortified against Berber raids.

**4. Mauretania Prima & Secunda:**
- **Location:** Western Algeria/Morocco.
- **Status:** Loosely held coastal outposts (Septem/Ceuta) surrounded by independent Berber kingdoms.

**5. The Aurès Mountains:**
- **Role:** Stronghold of the Mauri (Berbers) who resisted Byzantine rule.

**1. Carthage (The Capital):**
- **Coordinates:** [36.85°N, 10.32°E].
- **Role:** Seat of the Exarch (Governor). Heavily refortified by Belisarius.
- **Features:** The Byrsa Hill citadel, massive harbor, basilicas.

- **Coordinates:** [35.23°N, 9.12°E].

**3. Hippo Regius (Annaba):**
- **Coordinates:** [36.90°N, 7.76°E].

**4. Theveste (Tébessa):**

**5. Septem (Ceuta):**
- **Coordinates:** [35.89°N, 5.31°W].
- **Role:** Westernmost outpost, guarding the Straits of Gibraltar.

**6. The Aurès (Kingdom of the Masties):**
- **Coordinates:** [35.32°N, 6.64°E].
- **Leader:** Kusaila (later resistance leader).
- **Role:** Independent Berber Christian/Pagan kingdom resisting Byzantine taxation.

## PEOPLES & POPULATIONS

**1. The Romans (Romano-Africans):**
- **Language:** Latin.
- **Religion:** Chalcedonian Christian (Catholic).
- **Status:** Urban elites, landowners, heavily taxed by Constantinople.

**2. The Byzantines (Greeks):**
- **Language:** Greek (administration) / Latin (military).
- **Role:** Soldiers, administrators, tax collectors. Often viewed as foreign occupiers by locals.

**3. The Mauri (Berbers):**
- **Language:** Berber dialects.
- **Religion:** Mixed (Christian, Pagan, Donatist).
- **Status:** Rural tribes, often raiding or allied with Byzantium depending on payments.

**4. The Vandals (Remnants):**
- **Status:** Defeated in 534 AD. Most men deported to fight in Persia; women married Byzantine soldiers. Disappeared as a distinct group.

## HISTORICAL SOURCES & EVIDENCE

### Written Sources
**1. Procopius, *De Aedificiis* (On Buildings):**
- **Content:** Detailed account of Justinian's fortification program in Africa (walls, forts, churches).
- **Bias:** Imperial propaganda, praising Justinian's "restoration."

**2. Procopius, *Vandalic Wars*:**
- **Content:** Eyewitness account of Belisarius' campaign (533-534 AD).

**3. Corippus, *Iohannis*:**
- **Content:** Epic poem describing the Byzantine wars against the Berbers (c. 550 AD). Valuable for Berber ethnography.

**4. Archaeological Evidence:**
- **Fortifications:** The distinctive "Byzantine" style forts (small, high walls, reused stone) found all over Tunisia/Algeria (e.g., Limisa, Timgad).
- **Churches:** Mosaic floors from the 6th century showing a revival of Christian art (e.g., the "Lady of Carthage" mosaic).
- **Coinage:** Gold solidi minted in Carthage, showing the Emperors (Justinian, Heraclius) wearing military garb (reflecting the constant state of war).

## CONFLICTS & RESOLUTIONS

### Conflict 1: The Donatist Controversy (Purity vs. Unity)

**The Conflict:**
- **Donatists:** Believed the church must be "pure." Sacraments performed by "traditor" priests (who lapsed during persecution) were invalid. They rebaptized Catholics.
- **Catholics (Augustine):** Believed the church is a "mixed body" (wheat and tares) until Judgment Day. Sacraments are valid because of Christ, not the priest.

**Biblical Resolution:**
- **Augustine's Victory:** The Council of Carthage (411 AD) and subsequent imperial decrees suppressed Donatism, but the schism weakened the social fabric of North Africa.
- **Lesson:** The demand for "perfect purity" can lead to schism and pride (Pharisaism). The church must balance holiness with grace.

### Conflict 2: Justinian's Reconquest (Liberation or Tyranny?)

**The Conflict:**
- **Liberation:** Justinian claimed to be liberating Catholic North Africa from the Arian Vandal "heretics."
- **Tyranny:** Once in power, the Byzantines imposed heavy taxes to pay for the wars and persecuted those who disagreed with the Emperor's theology (e.g., the Three Chapters).

**Historical Reality:**
- The local population initially welcomed Belisarius but soon grew resentful of the corrupt Byzantine administration.
- **Result:** When the Arabs arrived (647 AD), many locals (especially Berbers and Jews) saw them as just another set of masters, or even preferable to the high-taxing Byzantines.

### Conflict 3: The "Three Chapters" Controversy (Church vs. State)

**The Conflict:**
- Emperor Justinian tried to appease the Monophysites (Eastern heretics) by condemning the writings of three long-dead theologians (the "Three Chapters").
- **African Resistance:** The North African bishops, led by Facundus of Hermiane, refused to sign the condemnation, arguing that the Emperor had no right to change church doctrine or contradict the Council of Chalcedon.

**Biblical Resolution:**
- **Acts 5:29:** "We must obey God rather than men."
- **Heroism:** Many African bishops were imprisoned or exiled for standing up to the Emperor. This demonstrates the fierce independence and theological depth of the African church.

### Conflict 4: The Failure to Evangelize the Berbers

**The Issue:**
- The Church in North Africa was largely Latin-speaking and urban.
- While some Berbers were converted, many inland tribes remained Pagan or superficially Christian (Donatist).
- The Bible was never translated into the Berber language (unlike Coptic in Egypt or Ge'ez in Ethiopia).

**The Consequence:**
- **Fragility:** When the Latin urban centers fell to the Arabs, the church had no deep roots in the indigenous rural population.
- **Comparison:** The Coptic Church in Egypt survived Islam because it was indigenous. The Latin Church in North Africa vanished because it was too tied to the Roman/Byzantine state.
- **Lesson:** The Gospel must be inculturated (translated) to survive the fall of empires.

## PEDAGOGICAL NOTES

### Discussion Questions

1.  **Church & State:**
    - Read Acts 5:29. Was it right for the African bishops to disobey Emperor Justinian? What happens when the government tries to control church doctrine?
    - *Answer Key:* Yes. The church must maintain its independence in matters of faith. State control (Caesaropapism) often leads to compromise.

2.  **The "Wheat and Tares":**
    - Explain the Donatist controversy using Jesus' parable (Matthew 13). Why is the search for a "perfect church" dangerous?
    - *Answer Key:* It leads to pride and schism. We are saved by grace, not by the perfection of our leaders.

3.  **Missiology Failure:**
    - Why did the church in North Africa disappear while the church in Egypt (Copts) survived?
    - *Answer Key:* Language. The Copts translated the Bible; the North Africans kept it in Latin (the language of the elite/occupiers). The Berbers never heard the Gospel in their heart language.

4.  **Prophecy:**
    - How does the "Iron mixed with Clay" (Daniel 2) describe the Byzantine era?
    - *Answer Key:* The empire was strong (Iron) but brittle and fragmented (Clay), unable to hold together against the Islamic "stone."

### Classroom Activities

1.  **Council of Carthage Simulation:**
    - **Roleplay:** Students act as Bishops.
    - **Scenario:** The Emperor orders you to sign a document condemning the "Three Chapters."
    - **Choice:** Sign and keep your job/life, or refuse and go to prison?
    - **Debate:** Discuss the cost of theological conviction.

2.  **Mosaic Art Project:**
    - **Materials:** Colored paper squares or tiles.
    - **Task:** Create a mosaic map of Tunisia.
    - **Feature:** Include a cross for Carthage and a fort for the border.

3.  **"The Last Sermon":**
    - **Creative Writing:** Write a short sermon from the perspective of a Bishop in Carthage in 697 AD, knowing the Arab army is approaching. What scripture would you use to comfort your people? (e.g., Psalm 46).
