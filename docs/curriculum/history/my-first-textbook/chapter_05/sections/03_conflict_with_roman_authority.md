## 5.3 Conflict with Roman Authority (c. 150–300 AD)

In this section, you will examine the reasons behind Roman persecution of the African church and meet some of the courageous martyrs who chose faith over imperial loyalty.

The rapid growth of African Christianity inevitably led to conflict. The Roman state, while generally tolerant of diverse religions, demanded a baseline of civic loyalty. This was expressed through public rituals, chief among them offering a pinch of incense to the *genius* (divine spirit) of the emperor. For Christians, this was not a mere political gesture; it was an act of idolatry. Their refusal to participate was seen by Roman authorities as stubbornness at best, and treasonous atheism at worst.

This tension erupted into open persecution. In 180 AD (conventional estimate), near the town of Scillium in Proconsular Africa, twelve Christians were brought before the proconsul. Their trial, known as the Acts of the Scillitan Martyrs, is one of our earliest records of official persecution in the region. Their confession was simple and unwavering: "We honor the emperor as the emperor, but we fear God." For this, they were executed.

The persecution intensified under Emperor Septimius Severus in 202 AD (conventional estimate). Carthage, the jewel of Roman Africa, became a stage for martyrdom. It was here that two young women, a noblewoman named Perpetua and her enslaved servant Felicitas, faced death in the arena.

> **Key Figure — Perpetua & Felicitas:** Vibia Perpetua was a well-educated noblewoman and young mother. Felicitas was her pregnant, enslaved servant. Arrested together, they faced pressure from family and officials to renounce their faith. Perpetua's prison diary, a rare and powerful document, records her visions and unwavering resolve. Their shared martyrdom in the amphitheater of Carthage became a celebrated story of courage and sisterhood in Christ, transcending social barriers.

Writing in Carthage during this very period, the theologian Tertullian observed the effect of these brutal public spectacles. He famously declared, "The blood of the martyrs is the seed of the Church." His words proved prophetic. Far from stamping out the faith, the persecutions seemed only to strengthen it, attracting new converts who were moved by the Christians' courage and conviction. By 250 AD (conventional estimate), the African church was a powerful and expanding force, yet this very success created new internal challenges, particularly how to handle believers who faltered under pressure.

### Theological Reflection
The suffering of the African martyrs raises a timeless and difficult question: why does God permit His people to endure such persecution? The story of Perpetua and Felicitas offers a profound insight. In their shared faith and death, the rigid social hierarchies of the Roman world—noble and slave—were erased. Their witness was not just about individual salvation, but about the creation of a new kind of community, a family bound by allegiance to Christ alone. This reflects the upside-down nature of the Kingdom of God, where the last are first and the greatest are those who serve. The martyrs' blood did not just "seed" the church with new members; it watered the soil of a radical new society founded on the sacrificial love of Jesus.
