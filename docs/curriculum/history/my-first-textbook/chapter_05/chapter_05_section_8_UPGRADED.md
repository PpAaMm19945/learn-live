## 5.8 Chronological Summary & Review

In this final section, you will find a summary timeline of key events and a set of review questions to help you synthesize the material from this chapter.

This chapter traced the dramatic rise and fall of the church in Roman North Africa. We began with the establishment of Roman rule under Augustus, which created the stable political and economic conditions for Christianity to spread from its apostolic origins in Cyrene. We met Simon of Cyrene, who bore the cross of Christ, and the Pentecost pilgrims who carried the Gospel back to African soil. We witnessed the church's growth, its violent collision with imperial authority during the age of martyrs like the Scillitan Martyrs, Perpetua, Felicitas, and Cyprian. We explored the golden age of intellectual flourishing led by giants like Tertullian (who gave us the Latin vocabulary of the Trinity), Cyprian (who defined ecclesiology and Christian unity), and above all, Augustine of Hippo (whose works on grace, sin, and the City of God shaped all of Western theology). 

We also examined the deep internal wounds from the Donatist schism—a century-long division along ethnic and geographic lines—and the critical Councils of Carthage, which settled the biblical canon (397 AD) and condemned Pelagianism (418 AD). We witnessed the trauma of the Vandal invasion and Arian persecution, the fragile Byzantine restoration, and the church's intellectual and spiritual decline in the 7th century. Finally, we watched as this once-great church faded in the face of the Islamic conquest, its failure to translate the faith into the Berber language sealing its fate, in stark contrast to the survival of Coptic Christianity in Egypt.

### Synthesis & Scripture
The story of ancient North Africa is deeply interwoven with the biblical narrative. The region was associated in the Hebrew Scriptures with the descendants of **Phut** (Genesis 10:6), one of the sons of Ham. The presence of Cyrenian Jews at **Pentecost** (Acts 2:10) marks a pivotal moment where the Gospel explicitly intersects with this land. But it goes deeper: a North African man, **Simon of Cyrene**, bore the very cross of Christ (Mark 15:21), a prophetic picture of the African church bearing the weight of Christian theology to the world. "Men of Cyrene" were pioneers in preaching to the **Gentiles** (Acts 11:20), and **Lucius of Cyrene** was a prophet and teacher in the influential church of Antioch (Acts 13:1).

Reflect on how the rise of a powerful, Latin-speaking, theologically sophisticated church in North Africa—a region once on the geographic periphery of Israel's story—fulfills the prophetic vision of a faith that transcends ethnic and geographic boundaries (Isaiah 49:6: "I will make you as a light for the nations, that my salvation may reach to the end of the earth"). North Africa was not a late addition to the Christian story; from Pentecost onward, it was **central**. The North African church gave the world the **biblical canon** (Council of Carthage, 397 AD), the vocabulary of the **Trinity** (Tertullian), the theology of **grace** (Augustine), and the doctrine of the **church** (Cyprian).

The fall of this great church is a sobering reminder that **faithfulness must be renewed in every generation**. Yet even in its fall, God's providence preserved its legacy. The texts of Augustine, copied by faithful monks, crossed the sea and fueled the Reformation. The testimony of the martyrs still inspires. And the warning of North Africa's failure—its division, its failure to translate the Gospel into the heart language of the people—remains urgent for the global church today.
