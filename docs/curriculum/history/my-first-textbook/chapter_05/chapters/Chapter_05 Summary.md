Chapter 5: The Church in Roman Africa (c. 30 BC – 700 AD)

Region Focus: Cyrenaica, Carthage, Numidia, and Mauretania

I. Africa under Roman Dominion (c. 30 BC – 100 AD)

When Rome finally subdued Carthage in 146 BC, it destroyed the Punic capital and annexed the region as the province of Africa Proconsularis. Yet, the memory of Carthage’s power never faded. By 30 BC, after Augustus consolidated control over Egypt, the entire North African coast—from Cyrenaica to Mauretania—lay under Roman influence.

Administrative divisions:

Africa Proconsularis (around Carthage and modern Tunisia)

Numidia (eastern Algeria)

Mauretania Caesariensis and Tingitana (western Algeria and Morocco)

Cyrenaica (Libya, often joined to Crete)

Economy and society:
North Africa became Rome’s “breadbasket.” Vast estates (latifundia) supplied grain and olive oil to Italy. Carthage rose again as a Roman colony—Colonia Julia Carthago—in 29 BC, flourishing as one of the empire’s largest cities, rivaling Alexandria and Antioch by the 2nd century.

Cultural blend:
Indigenous Berbers mixed with Roman settlers and Latin-speaking elites. A hybrid identity emerged: urban Africans adopted Roman law and citizenship, while rural Berbers retained older languages and customs.

Religious life:
Pagan cults persisted—especially those of Saturn, Baal Hammon, and Tanit—yet the region also attracted early Jewish communities (as noted by Philo and Josephus). These would form a bridge for the later spread of Christianity.

II. The Birth of the African Church (c. 50 – 300 AD)

1. Earliest Traces of Christianity (c. 50–150 AD)

By the mid-1st century, Africa entered the New Testament story. According to Acts 2:10, men from Libya near Cyrene were present at Pentecost. Tradition holds that some returned home as the first Christian witnesses.

Early communities likely appeared first in Cyrene (eastern Libya) and Alexandria (Egypt), then spread westward along trade routes to Carthage and Numidia.

Inscriptions from the 2nd century show Christian names and symbols appearing across Proconsular Africa.

2. Conflict with Roman Authority (c. 150–300 AD)

The Roman state demanded public loyalty through sacrifice to the emperor’s genius. Christians, refusing this civic act, were accused of atheism and treason.

Martyrs of Scillium (180 AD): the earliest recorded African martyrs—twelve believers executed under Marcus Aurelius near Carthage. Their simple confession, “We have no other lord but Christ,” became a rallying cry.

Persecutions under Septimius Severus (202 AD): Carthage again became a scene of executions, including Perpetua and Felicitas, two women whose prison diary remains one of the earliest Christian texts from Africa.

Tertullian, writing in the same period, famously declared, “The blood of the martyrs is the seed of the Church.”

By 250 AD, African Christianity had expanded rapidly among both elites and commoners. Yet this growth brought internal tensions over doctrine, discipline, and the treatment of those who faltered under persecution.

III. The Age of the African Fathers (c. 150 – 450 AD)

North Africa became one of the intellectual engines of early Christianity, shaping thought across the empire.

1. Tertullian of Carthage (c. 160 – 225 AD)

A trained lawyer, he defended Christian belief using Roman legal reasoning.

Introduced key Latin theological vocabulary: Trinitas (Trinity), substantia (substance), persona (person).

Critiqued moral laxity in the Church and eventually joined the Montanist movement, which emphasized prophetic rigor and holiness.

2. Cyprian, Bishop of Carthage (c. 200 – 258 AD)

Converted from rhetoric to faith around 246 AD, elected bishop soon after.

Faced persecution under Decius (250 AD) and grappled with the question: Can the lapsed be restored?

Advocated unity and pastoral discipline, captured in his phrase: “He cannot have God as Father who does not have the Church as mother.”

Executed in Carthage, 258 AD, becoming a symbol of episcopal courage.

3. Doctrinal Division: The Donatist Schism (c. 311 – 411 AD)

After the Great Persecution under Diocletian (303–305 AD), disputes erupted over bishops who had surrendered scriptures to Roman authorities (traditores).

Donatus Magnus led a movement claiming that sacraments administered by corrupt clergy were invalid.

The controversy split African Christianity for a century, drawing in emperors and theologians alike.

It reflected deep social divisions: rural Berber Christians often supported Donatism as a protest against Romanized elites.

4. Augustine of Hippo (354 – 430 AD)

Born in Thagaste (Numidia), trained in rhetoric at Carthage and Rome.

Converted in Milan (386 AD), became bishop of Hippo Regius.

Wrote Confessions and The City of God—masterpieces blending faith and philosophy.

Opposed both Donatism and Pelagianism, defending grace as central to salvation.

His thought later shaped medieval Europe, but he remained distinctly African in tone—passionate, dialectical, and rooted in Berber soil.

IV. Rome’s Shadow and the Collapse of the Latin Church (c. 430 – 700 AD)

1. The Vandal Invasion (429–534 AD)

Germanic Vandals, crossing from Spain, captured Hippo in 430 AD (Augustine died during the siege).

King Gaiseric established a kingdom in Carthage (439 AD).

The Vandals were Arians, rejecting the Nicene doctrine of Christ’s divinity, and persecuted Nicene bishops.

Many African Christians fled or went underground; monasticism spread in remote areas as refuge.

2. Byzantine Restoration (534–647 AD)

The Eastern Roman Emperor Justinian I reconquered Carthage in 534 AD under General Belisarius, restoring imperial control.

Orthodox Christianity was reestablished, but the Church was weakened by centuries of division.

Local Berber tribes, never fully integrated into Byzantine rule, retained autonomy in the interior.

3. Intellectual and Spiritual Decline (600s)

Few theological works emerged after 600 AD. The urban centers declined; trade diminished; and communication with Rome and Constantinople faltered.

The once vibrant Carthaginian Church stood isolated, its Latin clergy aging and few.

Yet small monastic enclaves persisted, preserving texts of Augustine and Cyprian that would later be rediscovered in Europe.

V. The Islamic Advance and the End of Roman Africa (c. 640 – 700 AD)

In 642 AD, Arab forces conquered Egypt, then turned westward.

By 647 AD, they invaded Tripolitania and Cyrenaica.

Local Berbers alternated between resistance and alliance.

Carthage fell in 698 AD, marking the end of Roman Africa.

The surviving Christians retreated to mountain monasteries or migrated to Europe. The once-great African Church—home of Tertullian, Cyprian, and Augustine—disappeared from history’s surface, its memory preserved only in manuscripts carried north across the Mediterranean.
