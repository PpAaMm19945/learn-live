## 5.3 Conflict with Roman Authority (c. 150–300 AD)

In this section, you will examine the reasons behind Roman persecution of the African church and meet some of the courageous martyrs who chose faith over imperial loyalty.

The rapid growth of African Christianity inevitably led to conflict. The Roman state, while generally tolerant of diverse religions and establishing no single "state religion" in the modern sense, demanded a baseline of civic loyalty. This was expressed through public rituals, chief among them offering a pinch of incense to the *genius* (divine spirit or guardian spirit) of the emperor. For most pagans, this was a simple political gesture, no different from saluting a flag. For Christians, however, this was not a civic formality; it was an act of **idolatry**. To burn incense before the emperor's image was to declare him divine, a status that belonged to Christ alone. Their refusal to participate was seen by Roman authorities as stubbornness at best, and treasonous atheism (paradoxically, for rejecting the gods) at worst. Christians were also accused of secretive, immoral rituals (misunderstandings of the Lord's Supper and the "love feast"), and of being enemies of the social order.

This tension erupted into open persecution. In **180 AD** (conventional estimate), near the town of **Scillium** in **Proconsular Africa** (Tunisia), twelve Christians were brought before the proconsul Saturninus. Their trial, known as the **Acts of the Scillitan Martyrs**, is one of our earliest records of official persecution in the region and one of the first pieces of Christian Latin literature. Their confession was simple and unwavering: "We honor the emperor as the emperor, but we fear God." When offered a reprieve if they would only swear by the *genius* of the emperor, they refused. For this, they were executed. Their calm dignity in the face of death made a profound impression on those who witnessed it.

The persecution intensified under Emperor **Septimius Severus** in **202 AD** (conventional estimate). The grim irony cannot be overlooked: Severus, who issued the edict forbidding conversion to Christianity, was himself from **Leptis Magna** in North Africa, a Berber-Punic African who had risen to rule the entire Roman Empire. His African heritage did not incline him toward mercy for African Christians. Political expediency and imperial ideology trumped ethnic solidarity, a stark reminder that earthly identity—whether Roman, African, or anything else—is no guarantee of spiritual allegiance. The persecution under his reign was fierce, and **Carthage**, the jewel of Roman Africa, became a stage for martyrdom.

It was here, in the **Amphitheater of Carthage**, that two young women, a noblewoman named **Perpetua** and her enslaved servant **Felicitas** (or Felicity), faced death for their faith in **203 AD**. Arrested together as catechumens (those preparing for baptism), they were joined by several other Christians, including a young man named Saturus. During their imprisonment, they were baptized, and Perpetua, surprisingly literate for her time, kept a diary. Her account—later supplemented by an anonymous editor—is one of the most precious documents of the early church. It is one of the only first-person female narratives from the ancient Christian world.

> **Key Figure — Perpetua & Felicitas:** Vibia Perpetua was a well-educated noblewoman, approximately 22 years old, and a young mother with an infant son. Felicitas was her pregnant, enslaved servant, who gave birth in prison just days before their martyrdom. Arrested together, they faced intense pressure from family (Perpetua's pagan father begged her to recant) and officials to renounce their faith. Perpetua's prison diary records her visions of heaven, her unwavering resolve, and her pastoral concern for her fellow prisoners. Felicitas, despite her newborn child, refused to deny Christ. Their shared martyrdom in the amphitheater of Carthage—torn by wild beasts and then killed by the sword—became one of the most celebrated stories of courage in the early church, transcending the rigid social barriers between free and enslaved, noble and servant, in the fellowship of suffering.

Writing in Carthage during this very period, the theologian **Tertullian** observed the effect of these brutal public spectacles. In his *Apologeticus*, he famously declared, "**The blood of the martyrs is the seed of the Church**" (*semen est sanguis Christianorum*). His words proved prophetic. Far from stamping out the faith, the persecutions seemed only to strengthen it. The courage of Christians facing death—often singing hymns, praying for their executioners, and going to their deaths with joy—attracted new converts who were moved by such conviction. Observers asked themselves: what kind of faith could inspire such fearlessness in the face of death? By **250 AD** (conventional estimate), the African church was a powerful and expanding force, with Christian communities in every major city from Cyrene to Tingis. Yet this very success created new internal challenges, particularly the painful question of how to handle believers who faltered under pressure—the *lapsi* (the lapsed)—a controversy that would tear the church apart in the next generation.

### Theological Reflection
The suffering of the African martyrs raises a timeless and difficult question: why does God permit His people to endure such persecution? If He is sovereign and good, why did He not spare Perpetua and Felicitas from the amphitheater? Why not strike down their pagan persecutors?

The story of Perpetua and Felicitas offers a profound insight. In their *shared faith and shared death*, the rigid social hierarchies of the Roman world—noble and slave, wealthy and poor—were utterly erased. Both women stood equal before God, and both were counted worthy to suffer for the name of Christ (Acts 5:41). Their witness was not just about individual salvation, but about the creation of a **new kind of community**, a family bound not by blood or social status but by allegiance to Christ alone. This reflects the upside-down nature of the **Kingdom of God**, where the last are first and the greatest are those who serve (Matthew 20:16; Mark 10:43-45). In the empire's brutal amphitheater, the world saw a picture of the church triumphant—not through political power, but through sacrificial love.

The martyrs' blood did not just "seed" the church with new members; it watered the soil of a radical new society founded on the sacrificial love of Jesus. Their witness testified to a reality more solid than Rome itself: that eternal life with Christ is worth any earthly loss, even life itself. And the irony that the persecutor, **Septimius Severus**, was himself African underscores a sobering truth: ethnicity, nationality, and cultural heritage are no shield against spiritual blindness. Only the grace of God, working through His sovereign choice, opens eyes to see Christ and bow the knee.
