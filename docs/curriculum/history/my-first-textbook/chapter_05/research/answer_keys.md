### Primary Source Question Answer Outlines

**Section 5.2: Early Christian Inscriptions from Carthage**
*   **Q1: What do these symbols tell us about the core beliefs of the earliest African Christians?**
    *   **A1 Outline:** The symbols point to core, non-controversial beliefs. The *ichthys* (fish) was an acrostic for "Jesus Christ, Son of God, Savior," affirming Christology and salvation. The anchor symbolized hope and steadfastness in Christ amidst persecution (cf. Hebrews 6:19). The Chi-Rho is a christogram, marking the person as belonging to Christ. These symbols reveal a focus on the person and work of Jesus as central to their identity.
*   **Q2: How might using symbols instead of explicit text have been a practical choice for a minority faith in a pagan society?**
    *   **A2 Outline:** In a society where Christianity was often misunderstood and periodically persecuted, symbols served as a form of coded language. They allowed believers to identify each other and mark burial sites without attracting unwanted attention from hostile authorities or neighbors. It was a visual shorthand that was meaningful to the initiated but inconspicuous to outsiders.

**Section 5.3: The Acts of the Scillitan Martyrs**
*   **Q1: How does the martyrs' distinction between honoring the emperor and fearing God capture the core of the conflict?**
    *   **A1 Outline:** This distinction is crucial. The martyrs were not anarchists; they were willing to give the emperor the civil honor due to a temporal ruler. However, they reserved *fear*—meaning ultimate worship, reverence, and allegiance—for God alone. This refusal to deify a human ruler was the central sticking point. For Rome, this was treason; for Christians, it was faithfulness.
*   **Q2: What does the proconsul's reaction reveal about the Roman state's view of religious dissent?**
    *   **A2 Outline:** The proconsul's reaction shows that the Roman state was less concerned with personal belief than with public conformity. He doesn't engage in theological debate; he simply demands the public act of sacrifice as a test of civic loyalty. The state saw religious dissent primarily as a political problem, a threat to the unity and stability of the empire, which was grounded in a shared civic religion.

**Section 5.5: Augustine's Confessions**
*   **Q1: How does Augustine's personal experience of conversion shape his later theological emphasis on grace?**
    *   **A1 Outline:** Augustine's conversion was, in his own telling, not something he achieved but something that happened *to him*. He felt pursued by God and was eventually overcome by a divine call he could no longer resist. This experience of being saved from his own destructive desires became the foundation for his doctrine of sovereign grace—the idea that salvation is initiated and completed by God, not by human free will.
*   **Q2: In what ways does this story challenge the idea that faith is a purely intellectual decision?**
    *   **A2 Outline:** Augustine had already been intellectually convinced of Christianity's truth for some time, but he couldn't bring himself to commit. His final conversion was not an argument but a surrender, prompted by a child's voice and a biblical passage. This challenges the idea that faith is merely about intellectual assent; for Augustine, it also involved the will, the emotions, and a moment of divine intervention that broke the paralysis of his will.

### Review Question Answer Key

**Short Answer:**
1.  **Divisions/Breadbasket:** The key divisions were Africa Proconsularis, Numidia, Mauretania, and Cyrenaica. The region was called the "breadbasket of Rome" because its large estates (*latifundia*) produced vast quantities of grain and olive oil that were exported to feed the city of Rome.
2.  **Earliest Connection:** The earliest connection is found in Acts 2:10, which records that visitors from "Egypt and the parts of Libya belonging to Cyrene" were present in Jerusalem on the Day of Pentecost and witnessed the outpouring of the Holy Spirit.
3.  **Central Conflict:** The central conflict was the Christian refusal to offer sacrifices to the divine spirit (*genius*) of the Roman emperor. Christians saw this as idolatry, while the Roman state viewed the refusal as an act of treason and atheism.
4.  ***Traditores*/Donatism:** The *traditores* were bishops and clergy who, during the Diocletian persecution, handed over copies of the Scriptures to Roman authorities to be burned. The Donatist Schism began when a group insisted that these *traditores* (and any clergy they ordained) had permanently invalidated their ministry, making sacraments they performed worthless.
5.  **Augustine's Works:** *Confessions* is a spiritual autobiography detailing his journey to faith and his soul's search for God. *The City of God* is a major work of Christian philosophy that contrasts the transient, earthly "City of Man" with the eternal, heavenly "City of God," written to defend Christianity after the sack of Rome.
6.  **Vandal Religious Difference:** The Vandals were Arian Christians, meaning they did not believe in the full divinity of Jesus Christ as defined by the Council of Nicaea. The majority of North African Christians were Nicene (or Catholic), which led to the Vandals persecuting them as heretics.
