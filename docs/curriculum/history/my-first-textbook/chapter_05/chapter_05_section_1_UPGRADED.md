## 5.1 Africa under Roman Dominion (c. 30 BC–100 AD)

In this section you will learn about the political, economic, and cultural landscape of North Africa after it was fully integrated into the Roman Empire, setting the stage for the arrival of Christianity.

When Rome finally subdued its great rival, Carthage, in 146 BC (conventional estimate), it razed the Punic capital to the ground, plowing salt into its ruins as a symbol of total destruction. The specter of Carthaginian power, however, lingered in the Roman imagination for generations—a reminder of Hannibal's elephants crossing the Alps and Rome's near-destruction. By 30 BC (conventional estimate), following Octavian's (soon to be Emperor Augustus) decisive victory over Mark Antony and Cleopatra in Egypt, the entire North African coastline—from the fertile fields of **Cyrenaica** in the east to the towering **Atlas Mountains** of **Mauretania** in the west—was firmly under Roman control.

This vast territory was organized into several key administrative divisions. **Africa Proconsularis** (encompassing the fertile lands around a rebuilt Carthage and modern Tunisia) was a senatorial province, Rome's most prized African possession, supplying two-thirds of the empire's grain. **Numidia** (modern eastern Algeria), initially a client kingdom under Berber rulers, was annexed by Julius Caesar in 46 BC and later reorganized as an imperial province in the 3rd century AD, with its capital at **Cirta** (modern Constantine). **Mauretania Caesariensis** (western Algeria, capital at Caesarea/Cherchell) and **Mauretania Tingitana** (northern Morocco, capital at Tingis/Tangier) were founded as imperial provinces under Emperor Claudius in 42 AD. Finally, **Cyrenaica** (modern Libya), often administered jointly with the island of Crete, had its capital at the ancient Greek city of **Cyrene**, a name that would echo throughout the New Testament.

> **Key Term:** **Latifundia** — Large, landed estates, often owned by wealthy Roman aristocrats, that specialized in cash-crop agriculture like grain, olives, and grapes for export. These massive farms, worked by enslaved labor and tenant farmers, were a defining feature of the Roman provincial economy.

Economically, North Africa swiftly became the "breadbasket of Rome." Its vast and fertile latifundia, stretching across the coastal plains (the narrow fertile "Tell" region between the Mediterranean and the Atlas Mountains), supplied the imperial capital with a steady flow of grain, olive oil, and other agricultural products. The grain ships mentioned in the book of Acts (Acts 27:6, 27:38)—including the Alexandrian vessel that carried Paul as a prisoner to Rome—were part of this vital maritime network that connected North Africa to the heart of the empire. The city of Carthage itself experienced a remarkable rebirth. Refounded as a Roman colony, **Colonia Julia Carthago**, in 29 BC (conventional estimate) by Augustus, it flourished once more. By the second century AD, it had grown to a population of approximately 500,000, making it one of the largest and most prosperous cities in the Western Roman Empire, rivaled only by Alexandria and Antioch.

The wealth of Roman North Africa was not confined to Carthage. **Leptis Magna**, a magnificent port city in Tripolitania (modern Libya), became one of the most splendid cities in the empire. It was the birthplace of **Septimius Severus**, who reigned as Roman Emperor from 193 to 211 AD. Severus was of Berber-Punic descent, a living evidence of the fact that Rome's African provinces did not merely submit to imperial power—they shaped it. As emperor, Severus lavished Leptis Magna with monumental architecture: a grand forum, basilica, and the Arch of Severus, all of which still stand as testimony to African grandeur. In the interior of Numidia, the emperor Trajan founded **Thamugadi** (modern Timgad) in 100 AD as a military colony for veterans. Its surprisingly preserved grid layout makes it one of the best examples of Roman urban planning in the world. Far to the west, in Mauretania Tingitana, the city of **Volubilis** (in modern Morocco) stood as Rome's westernmost major African outpost, complete with triumphal arches, temples, and baths—Roman civilization at the edge of the known world.

Roman control, however, did not extend uniformly across the vast African interior. The **Atlas Mountains**, running parallel to the coast from Morocco through Algeria, formed a natural barrier. While Rome controlled the coastal plains and major valleys, the mountain tribes—particularly the **Gaetuli** and **Mauri** (from whom "Mauretania" derives its name)—maintained a fierce independence, periodically launching raids on Roman settlements. To protect the southern frontier from desert tribes and to control trans-Saharan trade routes, Rome constructed the **Limes Tripolitanus**, a fortified frontier line in southern Tripolitania built primarily in the 3rd century AD. This line of forts and watchtowers stretched for hundreds of kilometers along the desert's edge, a evidence of the limits of Roman power.

This Roman-led prosperity and order fostered a unique cultural blend. Indigenous **Berber** (or Amazigh) populations mixed with Roman settlers, soldiers, and administrators, creating a vibrant, hybrid society. In the bustling urban centers like Carthage, Cirta, and Leptis Magna, a Latin-speaking elite emerged—wealthy landowners, merchants, and officials who embraced Roman law, literature, and the path to citizenship. Latin became the language of government, commerce, and educated discourse, leaving a permanent linguistic imprint on the region. In the rural hinterlands, however, many Berber communities preserved their ancestral languages (part of the Afro-Asiatic language family, related to ancient Egyptian and Hebrew) and customs, creating a complex cultural tapestry. Religious life reflected this diversity; ancient Punic and Libyan deities like Saturn (syncretized with Baal Hammon), Tanit, and others continued to be venerated, often blended with Roman gods. It was into this world—a world of political stability, economic integration, linguistic diversity, and religious pluralism—that the first seeds of Christianity would soon arrive, finding fertile ground in the established Jewish communities that dotted the coastline, particularly in Cyrenaica, as noted by contemporaries like Philo of Alexandria and the Jewish historian Josephus.

### Theological Reflection
The Roman subjugation of Africa, while born of conquest, ambition, and imperial greed, demonstrates the overarching and mysterious providence of God. The Roman Empire, for all its brutality and paganism, was an instrument in the divine hand. By forging a single political and economic unit across the Mediterranean, by constructing the vast network of roads and ports that connected Cyrene to Carthage to Tingis, by establishing Latin as a common language and *Pax Romana* (Roman Peace) as a fragile but real security, Rome inadvertently created the very infrastructure that would facilitate the rapid spread of the Gospel. The Apostle Paul himself traveled these Roman roads and sailed on Roman grain ships.

This historical reality reminds us of a profound theological truth: God often uses the designs of worldly empires, whether Rome, Persia, or Babylon, to advance His own redemptive purposes. He is sovereign over the rise and fall of nations (Daniel 2:21). The fact that a Berber-Punic African, **Septimius Severus**, could rise to rule the entire Roman Empire shows that God's purposes transcend ethnicity and geography. The "breadbasket of Rome" would soon become a cradle for the African Church, demonstrating that God's harvest can flourish in the most unexpected of soils. The Mediterranean Coastal Road—built to move grain to feed Rome's masses—would become the highway along which the Gospel would travel westward from Cyrene to Carthage and beyond, showing that even the logistics of empire serve the kingdom of God.
