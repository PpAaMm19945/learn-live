## 6.3 The Great Persecutions and the Rise of the Martyrs

In this section you will learn about the intense periods of Roman persecution faced by Egyptian Christians and how their response shaped the identity of the Coptic Church.

While Christians experienced sporadic, localized persecution since the first century, the third and early fourth centuries saw empire-wide campaigns aimed at enforcing religious conformity. The persecutions under emperors **Decius** (250 AD) and **Valerian** (257-259 AD) were particularly severe in Egypt. Decius issued an edict requiring all citizens to perform a sacrifice to the Roman gods and the emperor's genius (divine spirit) in the presence of a magistrate. Those who complied were issued a **libellus**, a certificate proving their loyalty. For Christians, this created a crisis: to sacrifice was apostasy, but to refuse meant imprisonment, torture, or death.

> **Key Term:** **Libellus** — A certificate issued during the Decian persecution to prove that a person had performed a sacrifice to the Roman gods. Those who obtained these without actually sacrificing (often by bribery) were called *libellatici*, and their status became a point of major controversy in the church.

The most brutal and systematic campaign came under Emperor **Diocletian** (303-311 AD), known as the "Great Persecution." Diocletian sought to completely eradicate Christianity by destroying churches, burning scriptures, and stripping Christians of legal rights. In Egypt, the persecution was so intense and the number of martyrs so high that the Coptic Church reset its calendar to mark this era. The **Coptic Calendar** (still used today) begins its years not with the birth of Christ, but with the year **284 AD** (the year Diocletian became emperor), designating it the **Era of the Martyrs** (*Anno Martyrum* or A.M.).

> **Key Term:** **Martyr** — From the Greek word for "witness," a martyr is someone who dies for their faith. In the early church, martyrs were highly venerated as powerful witnesses to the truth of the Gospel and the promise of resurrection.

The response of Egyptian Christians to these trials was profound. Many stood firm in their faith and were executed. Their stories became foundational to the identity of the Egyptian church. One famous tradition recounts the **Theban Legion**, a Roman legion recruited from the Thebaid (Upper Egypt) and composed entirely of Christians. According to the account, when ordered to sacrifice to pagan gods while stationed in Gaul (modern Switzerland), the entire legion—numbering 6,600 men—refused and was martyred *en masse*. While historical details are debated, the story reflects the deep conviction of Egyptian Christians that their ultimate allegiance belonged to Christ, not Caesar.

The persecutions also created deep pastoral challenges. Not all Christians were able to endure. Some offered the required sacrifices (*sacrificati*), while others obtained false certificates (*libellatici*). After the persecutions subsided, the question of how to treat these "lapsed" Christians, known as the *lapsi*, caused major divisions in the church. In Egypt, this tension contributed to the **Melitian Schism**, a split led by Bishop Melitius of Lycopolis who advocated for a stricter treatment of the lapsed than the Bishop of Alexandria allowed.

### Theological Reflection

The era of martyrs is a stark reminder that the call to follow Christ is a call to take up one's cross (**Matthew 16:24**). The witness (*martyria*) of these Egyptian Christians was not just in their final, heroic moments, but in their conviction that Jesus was Lord, not Caesar. They embodied the spirit of the apostles who, after being beaten, "left the presence of the council, rejoicing that they were counted worthy to suffer dishonor for the name" (**Acts 5:41**).

Their willingness to surrender their earthly lives testified to their unwavering belief in the resurrection and a kingdom not of this world. This raises a challenging question for us today: what "gods" does our culture demand we sacrifice to? Whether it is the idol of personal comfort, financial security, or social acceptance, the martyrs challenge us to examine our own allegiances. They teach us that a faith that costs nothing is worth nothing, and that true loyalty to Christ may require us to stand against the pressures of our time, confident that our ultimate hope is in Him alone.
