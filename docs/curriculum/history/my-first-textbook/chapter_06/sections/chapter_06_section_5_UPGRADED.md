## 6.5 The Arian Controversy and the Council of Nicaea

In this section you will learn about the Arian controversy, a major theological dispute about the nature of Jesus Christ, and how the Church addressed it at the First Council of Nicaea.

In the early fourth century, a dispute erupted in Alexandria that would shake the foundations of the Christian world. The controversy began with the teachings of **Arius** (c. 256–336 AD), a popular presbyter in Alexandria. Influenced by Greek philosophical concepts of God's absolute oneness, Arius taught that the Son of God was not eternal but was created by the Father before the ages. In his view, the Son was the first and greatest of all created beings, but he was not co-eternal and co-equal with the Father. His followers popularized this view with the slogan: "There was a time when the Son was not."

> **Key Term:** **Arianism** — The theological teaching of Arius and his followers that denied the full divinity of Jesus Christ. It held that the Son was a created being, subordinate to God the Father.

The teachings of Arius were fiercely opposed by his bishop, Alexander of Alexandria, and Alexander's brilliant young deacon, **Athanasius** (c. 296–373 AD). Athanasius, who had been trained in the Alexandrian Catechetical School, argued that the Arian position destroyed the Gospel. If Jesus were a mere creature, he could not be the savior of humanity. Only one who is fully God could bridge the infinite gap between a holy God and sinful man.

### The Council of Nicaea (325 AD)

To resolve the crisis, Emperor Constantine convened the **First Council of Nicaea** in 325 AD. This was the first ecumenical council, a gathering of over 300 bishops from across the Christian world. The council overwhelmingly condemned the teachings of Arius and formulated a creed to define orthodox Christian belief.

> **Key Term:** **Homoousios** — A Greek theological term meaning "of the same substance" or "of one being." The Council of Nicaea used this term to affirm that the Son is of the same divine substance as the Father, directly refuting Arianism.

The resulting **Nicene Creed** declared that the Son is "begotten, not made" and, crucially, "*homoousios*" with the Father. This affirmation became the cornerstone of Christian orthodoxy, defining the doctrine of the Trinity for all time.

### Athanasius Contra Mundum

The struggle did not end at Nicaea. For the next several decades, Arianism regained political favor under subsequent emperors. Athanasius, who became the **20th Pope of Alexandria** in 328 AD, spent his life defending the Nicene decision. He was exiled **five times** by various emperors for his refusal to compromise, spending years hiding with the monks in the Egyptian desert. His solitary stand for the truth earned him the Latin phrase *Athanasius contra mundum*—"Athanasius against the world."

### Theological Reflection

The Arian controversy was not just an argument over theological jargon; it was a battle for the heart of the Gospel. At stake was the very identity of Jesus Christ. Is He truly God, or is He something less? The Scriptures are clear: "In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God" (**John 1:1**). Paul affirms that "in him the whole fullness of deity dwells bodily" (**Colossians 2:9**).

The Nicene fathers understood that a created being cannot save; only God can. The doctrine of the Trinity, affirmed at Nicaea, is not an abstract philosophical puzzle; it is the bedrock of our redemption. It assures us that in Jesus Christ, we meet the living God, who loved us and gave Himself for us. The steadfastness of Athanasius is a powerful example of the courage required to defend the essential truths of the faith, even when they are unpopular or politically inconvenient.
