## 6.7 The Coptic Church under Byzantine and Early Arab Rule

In this section you will learn about the experience of the Coptic Church after the Council of Chalcedon, including its relationship with the Byzantine state and the dramatic changes brought by the Arab conquest of Egypt.

Following the Council of Chalcedon in 451 AD, the Coptic Church found itself officially at odds with the Byzantine Empire. The emperors in Constantinople, committed to the Chalcedonian formula, periodically attempted to impose their will on the Egyptian church. They appointed pro-Chalcedonian (or **Melkite**) patriarchs in Alexandria, supported by imperial troops. This created a deep and lasting alienation between the Coptic majority and the Byzantine state.

> **Key Term:** **Melkite** — A term derived from the Syriac word for "king" or "emperor," used to describe those Christians in the Middle East who accepted the Christology of the Council of Chalcedon, which was supported by the Byzantine emperor.

The Coptic Church, however, maintained its own line of non-Chalcedonian patriarchs, who were seen by the local population as the legitimate spiritual leaders. This dual-patriarch situation led to periodic violence and persecution of the Coptic faithful by Byzantine authorities, deepening the rift between Alexandria and Constantinople.

### The Islamic Conquest (639-646 AD)

This period of internal division and imperial pressure came to an abrupt end with the Arab conquest of Egypt. In **639 AD**, Arab forces led by **'Amr ibn al-'As** invaded Egypt. The Byzantine forces, weakened by decades of war with Persia and unpopular with the local populace, were defeated by **641 AD**. 

Many Copts were initially ambivalent or even cautiously welcoming of the change in rulers. After years of Byzantine persecution for their rejection of Chalcedon, some viewed the Arab conquest as liberation from an oppressive Christian emperor. However, they soon discovered that Islamic rule brought its own challenges.

> **Key Term:** **Dhimmi** — A protected, non-Muslim subject living in a state under Islamic law. Dhimmis, such as Jews and Christians, were allowed to practice their religion and manage their own communities, but were required to pay a special poll tax (*jizya*) and abide by certain social restrictions.

Under Arab rule, the Coptic Church was granted **dhimmi** status. This offered them a degree of religious freedom and autonomy that they had not enjoyed under the later Byzantine emperors. They were free to worship and to be governed by their own patriarch, without the imposition of a rival Chalcedonian leader. The Melkite patriarchate effectively ended, and the Coptic Pope was recognized as the legitimate leader of Egyptian Christianity.

However, this freedom came at the cost of the ***jizya* tax** and a subordinate social status. Christians were forbidden from riding horses (only donkeys), building new churches without permission, or openly displaying their faith in certain ways. Over the centuries, the pressures of Islamic rule—including taxation, social incentives to convert, and periodic persecutions—led to a gradual process of Islamization. The Coptic population, once the overwhelming majority, slowly became a minority.

### The Key to Survival: Language Translation

Why did Coptic Christianity survive Islamic conquest when North African Christianity completely disappeared? The answer lies in one crucial difference: **language translation**. The Coptic Church had translated the Bible into **Coptic**, the final stage of the ancient Egyptian language, written in Greek letters plus seven Demotic symbols. This meant that Egyptian Christians could read Scripture, worship, and be discipled in their heart language.

In contrast, the North African church failed to translate the Bible into **Berber**, the indigenous language of the population. Without Scripture in the language of the people, Christianity remained the religion of the Romanized urban elite. When the Arab conquest disrupted Roman culture, the church had no deep roots among the common people, and it withered away within a few generations.

### Theological Reflection

The long history of the Coptic Church under non-Christian rule is a powerful evidence of the resilience of faith in the midst of adversity. Their story illustrates the biblical theme of the church as a "sojourner and exile" in the world (**1 Peter 2:11**). The Copts learned to be the people of God under the authority of earthly powers who did not share their faith.

The lesson of translation is profound: the Gospel must be in the heart language of a people or it will not survive. God's Word is not bound to any one culture or language. The example of Pentecost, where people heard the Gospel "each in our own native language" (**Acts 2:8**), is the model for the church. The survival of the Copts and the disappearance of the Berber church stand as a stark reminder that cultural adaptation—translating Scripture, worship, and theology into indigenous expressions—is not optional. It is essential for the long-term flourishing of the faith.
