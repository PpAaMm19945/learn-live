## 6.6 Christological Debates and the Council of Chalcedon

In this section you will learn about the complex debates over the person of Christ that followed the Council of Nicaea, culminating in the pivotal and controversial Council of Chalcedon.

While the Council of Nicaea settled the question of Christ's divinity, it did not end the debate about how His divinity and humanity relate to each other in one person. In the fifth century, two major schools of thought emerged. The **Antiochene** school emphasized the distinction between Christ's human and divine natures, sometimes at the risk of dividing them into two separate persons. The **Alexandrian** school, led by figures like **Cyril of Alexandria** (376–444 AD), stressed the unity of Christ's person.

Cyril, a powerful Patriarch of Alexandria, championed the phrase "one nature of the incarnate Word." He successfully opposed Nestorius (who was accused of dividing Christ) at the **Council of Ephesus** in 431 AD. However, the debate continued to simmer, leading Emperor Marcian to convene the **Council of Chalcedon** in 451 AD.

> **Key Term:** **Christology** — The branch of Christian theology that studies the person, nature, and work of Jesus Christ.

> **Key Term:** **Dyophysitism / Miaphysitism** — *Dyophysitism* is the belief that Christ has two distinct natures, divine and human, in one person (the Chalcedonian view). *Miaphysitism* is the belief that Christ has one, unified nature that is both fully divine and fully human (the Coptic view). It is important to distinguish Miaphysitism from *Monophysitism*, a condemned heresy which held that Christ's human nature was absorbed into his divine nature.

### The Chalcedonian Definition and the Great Schism

The Council of Chalcedon produced the **Chalcedonian Definition**, which affirmed that Christ is one person in two distinct but inseparable natures, "without confusion, without change, without division, without separation." This definition became the standard for the Roman and Byzantine churches.

However, the definition was rejected by the church in Egypt. The Egyptian bishops felt that the "two natures" language came dangerously close to the Nestorian error of dividing Christ. They clung to Cyril's "one nature" formula. This refusal led to a tragic schism. The Egyptian church, along with churches in Syria and Ethiopia, separated from the imperial church, forming what are known today as the **Oriental Orthodox Churches**.

> **Debate Box — Chalcedon and National Identity: Theological Choice or Political Realignment?**
> Was the rejection of Chalcedon by the Egyptian church a purely theological decision, or was it intertwined with a growing sense of regional identity and resistance to the authority of Constantinople? Some historians argue that the debate was primarily about preserving the perceived integrity of Alexandrian theology. Others contend that theological language became a vehicle for political and cultural grievances. For many Egyptians, rejecting the emperor's council was a way to assert their independence from Byzantine imperial control.

### Theological Reflection

The debates leading to Chalcedon touch on the deepest mysteries of our faith. Who is Jesus? In Him, we see both the face of God and the face of a perfect man. The Chalcedonian Definition is a careful attempt to hold these two truths in tension without sacrificing either. It is a guardrail against error, protecting the biblical portrait of Christ.

The tragedy of the schism that followed, however, is a somber reminder of human fallibility. The division weakened the church in the East just before the rise of Islam. It teaches us the importance of pursuing both theological precision and charitable understanding. We must speak the truth in love (**Ephesians 4:15**), recognizing that even those with whom we disagree may be earnestly seeking to honor the same Lord. The legacy of Chalcedon is therefore twofold: a beautiful and enduring confession of faith, and a painful lesson in the cost of Christian disunity.
