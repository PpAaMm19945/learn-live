## 6.6 Christological Debates and the Council of Chalcedon

In this section you will learn about the complex debates over the person of Christ that followed the Council of Nicaea, culminating in the pivotal and controversial Council of Chalcedon.

While the Council of Nicaea settled the question of Christ's divinity, it did not end the debate about how His divinity and humanity relate to each other in one person. In the fifth century (conventional estimate), two major schools of thought emerged. The **Antiochene** school, associated with Antioch, emphasized the distinction between Christ's human and divine natures, sometimes at the risk of dividing them into two separate persons. The **Alexandrian** school, centered in Alexandria, stressed the unity of Christ's person, sometimes at the risk of blurring the distinction between his two natures.

> **Key Term:** **Christology** — The branch of Christian theology that studies the person, nature, and work of Jesus Christ.

> **Key Term:** **Dyophysitism / Miaphysitism** — *Dyophysitism* is the belief that Christ has two distinct natures, divine and human, in one person (the Chalcedonian view). *Miaphysitism* is the belief that Christ has one, unified nature that is both fully divine and fully human (the non-Chalcedonian view). It is important to distinguish Miaphysitism from *Monophysitism*, a condemned heresy which held that Christ's human nature was absorbed into his divine nature.

The controversy came to a head over the teachings of Nestorius, the Patriarch of Constantinople, who was accused of dividing Christ into two persons. This led to the Council of Ephesus in 431 AD (conventional estimate), which condemned Nestorius. The debate continued, however, leading Emperor Marcian to convene the Council of Chalcedon in 451 AD (conventional estimate). This council produced the **Chalcedonian Definition**, which affirmed that Christ is one person in two distinct but inseparable natures, "without confusion, without change, without division, without separation."

> **Debate Box — Chalcedon and National Identity: Theological Choice or Political Realignment?**
> Was the rejection of Chalcedon by the Egyptian church a purely theological decision, or was it intertwined with a growing sense of regional identity and resistance to the authority of Constantinople? Some historians argue that the debate was primarily about preserving the perceived integrity of Alexandrian theology, as championed by figures like Cyril of Alexandria. Others contend that theological language became a vehicle for political and cultural grievances. For many Egyptians, rejecting the emperor's council was a way to assert their independence from Byzantine imperial control.

The Definition of Chalcedon became the standard Christological formula for the Roman and, later, the Eastern Orthodox and Protestant traditions. However, it was rejected by a large portion of the church in Egypt and Syria. They felt that the "two natures" language of Chalcedon came dangerously close to the Nestorian error of dividing Christ. These non-Chalcedonians, who preferred the "one nature" (*mia physis*) language of Cyril of Alexandria, eventually formed the Oriental Orthodox Churches, including the Coptic Orthodox Church of Alexandria. This schism, one of the most significant in church history, permanently separated the see of Alexandria from Rome and Constantinople.

### Theological Reflection
The debates leading to Chalcedon may seem like ancient history, but they touch on the deepest mysteries of our faith. Who is Jesus? In Him, we see both the face of God and the face of a perfect man. The Chalcedonian Definition is a careful attempt to hold these two truths in tension without sacrificing either. It is a guardrail against error, protecting the biblical portrait of Christ. The tragedy of the schism that followed, however, is a somber reminder of human fallibility and the danger of division in the body of Christ. It teaches us the importance of pursuing both theological precision and charitable understanding. We must speak the truth in love (Ephesians 4:15), recognizing that even those with whom we disagree may be earnestly seeking to honor the same Lord. The legacy of Chalcedon is therefore twofold: a beautiful and enduring confession of faith, and a painful lesson in the cost of Christian disunity.
