## 6.5 The Arian Controversy and the Council of Nicaea

In this section you will learn about the Arian controversy, a major theological dispute about the nature of Jesus Christ, and how the Church addressed it at the First Council of Nicaea.

In the early fourth century (conventional estimate), a dispute erupted in Alexandria that would shake the foundations of the Christian world. The controversy began with the teachings of Arius, a presbyter in Alexandria. Arius taught that the Son of God was not eternal but was created by the Father before the ages. In his view, the Son was the first and greatest of all created beings, but he was not co-eternal and co-equal with the Father. "There was a time when the Son was not," became the infamous slogan of his followers.

> **Key Term:** **Arianism** — The theological teaching of Arius and his followers that denied the full divinity of Jesus Christ. It held that the Son was a created being, subordinate to God the Father.

> **Key Term:** **Homoousios** — A Greek theological term meaning "of the same substance" or "of one being." The Council of Nicaea used this term to affirm that the Son is of the same divine substance as the Father, directly refuting Arianism.

The teachings of Arius were fiercely opposed by his bishop, Alexander of Alexandria, and Alexander's brilliant young deacon, Athanasius. They argued that if Jesus were a mere creature, he could not be the savior of humanity. Only a truly divine savior could reconcile a sinful world to a holy God. The dispute quickly spread, threatening to split the church and destabilize the Roman Empire, which had only recently embraced Christianity under Emperor Constantine.

To resolve the crisis, Constantine convened the First Council of Nicaea in 325 AD (conventional estimate). This was the first ecumenical council, a gathering of bishops from across the Christian world. The council overwhelmingly condemned the teachings of Arius and formulated a creed, or statement of faith, to define orthodox Christian belief about the nature of the Son.

The resulting Nicene Creed declared that the Son is "begotten, not made" and, crucially, "*homoousios*" with the Father. This affirmation became the cornerstone of Christian orthodoxy, defining the doctrine of the Trinity for all time. Though Arianism would persist for decades, the Council of Nicaea marked a defining moment in the development of Christian doctrine.

### Theological Reflection
The Arian controversy was not just an argument over theological jargon; it was a battle for the heart of the Gospel. At stake was the very identity of Jesus Christ. Is He truly God, or is He something less? The Nicene fathers, led by the Spirit, understood that the salvation of the world depended on the answer. A created being cannot save; only God can. The doctrine of the Trinity, affirmed at Nicaea, is not an abstract philosophical puzzle; it is the bedrock of our redemption. It assures us that in Jesus Christ, we meet the living God, who loved us and gave Himself for us. The steadfastness of Athanasius and his allies is a powerful example of the importance of theological clarity and the courage required to defend the essential truths of the faith, even when they are unpopular or divisive.
