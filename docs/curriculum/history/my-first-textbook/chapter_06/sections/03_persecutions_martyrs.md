## 6.3 The Great Persecutions and the Rise of the Martyrs

In this section you will learn about the intense periods of Roman persecution faced by Egyptian Christians and how their response shaped the identity of the Coptic Church.

While Christians experienced sporadic, localized persecution since the first century, the third and early fourth centuries (conventional estimate) saw empire-wide campaigns aimed at enforcing religious conformity. The persecutions under emperors Decius (250 AD), Valerian (257-259 AD), and Diocletian (303-311 AD) were particularly severe in Egypt. Diocletian's campaign, known as the "Great Persecution," was the most brutal and systematic. It sought to eradicate Christianity by destroying churches, burning scriptures, and forcing Christians to sacrifice to the Roman gods on pain of imprisonment, torture, or death.

> **Key Term:** **Martyr** — From the Greek word for "witness," a martyr is someone who dies for their faith. In the early church, martyrs were highly venerated as powerful witnesses to the truth of the Gospel and the promise of resurrection.

> **Key Term:** **Libellus** — A certificate issued during the Decian persecution to prove that a person had performed a sacrifice to the Roman gods. Those who obtained these without actually sacrificing were called *libellatici* and their status became a point of controversy in the church.

The response of Egyptian Christians to these trials was profound. Many stood firm in their faith and were executed, becoming celebrated martyrs. Their stories, such as that of the Theban Legion (a Roman legion of Egyptian Christians who allegedly were martyred *en masse*), became foundational to the identity of the Egyptian church. The Coptic Orthodox Church dates its calendar from 284 AD (conventional estimate), the year Diocletian became emperor, calling it the "Era of the Martyrs" (*Anno Martyrum*).

The persecutions also created deep pastoral challenges. Not all Christians were able to endure. Some offered the required sacrifices (*sacrificati*), while others obtained false certificates (*libellatici*). After the persecutions subsided, the question of how to treat these "lapsed" Christians, known as the *lapsi*, caused major divisions in the church, leading to schisms over the nature of repentance and church authority.

### Theological Reflection
The era of martyrs is a stark reminder that the call to follow Christ is a call to take up one's cross (Matthew 16:24). The witness (*martyria*) of these Egyptian Christians was not just in their final, heroic moments, but in their conviction that Jesus was Lord, not Caesar. Their willingness to surrender their earthly lives testified to their unwavering belief in the resurrection and a kingdom not of this world. This raises a challenging question for us today: what "gods" does our culture demand we sacrifice to? Whether it is the idol of personal comfort, financial security, or social acceptance, the martyrs challenge us to examine our own allegiances. They teach us that a faith that costs nothing is worth nothing, and that true loyalty to Christ may require us to stand against the pressures of our time, confident that our ultimate hope is in Him alone.
