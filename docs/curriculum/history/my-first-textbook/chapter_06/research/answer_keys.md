# Answer Keys for Chapter 6

## Primary Source Analysis Questions

### Section 6.3: The Martyrdom of Perpetua and Felicitas

*   **1. Though from North Africa (Carthage), how does Perpetua's account illustrate the Roman state's view of Christian refusal to sacrifice?**
    *   **Model Answer:** Perpetua's account demonstrates that the Roman state viewed the Christian refusal to sacrifice not simply as a private religious choice, but as an act of public defiance and insubordination (*contumacia*). The repeated attempts by the governor to persuade her, including appealing to her duty to her father and infant son, show that the state's primary goal was conformity and allegiance to the imperial order. Her refusal was seen as a rejection of Roman authority and social norms, making it a political crime as much as a religious one.

*   **2. What does the vision described in the text suggest about the martyr's understanding of death and the afterlife?**
    *   **Model Answer:** The visions, such as Perpetua's dream of climbing a ladder to a heavenly garden or fighting an Egyptian opponent to reach Christ, suggest that martyrs understood their impending death not as a tragic end, but as a victorious transition into the presence of God. Death was reframed as a contest (*agon*) in which the martyr, empowered by Christ, would defeat evil and receive the prize of eternal life. This perspective transformed an execution into a triumph, giving martyrs the strength to face torture and death with hope and even joy.

### Section 6.5: Athanasius, *On the Incarnation*

*   **1. According to Athanasius, why was it necessary for the Word of God to become human?**
    *   **Model Answer:** Athanasius argues that the Incarnation was necessary for two primary reasons. First, humanity was subject to corruption and death because of sin, and only the Creator (the Word of God) could recreate and restore humanity to its original state. Second, humanity had lost the true knowledge of God. It was necessary for the invisible Word to become visible and tangible in a human body so that humanity could know God again. In essence, only God could solve the problems of death and ignorance, and He had to do it from within the human condition.

*   **2. How does Athanasius's view of salvation depend on the full divinity of Christ?**
    *   **Model Answer:** For Athanasius, salvation is a process of *theosis* or deification, meaning humanity is restored to the image of God and made partakers of the divine nature. This is only possible if the one who saves is fully God. A created being, no matter how exalted, could not unite humanity to God because it would not be God itself. If Jesus were merely a creature, as Arius claimed, he could not bestow eternal life or true knowledge of the Father. Therefore, Athanasius's entire understanding of salvation rests on the conviction that Jesus is *homoousios*—of the same divine substance as the Father.
