## 7.5 Monasticism and the “Nine Saints” (c. 5th–7th centuries)

In this section you will learn about the flourishing of monasticism in Aksum. We'll focus on the traditional story of the "Nine Saints" and the vital role monasteries played in shaping Ethiopian culture.

### Quick Start
*   **Learning Focus:** Understand the impact of monasticism on the Ethiopian Church.
*   **Key Idea:** The arrival of the "Nine Saints" is traditionally seen as the spark for a monastic movement that made monasteries the spiritual and cultural heartlands of Ethiopian Christianity.

While the first seeds of monasticism appeared in the 4th century, tradition holds that the monastic life truly flowered in Ethiopia with the arrival of the "Nine Saints." These nine missionaries are said to have come in the late 5th or early 6th century from various parts of the Roman Empire, including Syria and Anatolia. Many believe they were refugees from the theological controversies after the Council of Chalcedon (451 AD). Ethiopia reveres their names: Abba Aregawi, Abba Pantelewon, Abba Garima, and others.

According to tradition, these saints traveled throughout the Aksumite kingdom. They founded monasteries in remote, often inaccessible locations, like the tops of sheer cliffs and isolated mountains. [citation needed] They chose these locations for their solitude, which provided a space for intense prayer and ascetic discipline, removed from the world's distractions. The monastery of Debre Damo, which Abba Aregawi is said to have founded, is a famous example. It is accessible only by climbing a 60-foot rope up a sheer cliff face.

These monasteries became the spiritual powerhouses of the Ethiopian Church. They were centers of prayer and worship, but also of scholarship and art. Monks painstakingly copied the Scriptures and other holy books, preserving them for future generations. The saints are also credited with translating many important theological works from Syriac and Greek into Geʽez. This work enriched the intellectual life of the church. Over time, these monastic centers became the primary custodians of Ethiopian culture. They preserved its language, literature, and history, and deeply wove the Christian faith into the fabric of national identity.

> **Key Term:** **The Nine Saints** — A group of missionaries who, according to tradition, came to Ethiopia in the late 5th or early 6th century. They were instrumental in spreading monasticism and are credited with founding many of the country's most famous ancient monasteries.

> **Key Term:** **Monasticism** — A religious way of life where one renounces worldly pursuits to devote oneself fully to spiritual work. Ethiopian monasticism, shaped by the Nine Saints, emphasized asceticism, prayer, and the preservation of sacred knowledge.

> **Debate Box — The Nine Saints: foreign missionaries or adapted local agents?**
> The tradition of the Nine Saints emphasizes their foreign origins, linking Ethiopia to the wider Christian world. However, some scholars question if the stories are literal history. Is it possible the "Nine Saints" are a symbolic narrative for a broader monastic expansion, led by local Ethiopians who adapted foreign traditions? The stories of their miraculous feats, like a giant serpent lifting Abba Aregawi up a cliff, suggest a legendary quality. Does it matter if the saints were foreigners or locals? What does the *story* of their foreign origin tell us about how the Ethiopian Church saw its place in the Christian world?

### Theological Reflection
The monastic movement in Ethiopia is a powerful evidence of the Christian call to a life of radical discipleship. The monks, like the desert fathers before them, took seriously Jesus's call to deny themselves, take up their cross, and follow Him. Their retreat from the world was not an escape from reality. It was a courageous confrontation with the spiritual realities of prayer, fasting, and spiritual warfare. They established "cities of God" in the wilderness, places where the worship of God was the central organizing principle of life. These monasteries became spiritual wells from which the entire nation could draw living water. They remind us that the Church's health depends not only on its public proclamation but also on the deep, hidden life of prayer and devotion. The quiet, patient, and often anonymous labor of these monks—copying manuscripts, praying the psalms, and mentoring younger believers—preserved the faith. They created a legacy of spiritual beauty that continues to enrich the world.
