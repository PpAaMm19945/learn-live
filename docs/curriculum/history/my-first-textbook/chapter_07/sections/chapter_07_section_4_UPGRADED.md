## 7.4 The Christian Mission: Frumentius and King Ezana (c. 320–360 AD)

In this section you will learn about the pivotal moment when Aksum officially adopted Christianity. We'll explore the dramatic story of Frumentius and the archaeological evidence left by King Ezana.

### Quick Start
*   **Learning Focus:** Examine the historical and archaeological evidence for Aksum's conversion.
*   **Key Idea:** The conversion of Aksum was catalyzed by a shipwrecked Syrian missionary, Frumentius, and solidified by King Ezana, who left a permanent record of his faith on coins and stone.

### The Shipwrecked Tutor: Frumentius
The story of Aksum's conversion begins with a tragedy turned into triumph. Around **316 AD**, a ship returning from India was attacked at a Red Sea port. Two young Syrian Christians, **Frumentius** and **Aedesius**, survived and were taken as slaves to the court of King **Ella Amida**. Impressed by their wisdom, the king made Frumentius the tutor to his young son and heir, **Ezana**.

When the king died, Frumentius helped the queen regent rule until Ezana came of age. Having sown the seeds of faith in the young prince's heart, Frumentius traveled to Alexandria (Egypt) around **330 AD**. He asked the great bishop **Athanasius** to send a bishop to the growing Christian community in Aksum. Athanasius, recognizing God's hand, consecrated Frumentius himself as the first *Abuna* (Bishop) of Ethiopia, giving him the name *Kesate Birhan* ("Revealer of Light").

### King Ezana: The Constantine of Africa
King **Ezana** (reigned c. 320–360 AD) became the first African king to officially embrace Christianity, roughly contemporary with the Roman Emperor Constantine. His reign marked a golden age of expansion; in **c. 350 AD**, he marched north and conquered the declining kingdom of **Meroe** (Kush), effectively ending that ancient civilization and uniting the Nile Valley and Highlands under Aksumite rule.

### Evidence in Stone and Gold
Ezana's conversion is not just a legend; it is a historical fact stamped onto the material culture of the kingdom.
*   **Inscriptions:** Ezana left trilingual inscriptions (Ge'ez, Sabaean, Greek) recording his victories. His early inscriptions praise the pagan god Mahrem (Ares). His later inscriptions, however, open with: "By the power of the Lord of Heaven, who in heaven and upon earth is mightier than everything that exists."
*   **Coins:** Aksum was the only sub-Saharan African state to mint its own currency in gold, silver, and bronze. Early coins bear the pagan crescent and disc. Coins from the middle of Ezana's reign suddenly replace these symbols with the **Cross**—the first time the Christian cross ever appeared on coinage anywhere in the world.

> **Key Term:** **Abuna** — "Our Father," the title of the head of the Ethiopian Church. Following the precedent of Frumentius, the *Abuna* was historically an Egyptian monk appointed by the Patriarch of Alexandria, linking the two churches for 1,600 years.

> **Key Term:** **Ezana Stone** — A stone slab with inscriptions in three languages (Ge'ez, Sabaean, Greek). Like the Rosetta Stone, it was a key to deciphering ancient scripts and provides irrefutable proof of Ezana's conversion.

### Theological Reflection: The Sovereignty of God in History
The conversion of Aksum illustrates how God weaves individual lives into the destiny of nations. A shipwreck, seemingly a disaster, placed Frumentius in the royal court at the precise moment to influence the future king. This parallels the story of Joseph in Egypt or Daniel in Babylon. In addition, Ezana's public confession of Christ ("Lord of Heaven") transformed the identity of his people. It reminds us that the Gospel claims lordship not just over private hearts, but over culture, leadership, and the public square.
