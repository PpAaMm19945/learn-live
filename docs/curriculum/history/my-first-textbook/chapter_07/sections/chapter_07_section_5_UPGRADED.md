## 7.5 The Nine Saints: Syrian Refugees and the Golden Age of Ge'ez (c. 480–520 AD)

In this section you will learn about the "Nine Saints," a group of foreign monks who fled persecution and transformed the Ethiopian Church. We'll explore their role in translating the Bible and founding the monasteries that would preserve the faith for centuries.

### Quick Start
*   **Learning Focus:** Understand the impact of the Nine Saints on Ethiopian monasticism and literature.
*   **Key Idea:** The arrival of Syrian refugees fleeing the Council of Chalcedon sparked a spiritual revival, leading to the translation of the Bible into Ge'ez and the establishment of the "Holy Mountains" of Ethiopia.

While Frumentius planted the seeds of the Gospel, it was the arrival of the "Nine Saints" (*Tsadkan*) in the late 5th century that caused the faith to fully bloom in the hearts of the people. These men were not conquerors or traders, but refugees. They were monks from Syria, Egypt, and Cappadocia who fled the Byzantine Empire after the Council of Chalcedon (451 AD) condemned their Miaphysite theology. Seeking a safe haven where they could practice their faith in peace, they found refuge in Aksum.

### The Founders and Their Fortresses
The Nine Saints did not stay in the comforts of the capital. They ventured into the rugged countryside to establish centers of prayer and learning.
*   **Abba Aragawi** founded **Debre Damo**, the most famous monastery in Ethiopia. Located on a flat-topped mountain (*amba*) with sheer cliffs on all sides, it is accessible only by a 15-meter (50 ft) leather rope, which visitors must climb to this day. This "fortress of prayer" has remained a center of monastic life for over 1,500 years.
*   **Abba Garima** established the **Abba Garima Monastery** near Adwa. He is credited with producing the *Garima Gospels*, two illuminated manuscript books. Recent radiocarbon dating places these manuscripts between 390 and 660 AD, making them the **oldest complete illuminated Christian manuscripts in the world**.
*   **Abba Aftse** settled at **Yeha**, the site of an ancient pre-Aksumite temple. By building his church on this site, he symbolically claimed the ancient roots of the culture for Christ.
*   **Abba Yem'ata** founded a church in the **Gheralta** mountains, carving it into a vertical rock face 200 meters above the valley floor. The climb to reach it is perilous, a evidence of the monks' desire for absolute solitude and dependence on God.

### The Word in the Tongue of the People
The greatest legacy of the Nine Saints was the completion of the Ge'ez Bible. While translation may have begun earlier, these scholar-monks finished the task, translating the Old Testament from the Greek Septuagint and the New Testament from Greek and Syriac sources. This was a monumental achievement. Unlike the church in North Africa, which retained Latin and eventually disappeared under Islamic rule, the Ethiopian Church possessed the Scriptures in its own language. This "indigenization" of the Word meant that Christianity was not a foreign religion but the faith of the ancestors, deeply rooted in the soil of the culture.

> **Key Term:** **Debre Damo** — A 6th-century monastery founded by Abba Aragawi. It is the oldest existing church building in Ethiopia and preserves the Aksumite style of architecture, featuring layers of stone and timber ("monkey-head" construction).

> **Key Term:** **Garima Gospels** — The world's oldest surviving illuminated Gospels, kept at the Abba Garima Monastery. Their existence confirms the high level of artistic and literary culture in Aksum during the time of the Nine Saints.

### Theological Reflection: The Blessing of the Refugee
The story of the Nine Saints challenges our modern view of refugees. These men came with nothing but their faith and their learning, yet they enriched their host country beyond measure. They remind us that God often uses the displaced and the persecuted to carry His truth to new places (Acts 8:4). Their flight from persecution in Byzantium led to a golden age of faith in Ethiopia. In addition, their work in translating the Bible fulfills the pattern of Pentecost (Acts 2), where every nation hears the mighty works of God in its own tongue. The survival of the Ethiopian Church through centuries of isolation is directly linked to this foundational work: because the Word was in their hearts and on their lips in their own language, no external force could take it away.
