## Primary Source Spotlight Answer Keys

### Section 7.2: The Ezana Inscription

*   **Analysis Question 1:** How does this inscription differ from the religious language of earlier Aksumite kings? What does this tell us about the change in Ezana's beliefs?
    *   **Model Answer:** Earlier Aksumite inscriptions dedicated victories to polytheistic gods like Mahrem (Ares). Ezana's inscription is radically different because it explicitly invokes the Christian Trinity ("Father, Son, and Holy Spirit"). This shows a fundamental shift from a polytheistic worldview to a monotheistic, specifically Christian, belief system. It indicates that Ezana's conversion was not just a private belief but a public declaration that he attributed his power and success to the Christian God.
*   **Analysis Question 2:** Why do you think Ezana chose to have this inscription written in three different languages? What does this reveal about Aksum's international relationships?
    *   **Model Answer:** The use of Geʽez, Sabaean, and Greek demonstrates Aksum's position as an international crossroads. Geʽez was the official language of his kingdom. Sabaean was the language of the neighboring kingdom of Saba (in South Arabia), indicating Aksum's influence and engagement across the Red Sea. Greek was the lingua franca of the Roman Empire and eastern Mediterranean trade. Using Greek was a way for Ezana to communicate his conversion and power to the wider Roman/Byzantine world, signaling his entry into the community of Christian nations.

### Section 7.6: The Tabot

*   **Analysis Question 1:** The reverence for the *Tabot* connects the Ethiopian church directly to the Old Testament. How might this emphasis on the Old Testament have shaped the way Ethiopian Christians understood their identity as the "New Israel"?
    *   **Model Answer:** The emphasis on the *Tabot*, a replica of the Ark of the Covenant, powerfully shaped the Ethiopian Church's identity as a "New Israel." It suggested a transfer of divine presence and covenantal favor from ancient Israel to Ethiopia. This practice would have encouraged Ethiopian Christians to see their nation as a new chosen people, with a special relationship to God. It frames their history and rituals in Old Testament terms, giving them a deep sense of historical and spiritual continuity with the people of the Bible.
*   **Analysis Question 2:** This practice is unique to Ethiopia. What does the development of such a distinctive local tradition tell us about the process of inculturation, where the universal Christian faith takes on local cultural expression?
    *   **Model Answer:** The *Tabot* tradition is a prime example of inculturation. It shows that when the universal Christian faith enters a new culture, it doesn't remain an abstract set of beliefs. Instead, it is expressed through tangible, culturally specific symbols and practices that resonate with the local people. The development of the *Tabot* tradition demonstrates that the Ethiopian Church was not passively receiving a foreign religion but was actively shaping its expression of Christianity, creating new rituals that were both deeply Christian and uniquely Ethiopian.

## Chapter 7 Review Worksheet Answer Key

### Short Answer Questions

1.  **What were the two main sources of the Aksumite kingdom's economic power?**
    *   The two main sources of Aksum's economic power were its fertile highlands, which supported agriculture and a large population, and its strategic control over the Red Sea trade routes via the port of Adulis, which connected Africa to the Roman Empire and India.
2.  **What are the two main forms of evidence for King Ezana's conversion to Christianity?**
    *   The two main forms of evidence are his monumental inscriptions (like the Ezana Stone), which shift from dedicating victories to pagan gods to the Christian God, and his coinage, which replaced the pre-Christian symbols of the disc and crescent with the cross.
3.  **What was the "Alexandrian connection," and why was it important for the Ethiopian Church?**
    *   The "Alexandrian connection" was the tradition whereby the head of the Ethiopian Church (the *Abuna*) was appointed by the Coptic Pope of Alexandria. This was important because it linked Ethiopia to a major, ancient center of Christianity, shaping its theology (specifically its Miaphysite Christology) and institutional life for over 1,600 years.
4.  **Briefly describe the motivations behind King Kaleb's invasion of Himyar.**
    *   King Kaleb's invasion was driven by a complex mix of motives. Religiously, he acted to defend persecuted Christians in Himyar. Geopolitically, it was a move to counter the rival power of Himyar, which was aligning with Persia. Economically, it was a way to secure the vital Red Sea trade routes for Aksum and its Byzantine allies.
5.  **What was the role of monasteries in the preservation of Ethiopian culture?**
    *   Monasteries were the primary cultural custodians in Ethiopia, especially after the decline of Aksum. Monks painstakingly copied the Scriptures and other religious texts, preserving the Geʽez language and literature. They became the centers of education, scholarship, and national identity.
6.  **What major historical event led to the decline of Aksum's power on the world stage?**
    *   The rise of Islam in the 7th century and the subsequent expansion of the Arab caliphates was the primary event that led to Aksum's decline. This shifted control of the Red Sea trade routes away from Aksum, cutting off its main source of wealth and leading to its economic and political marginalization.
