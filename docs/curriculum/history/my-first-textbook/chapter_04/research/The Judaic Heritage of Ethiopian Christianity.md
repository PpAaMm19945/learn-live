The Judaic Heritage of Ethiopian Christianity
Ethiopian tradition ties the Tewahedo Church to ancient Israel – for example through the Queen of Sheba legend – but what do the facts say? Archaeological and historical evidence suggests that Judaic customs did reach the highlands long before European contact, even if the famous Solomon-Sheba story is a much later epic. In Axumite-era Ethiopia (1st c. BCE–6th c. CE), one finds clear Semitic links: excavations have uncovered pre-Christian temples built in a South Arabian (Sabaean) style, reflecting deep Arabian ties
smithsonianmag.com
. Intriguingly, a royal stele of King Ezana (4th c. CE) records that he marched on a Saturday (the 8th of Magabit)
scielo.org.za
, a detail interpreted by some as an early reference to the Sabbath (the inscription even uses the title “Lord of Heaven,” echoing the Hebrew יְהוָה or “Lord of Hosts”
scielo.org.za
). However, Ezana’s monuments never claim any Solomonic ancestry – they invoke local deities and honor his Ethiopian lineage – and late sources place Sheba in Arabia, not Abyssinia
researchgate.net
researchgate.net
. In short, the striking story of Makeda and Menelik is a later national myth, whereas the material evidence (coins, stelae, architecture) shows that Axum was a literate Semitic realm with both pagan and emerging Christian symbolism, some of which resonates with Jewish tradition
smithsonianmag.com
scielo.org.za
.
Beta Israel: Ancient Judaic Traditions
Ethiopia’s historic Jewish community – the Beta Israel (House of Israel) – embodies the question of an ancient Jewish presence. Their own lore links them to Israel (even Solomon’s son, Menelik, in legend), and by the 14th century they are unambiguously recorded as “Jews” in royal chronicles. Modern scholars propose several theories:
Israelite or Arabian origins: Some suggest Beta Israel descend from Judean refugees or traders who crossed the Red Sea. For instance, waves of South Arabian (Sabaean) migration in the early 1st millennium BCE may have included Jewish exiles, escaping Assyrian or Babylonian conquest
en.wikipedia.org
. In these models, some Israelites reached the Horn of Africa, preserving Old Testament faith outside Palestine.
Local Agaw converts: Another view is that Beta Israel were originally local Agaw people converted by a small Jewish community in southern Arabia. These converts adopted Mosaic customs and developed an independent Jewish identity in the highlands
en.wikipedia.org
.
Medieval development: A third hypothesis holds that Beta Israel formed much later (roughly 9th–14th c. CE) from a Judaizing sect of Ethiopian Christians or pagans, adopting biblical law under Islam-era isolation.
Regardless of its exact origin, Beta Israel religion is notably pre-rabbinic. Their liturgy and scripture are in Ge‘ez, and they maintain a form of Mosaic Judaism (blood sacrifice outside a Temple, unique festivals) that lacks Rabbinic Talmud or prayers
smarthistory.org
. In fact, Beta Israel strictly observe the Pentateuch, following Sabbath, dietary laws and male circumcision as in ancient Israel
smarthistory.org
en.wikipedia.org
. This continuity suggests a very old Judaic substratum: one study notes that some Beta Israel practices mirror those of late Second-Temple Jewish sects whose traditions are extinct elsewhere
en.wikipedia.org
. In sum, Beta Israel themselves bear witness to a deep-rooted Jewish heritage in Ethiopia, even as scholars debate whether it came via migration or local evolution.
Biblical Legend vs. Historical Record
The romantic Queen-of-Sheba narrative is well-known in Ethiopia, but it must be weighed against contemporary evidence. Importantly, Aksumite-era inscriptions do not reflect it: Axumite kings never styled themselves as descended from Israel, and Solomon or Menelik never appear in their royal titles. The Kebra Nagast (14th c. CE) is the first text to weave the Solomonic legend
researchgate.net
. Earlier sources are silent. For example, the 6th-century Byzantine-Egyptian writer Cosmas Indicopleustes specifically identifies “Saba” with the Himyarite kingdom of Arabia, treating Ethiopia only as a source of incense
researchgate.net
. In other words, Late Antique evidence places Sheba in Arabia, not Abyssinia. Thus historians regard the Solomon–Makeda saga as a later national myth: evocative for cultural identity, but not proof of a historical Jewish kingdom in Ethiopia
researchgate.net
researchgate.net
.
Judaic Traditions in the Tewahedo Church
Even as Christianity became dominant, many Hebrew customs persisted in Ethiopia. Aksumite coinage itself illustrates a shift: after Ezana’s conversion (~330 CE), traditional celestial symbols on coins were replaced by the Christian cross
smarthistory.org
. Yet Mosaic practices remained in the church. Ethiopian Christians (and formerly Beta Israel) circumcise male infants on the eighth day of life, following Genesis 17
researchgate.net
; this matches Old Testament law and differs from neighboring Christians (Coptic practice is much later). Likewise, pork was universally avoided: archaeologists note virtually no pig bones at Axumite sites
researchgate.net
. (Hatke points out that pork-avoidance was common across Northeast Africa – absent at Iron Age Yemen and Nubia
researchgate.net
 – so this taboo may be regional, though medieval Ethiopians retroactively traced it to the Old Testament.) Importantly, the Saturday Sabbath also became entrenched. Ethiopian Christians long celebrated both Sunday and Saturday, a practice firmly documented by the medieval era
researchgate.net
. In short, the Tewahedo Church retained many Old Testament customs (diet, Sabbath, circumcision, use of the “tabot” altars, etc.), setting it apart from most other Eastern churches. These features reflect an indigenous Hebraic imprint: the Gospel was preached into an already Old-Testament–minded society, producing a uniquely Jewish-patterned Christianity in Ethiopia. In conclusion, the evidence points to a genuine ancient Judaic influence in Ethiopia, even as legend embellishes it. The Beta Israel community’s traditions and the Ethiopian Church’s ritual life strongly mirror pre-Christian Jewish practice
smarthistory.org
researchgate.net
. While the Solomonic queen and Ark saga provide spiritual inspiration, real “hard” evidence – from Ge‘ez tomb inscriptions and dietary remains to liturgical customs – shows that Judaism left deep roots in the highlands long before European missionaries arrived
smarthistory.org
