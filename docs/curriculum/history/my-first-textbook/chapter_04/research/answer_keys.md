## Section 4.1: Think It Through

*   **Data Analysis:**
    *   **Challenges:** The rugged highlands might have made agriculture and transportation difficult. The Nile's cataracts (rapids) would have been obstacles to river travel and trade. The vast distances could have made communication and unification challenging.
    *   **Opportunities:** The highlands offered fertile soil and good rainfall for agriculture, providing a stable food source. The Nile provided a reliable source of water. The region's location made it a natural crossroads for trade between the African interior, Egypt, and the Red Sea.

## Section 4.3: Think It Through

*   **Primary Source Analysis (Isaiah 37:8-9):**
    *   The passage shows that the Assyrian field commander, one of the most powerful military leaders in the world at the time, took the threat of Tirhakah, king of Cush, very seriously. The mere news that Tirhakah was marching out to fight him was enough to make him send a threatening message to Hezekiah, suggesting he felt pressured. This indicates that Cush was perceived as a major military power, capable of challenging a superpower like Assyria on the battlefield.

## Section 4.7: Primary Source Spotlight

*   **Analytic Prompt 1 (Ezana's motivations):**
    *   Ezana's stated motivations were primarily political and economic, not religious. He claims the Noba people had wronged him by attacking his subjects and merchants, thus interfering with Aksum's trade and security. The invasion was framed as a punitive expedition to secure his borders and punish those who had disrupted Aksum's sphere of influence.
*   **Analytic Prompt 2 (Three languages):**
    *   Writing the inscription in Ge'ez, Greek, and Sabaean was a statement of Aksum's international standing. Ge'ez was the official language of his kingdom. Greek was the lingua franca of the Roman and Hellenistic world, the superpower to the north. Sabaean was the language of the influential kingdoms in South Arabia, a key trading partner. By using all three, Ezana was proclaiming his victory and his importance to the three major cultural and economic zones that his empire touched. It was a declaration that Aksum was a peer to these other great civilizations.
