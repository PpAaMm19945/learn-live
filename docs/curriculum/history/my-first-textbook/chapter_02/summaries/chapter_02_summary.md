# Chapter 2 Summary: The Story of Egypt

Chapter 2 traces the epic, three-millennia history of ancient Egypt, from its origins to its final conquest by Rome, all through the lens of God’s sovereign plan. The chapter presents Egypt as the archetypal kingdom of man—powerful, innovative, and proud—and shows how its entire history is interwoven with the biblical narrative.

The story begins after the dispersal at Babel, with the descendants of Ham’s son **Mizraim** settling along the Nile. In the **Predynastic Period**, they developed a unique culture centered on the river’s rhythms and the concept of **Ma’at** (order) versus *Isfet* (chaos). This laid the foundation for the **Old Kingdom**, where King **Narmer** unified Upper and Lower Egypt, establishing the world’s first nation-state. This era was defined by the divine status of the **Pharaoh** and the construction of the pyramids, which stand as monuments to human ambition and a desire to achieve immortality on human terms.

This glorious age ended in the **First Intermediate Period**, a time of chaos, famine, and civil war that shattered Egypt’s confidence. This suffering, however, led to a new cultural maturity, with literature that questioned divine justice and the meaning of order. Egypt was reunified in the **Middle Kingdom**, its “Classical Age.” The Pharaoh was now seen as a wise “shepherd” of his people. It was during this stable period that **Abraham** first visited Egypt, an encounter that foreshadowed God’s power over the mighty kingdom.

The Middle Kingdom gave way to the **Second Intermediate Period**, marked by the invasion of the **Hyksos**, a Semitic people who introduced the chariot and new weapons. It is within this context of foreign rule that many scholars place the story of **Joseph**, whose rise to power as a fellow Semite becomes historically plausible. Joseph’s wisdom saved Egypt from famine and provided a refuge for the family of Jacob.

The expulsion of the Hyksos by **Ahmose I** launched the **New Kingdom**, Egypt’s imperial age. Pharaohs like **Thutmose III** and **Ramesses II** built a vast empire, funded by conquest and trade. But this golden age was built on the enslavement of the Hebrews, setting the stage for the Exodus. A brief, failed religious revolution by **Akhenaten** to worship the sun-disk, the **Aten**, only hardened Egypt’s polytheistic heart, making God’s judgment necessary.

The decline of Egypt began after the Exodus, leading to the **Late Period**. The nation was weakened internally and dominated by a series of foreign powers: Libyans, Nubian Kushites (who led a remarkable African resurgence), Assyrians, and finally Persians. The biblical prophets now spoke of Egypt not as a superpower, but as a “broken reed”—an unreliable ally.

The final act came during the **Hellenistic Period**, beginning with the conquest of **Alexander the Great**. His general, **Ptolemy I**, established a Greek dynasty that blended Greek and Egyptian culture. In the vibrant, multicultural city of **Alexandria**, the Hebrew Bible was translated into Greek (the **Septuagint**), an event of immense importance for the future spread of the Gospel. The last Pharaoh, the brilliant **Cleopatra VII**, tried to navigate the rising power of Rome but was eventually defeated. Her death in 30 BC marked the end of sovereign Egypt, which became a Roman province.

In conclusion, the chapter presents Egypt’s legacy as twofold. It gave the world incredible contributions in architecture, science, and writing. But more importantly, its entire history serves as a biblical object lesson.
