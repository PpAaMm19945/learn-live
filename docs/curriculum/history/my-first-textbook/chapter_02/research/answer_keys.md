## Primary Source Exercise Answer Keys

### Exercise 1: Ipuwer Papyrus (from Section 3)

**Primary Text:** "Behold, the fire has gone up on high. Its burning goes forth against the enemies of the land... Behold, the river is blood, yet men drink of it. Men shrink from human beings and thirst after water. Behold, the son of a man of rank is no longer recognized... The land is without light. Poor men have become owners of wealth; he who could not make sandals for himself is now a possessor of riches."

**Question:** Identify two claims in the excerpt that align with the chapter's narrative of chaos and one major limitation the excerpt has as historical evidence.

**Answer Key:**
*   **Alignment 1:** The text directly aligns with the chapter's description of chaos in the First Intermediate Period. The phrases "the river is blood" and "the land is without light" echo the environmental and social collapse described.
*   **Alignment 2:** The social upheaval mentioned, where "Poor men have become owners of wealth" and a "man of rank is no longer recognized," perfectly matches the narrative of nomarchs seizing power and the central authority of the Pharaoh dissolving.
*   **Limitation:** The primary limitation is that the text is literature, not a historical chronicle. It uses poetic, hyperbolic language to convey a feeling of chaos. We cannot know if the river was literally blood or if this is a metaphor for violence and death. Its contested dating also makes it difficult to tie to a specific, verifiable event.

### Exercise 2: Biblical Kingship (from Section 5)

**Primary Text:** "When you come to the land that the LORD your God is giving you, and you possess it and dwell in it and then say, ‘I will set a king over me, like all the nations that are around me,’ you may indeed set a king over you whom the LORD your God will choose... Only he must not acquire many horses for himself... And he shall not acquire many wives for himself, lest his heart turn away, nor shall he acquire for himself excessive silver and gold... And when he sits on the throne of his kingdom, he shall write for himself in a book a copy of this law... and it shall be with him, and he shall read in it all the days of his life, that he may learn to fear the LORD his God by keeping all the words of this law and these statutes, and doing them." (Deuteronomy 17:14-20)

**Question:** Based on this text, identify two specific limitations placed on a biblical king that stand in stark contrast to the powers of an Egyptian Pharaoh.

**Answer Key:**
*   **Contrast 1 (Limitation on Power/Wealth):** The text explicitly forbids the king from acquiring "many horses" (military power), "many wives" (political alliances), or "excessive silver and gold" (personal wealth). This is in direct opposition to the Egyptian Pharaoh, whose power was absolute and whose wealth, by definition, was limitless and a reflection of his divine status.
*   **Contrast 2 (Accountability to Law):** The most significant contrast is the king's relationship to the law. The biblical king is commanded to write and read his own copy of God's law, making him subject to it. The Pharaoh, on the other hand, *was* the law; his word was the ultimate authority, and he was not accountable to any external legal standard.

## Chapter 2 Review Worksheet Answer Key

### Part 1: Short-Answer Factual Questions

1.  **King Narmer** is credited with unifying Egypt, an event depicted on the **Narmer Palette**.
2.  The Hyksos introduced the **horse-drawn chariot** and advanced bronze weapons like the **composite bow**.
3.  The Septuagint was the **Greek translation of the Hebrew Old Testament**. It was significant because it was the version of scripture most often used by the apostles and the early church to spread the gospel in the Greek-speaking Roman world.
4.  The "heretic" pharaoh was **Akhenaten**, and he promoted the worship of the **Aten**.
5.  The last pharaoh was **Cleopatra VII**, who was defeated by **Octavian** (later Emperor Augustus).
6.  After being annexed by Rome, Egypt's primary role was to be the **"breadbasket of Rome,"** supplying the capital with grain.

### Part 2: Critical-Thinking Prompts

1.  **(Ma'at vs. Isfet):** Student answers should explain that the Pharaoh was seen as the divine guarantor of *Ma'at*. His job was to maintain cosmic order, which was reflected in the predictable flooding of the Nile and the stability of the nation. During the First Intermediate Period, when the floods failed and civil war broke out (*Isfet*), it triggered a theological crisis. If the Pharaoh could not uphold *Ma'at*, his divine status and the entire basis of the state were called into question, leading to the despair and introspection seen in the era's literature.
2.  **(Divine vs. Biblical Kingship):** Students should identify two key differences. **Source of Authority:** The Pharaoh's authority was inherent; he *was* a god. A biblical king's authority was delegated; he was *chosen by* God and was only legitimate as long as he was obedient. **Accountability:** The Pharaoh was theoretically accountable to no one on earth. The biblical king was explicitly accountable to God and was commanded to be subject to God's written law (the Torah), a standard outside of himself.
3.  **("Broken Reed" Metaphor):** Students should explain that the metaphor was fitting because, during the Late Period, Egypt looked powerful but was internally weak and unreliable. It was conquered by a series of foreign powers (Kushites, Assyrians, Persians), proving it could not defend itself, let alone be a trustworthy ally for nations like Judah. This contrasts sharply with the New Kingdom, when Egypt was an aggressive, expansionist empire and the world's dominant superpower. The "reed" appeared strong but would snap under pressure.
4.  **(God's Providence in Foreign Rule):** Student answers should connect the two eras to God's plan. **Hyksos Period:** God used this period of foreign (Semitic) rule to create a plausible political environment for Joseph (another Semite) to rise to power. This act of providence saved the nascent nation of Israel from famine and positioned them in Egypt for the next stage of their history. **Hellenistic Period:** God used the Greek-speaking empire of the Ptolemies to create the conditions for the Septuagint's translation in Alexandria. This provided a universal language for the Old Testament, which was essential for the rapid spread of the Gospel beyond Judea in the New Testament era. In both cases, God worked through pagan, foreign powers to prepare the way for His redemptive purposes.
