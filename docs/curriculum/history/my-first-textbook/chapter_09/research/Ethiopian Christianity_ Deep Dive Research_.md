# **The Ark, the Cross, and the Mountain Kingdom: A Historical and Theological Analysis of Ethiopian Christianity from Antiquity to the Early Medieval Period**

## **Introduction**

Ethiopian Christianity stands as one of the most ancient and distinctive traditions within the global Christian family. Its history is not merely a regional variation of a more familiar narrative but a unique trajectory forged in the highlands of Africa, shaped by its own sacred epics, deep-seated Judaic heritage, and a singular theological vision. Unlike many Christian communities that were either absorbed by larger imperial churches or extinguished by geopolitical shifts, the Ethiopian Church developed and endured in relative isolation, cultivating a rich and self-sufficient civilization. This report provides a comprehensive analysis of the historical and theological development of Ethiopian Christianity from its ancient roots to its consolidation in the early medieval period. It examines the foundational pillars that define its identity: a national consciousness rooted in the biblical epic of the *Kebra Nagast*; a profound and complex relationship with a native Judaic community; a dual apostolic and royal conversion narrative that grants it both biblical and political legitimacy; a unique theological and liturgical framework known as *Tewahedo*; and a remarkable history of survival and resilience against overwhelming geopolitical pressures. The central argument of this report is that the identity of the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church was not a passive inheritance. Rather, it was actively and deliberately forged through a dynamic and centuries-long synthesis of myth, history, theology, and sophisticated institutional strategies, resulting in a Christian tradition that is unparalleled in its antiquity and character.

## **Part I: The Glory of Kings \- Forging a National-Religious Identity**

The national and religious identity of Ethiopia is inextricably linked to a foundational epic that intertwines the nation's destiny with the sacred history of ancient Israel. This narrative, codified in the 14th-century text known as the *Kebra Nagast*, provided the ideological bedrock for the Ethiopian state for over 700 years, legitimizing its monarchy and defining its people's unique place in divine history.

### **1.1 The Narrative of the Kebra Nagast ("The Glory of Kings")**

The *Kebra Nagast*, or "The Glory of the Kings," is a monumental work of Ethiopian literature, composed in the classical liturgical language of Ge'ez in the 14th century.1 Far from being a simple collection of folklore, it is a complex and masterful "gigantic conflation of legendary cycles" that draws extensively from the Hebrew Bible, apocryphal traditions, and local oral histories to construct a coherent and powerful national origin story.2 The text frames its narrative as a debate among the 318 "orthodox fathers" of the First Council of Nicaea, lending it an air of ancient ecumenical authority.2

The core of the epic revolves around the fateful encounter between two legendary monarchs. The story begins with Tamrin, a wealthy merchant in the service of the ruler of Ethiopia, Queen Makeda.1 (While she is popularly known as Makeda, this name does not appear in the

*Kebra Nagast* itself and is a later attribution of unknown origin.1) Tamrin's trade journeys bring him to Jerusalem, where he is deeply impressed by the wisdom and wealth of King Solomon.1 Upon his return, his effusive accounts captivate the Queen, who resolves to travel to Jerusalem herself to witness Solomon's famed wisdom firsthand.1

Makeda's journey to Jerusalem marks a pivotal moment in the narrative. She engages Solomon in deep intellectual and spiritual discourse, testing his wisdom with riddles and questions. Overwhelmed by his knowledge and the grandeur of his court, she renounces her kingdom's traditional worship of the sun and declares her allegiance to Solomon's deity, stating, "From this moment I will not worship the sun, but will worship the Creator of the sun, the God of Israel".2 This conversion establishes the theological foundation for the events that follow.

The relationship between the monarchs soon moves from the intellectual to the personal. On the eve of Makeda's departure, Solomon, desiring an heir from her, devises a cunning plan. After making her swear an oath not to take anything from his house by force, he serves her a spicy meal and denies her water, later tricking her into drinking from a goblet placed by her bed. He construes this act as her breaking her oath, thereby releasing him from his own promise not to take her by force, and they consummate their relationship.1 As a token, Solomon gives her his ring, so that any child born of their union could later identify himself.2

On her journey back to Ethiopia, Makeda gives birth to a son, whom she names Bayna-Lehkem, later known as Menelik I.1 When Menelik reaches the age of 22, he travels to Jerusalem, using the ring to prove his parentage to a joyous King Solomon. Solomon offers to make Menelik his successor in Israel, but Menelik insists on returning to his mother's kingdom.2

The climax of the *Kebra Nagast* is the transfer of Israel's most sacred object—the Ark of the Covenant, referred to in the text as Zion—to Ethiopia. Solomon, acceding to Menelik's return, commands that the first-born sons of his kingdom's nobles and priests accompany the young prince to establish a new Israelite-style kingdom in Ethiopia. Deeply resentful of being forced to leave their homeland, these young men, led by Azariah, the son of the high priest Zadok, conspire to steal the Ark from the Temple. They succeed in their plot, removing the Ark in secret and replacing it with a replica.2 The narrative emphasizes that this act was not mere theft but was divinely ordained; an angel had appeared to Azariah, sanctioning the removal of the Ark and predicting the future downfall of Israel due to Solomon's impending sin of idolatry.3

During the return journey, Menelik and his entourage discover that the Ark is with them. Its miraculous power is immediately evident, allowing their caravan to travel at supernatural speed. When Solomon discovers the Ark's absence and his son's departure, he is devastated and gives chase, but it is too late. Menelik and his followers are miraculously transported back to Ethiopia.2 The epic concludes with the Ark's arrival in Aksum, an event that symbolizes the transference of God's covenant and divine favor. Ethiopia, through the line of Solomon and the possession of the Ark, is established as the new Zion, and its people become God's new "chosen" people.1

### **1.2 The Solomonic Mandate and its Historical Function**

The *Kebra Nagast* is far more than a religious text or a collection of folklore; it is a sophisticated "politico-religious epic" whose creation and dissemination were intimately tied to a pivotal moment of political transformation in medieval Ethiopia.2 Its primary historical function was to provide an unassailable ideological foundation for the Solomonic dynasty, which seized power in 1270 CE.

The rise of the Solomonic dynasty was the result of a political upheaval. Its founder, Yekuno Amlak, overthrew the last king of the preceding Zagwe dynasty.5 To secure his rule and distinguish his lineage from that of his predecessors, Yekuno Amlak needed a powerful source of legitimacy. The narrative of the

*Kebra Nagast*, codified just over a century later, provided exactly that. By tracing the lineage of Ethiopian monarchs directly back to King Solomon and the Queen of Sheba, the epic masterfully framed Yekuno Amlak's ascendance not as a violent usurpation but as the righteous "restoration" of the one true royal line that had been interrupted by the Zagwe.5 This claim to an ancient, divinely sanctioned heritage became the central pillar of Solomonic propaganda and statecraft.7

This process of legitimation demonstrates a sophisticated understanding of how narrative can be used to construct a past that serves a present political purpose. The *Kebra Nagast* was composed in the 14th century, centuries after the events it purports to describe, yet its timing was perfect for the needs of the new dynasty that had come to power in 1270\.1 The dynasty required a foundational myth to justify its rule, and the

*Kebra Nagast* delivered it by creating a 3,000-year-old lineage. This retroactively applied charter transformed a contemporary power grab into a divinely mandated return to order, a classic example of using a mythological past to secure a political present. The epic was not a passively inherited legend but a deliberately crafted instrument of state-building.

In addition, the Solomonic myth forged an unbreakable bond between the monarchy, the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church, and the very concept of the Ethiopian nation. By claiming descent from the House of David, the Solomonic kings established a direct genealogical link to Christ himself, enveloping their reign in an "aura of divinity" that greatly enhanced their authority in the eyes of their Christian subjects.5 This divine mandate was a potent force for achieving political cohesion, territorial expansion, and a remarkable "revival of Christian culture".6 The Ethiopian Orthodox Church, for its part, was a willing and crucial partner in this arrangement. Eager for a strong political leadership capable of confronting the growing threat of Islamic sultanates on its borders and expanding the frontiers of Christendom, the Church threw its considerable weight behind the new dynasty.5 Influential monastic leaders, such as Iyasus Moa and Tekle Haymanot, are credited with playing key roles in the rise of the Solomonids and cementing the church-state alliance that would define Ethiopian governance for the next seven centuries.5 In return for its loyalty and support, the Church received immense imperial patronage, including vast grants of land, which secured its institutional power.5

The enduring power of this foundational myth is perhaps its most remarkable feature. The Solomonic dynasty, legitimized by the *Kebra Nagast*, became one of the longest-reigning dynasties in world history, ruling Ethiopia from 1270 until the deposition of Emperor Haile Selassie in 1974\.2 The belief in this lineage was so deeply embedded in the national consciousness that the 1955 Ethiopian Constitution explicitly stated that the emperor "descends without interruption from the dynasty of Menelik I, son of Queen of Ethiopia, the Queen of Sheba and King Solomon of Jerusalem".6 Thus, a 14th-century epic succeeded in shaping the political and religious identity of a nation for the better part of a millennium.

## **Part II: The House of Israel in the Highlands \- The Judaic Presence in Ethiopia**

The story of Ethiopian Christianity cannot be fully understood without acknowledging the deep and ancient Judaic substratum upon which it was built. The presence of the Beta Israel, a community practicing a unique form of pre-rabbinic Judaism, is not a peripheral footnote but a central element in the formation of Ethiopia's distinct religious identity. Their contested origins and their long, often fraught, relationship with the Christian kingdom reveal a history of interaction, conflict, and synthesis that deeply shaped both communities.

### **2.1 The Enigma of the Beta Israel: Competing Origin Theories**

The origins of the Beta Israel (a self-referential term meaning "House of Israel") are shrouded in mystery and subject to intense scholarly and communal debate.8 They were historically referred to by their neighbors with the term

*Falasha*, a Ge'ez word meaning "strangers" or "exiles," which the community considers pejorative.9 The debate over their genesis revolves around several competing theories, each with its own body of evidence and critical challenges.

The community's own sacred tradition posits an ancient Israelite lineage. One version of this tradition connects their ancestry directly to the narrative of the *Kebra Nagast*, claiming they are descended from the Israelite nobles, specifically from the tribe of Dan, who were said to have accompanied Menelik I on his return from Jerusalem with the Ark of the Covenant.9 Another tradition maintains that Jews arrived in Ethiopia in successive waves, migrating south via the Nile River and its tributaries.10 This view of ancient descent finds support in the writings of early Jewish travelers and authorities. The 9th-century traveler Eldad Ha-Dani claimed to be a citizen of an independent Jewish state "beyond the rivers of Cush" and a member of the tribe of Dan, a description that aligns with the Beta Israel's location and traditions.9 In the 16th century, the eminent Rabbi David ben Solomon ibn Zimra (the Radbaz) of Egypt ruled that the Beta Israel were "without doubt of the tribe of Dan" and were to be considered fully Jewish according to

*Halacha* (Jewish law).9

In direct opposition to this view is a prominent academic theory, advanced by historians such as James Quirin and Steven Kaplan, which argues that the Beta Israel are of a much more recent origin.9 This "Christian separatist" model proposes that the community emerged between the 14th and 16th centuries from a schismatic movement within Ethiopian Christianity. According to this hypothesis, a group of dissident Christians, perhaps in protest against the centralizing Christian state, adopted and intensified the Judaic practices and beliefs that were already a prominent feature of the Ethiopian Orthodox Church, eventually forming a distinct Jewish-identifying community.9 This theory holds that the Beta Israel are descendants of indigenous, non-Jewish Ethiopians and that their belief in an ancient Israelite origin is a later mythological development.9

Modern scientific research has introduced a third, more complex perspective that challenges the simplicity of both traditional narratives. Genetic studies conducted on the Beta Israel population reveal a nuanced picture. The DNA of Ethiopian Jews is predominantly of East African origin, consistent with the local Habesha populations (Amhara and Tigray-Tigrinya).11 However, these studies also identify a significant minority component—approximately 20%—of their genetic makeup as being of Semitic origin, linking them to peoples of the Middle East, including other Jewish and modern Arab populations.11 In addition, genetic dating of the divergence of the Beta Israel from other populations suggests a founding event that occurred as early as 2,000 years ago, with a small number of Jewish settlers intermarrying with a local population.9 This timeline predates the 14th-century schism proposed by the separatist theory, lending credence to the idea of a more ancient Jewish presence in the region.9 Archaeological evidence further complicates the picture, pointing to a strong historical connection between the Ethiopian highlands and the ancient kingdom of Kush in what is now Sudan, a region with documented Jewish communities, suggesting a possible migration route for Jews into Ethiopia that predates the Christianization of Aksum.9

The history of the Beta Israel, therefore, cannot be separated from the history of Ethiopian Christianity. The very existence of a deeply Judaic form of Christianity in Ethiopia presents a logical challenge to the Christian separatist theory. If the Beta Israel simply adopted their practices from the Church, it begs the question: from where did the Church acquire such a pronounced Hebraic character in the first place? The presence of an established, pre-existing Jewish community, as suggested by the ancient descent and genetic synthesis models, provides a compelling answer. This ancient community would have been a natural source of influence, leading to the development of a syncretic form of Christianity that retained many Judaic practices after the kingdom's official conversion. In this light, the Beta Israel are not an anomaly to be explained away, but are potentially the key to understanding the unique theological fabric of Ethiopian Orthodoxy itself. Their history is not a separate stream, but a foundational current within the broader river of Ethiopian religious history.

### **2.2 A Kingdom Within a Kingdom: The Beta Israel and the Christian State**

The relationship between the Beta Israel and the Ethiopian Christian state was a long and complex saga of conflict, persecution, and pragmatic coexistence. Ethiopian chronicles themselves suggest that Judaism was a common faith in the Aksumite kingdom, which was established by Semitic immigrants from Southern Arabia, prior to the state's official adoption of Christianity in the 4th century CE.10

The conversion of King Ezana marked a turning point. As Christianity became the religion of the state and the court, those who adhered to Judaism faced increasing pressure and persecution. This forced many Jewish communities to retreat from the centers of power into the rugged, defensible highlands, particularly in the Semien Mountains region north of Lake Tana.8 In this relative isolation, they coalesced and gained strength, eventually establishing what their traditions describe as an independent Jewish kingdom.10

This period of Jewish political autonomy reached its zenith, according to tradition, in the 10th century. A powerful Jewish queen named Yehudit (Judith) is said to have led a rebellion of Jewish and other non-Christian Agaw tribes against the declining Aksumite kingdom. Her forces reportedly conquered and destroyed the capital city of Aksum, burned its churches, and established a Jewish dynasty that ruled for 40 years.10 While the details are a mix of history and legend, this event represents a period of significant Jewish political power and a direct challenge to Christian dominance.

The rise of the Solomonic dynasty in 1270 ushered in a new and more aggressive era of conflict. The Solomonid emperors, armed with their legitimizing myth and a mandate to expand the Christian faith, launched sustained campaigns against the Beta Israel. In the 14th century, Emperor Amda Seyon sent troops to pacify the northern provinces where the Beta Israel were prominent.13 His successor, Emperor Yeshaq (r. 1414–1429), intensified this persecution, issuing an edict that forced the Beta Israel to convert to Christianity or lose their rights to own land. It was during this period of dispossession that the label

*Falasha* was systematically applied to them.10 The persecution reached a peak under Emperor Zara Yaqob (r. 1434–1468), who earned the title "Destroyer of the Jews" for his ruthless campaigns.10

Despite this intense pressure, the Beta Israel demonstrated remarkable resilience. They resisted conversion, often at the cost of their lives, and managed to preserve their faith and traditions. This period of persecution even led to the development of a unique form of monasticism within the Beta Israel community, with monks playing a crucial role in preserving sacred texts and resisting forced conversions.10

The relationship was not, however, one of perpetual warfare. In later centuries, particularly during the Gondarine period which began in the 17th century, a more pragmatic coexistence emerged. With the imperial capital established at Gondar, in the heart of a region with a large Beta Israel population, their skills as craftsmen became indispensable to the Christian court. Beta Israel artisans served the emperors as talented masons, ironsmiths, carpenters, and weavers, contributing to the construction of the famous castles and churches of Gondar.13 This demonstrates a complex and fluctuating relationship that could shift from violent religious conflict to periods of practical, symbiotic codependence, shaped by the changing political and economic needs of the Christian state.

## **Part III: The Conversion of the Kingdom**

The Christianization of Ethiopia is recorded in two distinct yet complementary narratives: one providing a timeless, apostolic foundation rooted in the New Testament, and the other a concrete, historical account of royal conversion and state formation. Together, these stories construct a uniquely robust identity for the Ethiopian Church, grounding it in both the sacred time of the apostles and the historical time of kings.

### **3.1 Apostolic Seeds: The Ethiopian Eunuch in Acts 8**

The biblical charter for Ethiopian Christianity is found in the eighth chapter of the Acts of the Apostles. This passage serves as the foundational claim for the church's ancient and apostolic origins, establishing a direct link to the very first generation of Christians, independent of the later patriarchal sees of Rome, Constantinople, or even Alexandria.14

The narrative, recounted in Acts 8:26-39, describes how Philip the Evangelist is guided by an angel to a desert road leading from Jerusalem to Gaza.14 There, he encounters a high-ranking official: an Ethiopian eunuch who held the powerful position of treasurer for the "Candace, Queen of the Ethiopians".14 The title "Candace" (or

*Kandake*) was the Meroitic term for a queen or royal woman of the Kingdom of Kush, a powerful civilization centered in what is now modern Sudan.14 The eunuch, a devout man, was on his return journey after having traveled to Jerusalem to worship.15

As Philip approaches, he finds the eunuch sitting in his chariot, reading aloud from the scroll of the prophet Isaiah, specifically a passage from Isaiah 53 describing a suffering servant led like a sheep to the slaughter.14 Philip asks if he understands what he is reading. The eunuch's reply is one of sincere humility and longing: "How can I understand unless someone guides me?".15 He invites Philip into his chariot, and Philip proceeds to interpret the passage, proclaiming the "good news about Jesus" and explaining that the suffering servant is Christ.16 Captivated by the message, the eunuch sees a body of water and immediately asks to be baptized. Philip baptizes him, and the eunuch goes on his way "rejoicing," his spiritual quest fulfilled.14

This story holds immense theological significance for the Ethiopian Church. It is seen as the moment the Gospel first reached African soil, carried back by a high-ranking member of an African court. Early Church Fathers, such as St. Irenaeus of Lyons writing around 180 CE, identified this eunuch (whom tradition names Simeon Bachos) as the first missionary to the lands of Ethiopia, sent to preach the faith he had just received.14 This narrative provides the church with an unimpeachable apostolic pedigree, rooting its origins in the sacred history of the New Testament itself.

The identity of the eunuch has been a subject of considerable scholarly interpretation, which only adds to the richness of the story. His religious status before his conversion is ambiguous. Was he a born Jew, a Gentile "God-fearer" (a non-Jew who worshipped the God of Israel and followed some Jewish customs), or a full proselyte to Judaism?.14 Modern scholarship often favors the "God-fearer" interpretation, in part because the law in Deuteronomy 23:1 would have excluded a literal eunuch from full conversion to Judaism and from entering the Temple assembly.14 This ambiguity is theologically potent, placing him at the crossroads of the Jewish and Gentile worlds and making his conversion a powerful symbol of the Gospel's universal call. His status as a eunuch, a person from a marginalized group, further underscores a message of radical inclusivity at the heart of the new Christian faith; the very person excluded by the old law is one of the first to be welcomed into the new covenant.14

Historians also note a geographical distinction. The term "Ethiopia" in 1st-century Greco-Roman usage typically referred to the Kingdom of Kush, with its capital at Meroë, not the Aksumite kingdom that would later form the basis of modern Ethiopia.9 While this complicates any attempt to draw a direct, unbroken historical line from the eunuch to the 4th-century Aksumite court, it does not diminish the story's foundational power within the

*tradition* and self-conception of the Ethiopian Church. The narrative's purpose is primarily theological: to establish that the seeds of faith were planted in Ethiopia from the very beginning, by an apostle, at God's express command.

### **3.2 The Royal Proclamation: King Ezana, Frumentius, and the Christianization of Aksum**

While the story of the eunuch provides the apostolic foundation, the historical establishment of Christianity as the religion of the Ethiopian state occurred in the 4th century CE under the Aksumite ruler, King Ezana (r. c. 320s–360 CE).17 This event marks the moment when the faith became inextricably linked with royal power and national identity, a union that would define Ethiopia for the next 1,600 years.

The central figure in this royal conversion, according to the 4th-century historian Tyrannius Rufinus, was Frumentius, a Christian youth from Syria.17 The story recounts that Frumentius and his brother Aedesius were the sole survivors of a shipwreck or attack on the Red Sea coast. Taken to the court of Aksum, their education and integrity impressed the king, and they were given positions of influence. Frumentius eventually became the tutor and chief advisor to the young crown prince, Ezana.17 Over the years, he cultivated a close relationship with the prince and introduced him to the Christian faith, eventually leading to Ezana's conversion in the mid-340s CE.17

Following the king's conversion, a formal ecclesiastical structure was sought. Frumentius traveled to Alexandria, the preeminent center of Christianity in Africa, to request that a bishop be sent to shepherd the new and growing Christian community in Aksum.18 The great patriarch of Alexandria, St. Athanasius, listened to Frumentius's account and concluded that no one was better suited for the role than Frumentius himself. Athanasius therefore consecrated Frumentius as the first bishop, or

*abun*, of Ethiopia.17 This act established a formal hierarchical link with the Coptic Orthodox Church of Alexandria that would endure for sixteen centuries, with the Coptic Patriarch appointing the head of the Ethiopian Church until 1959\.23 In Ethiopian tradition, Frumentius is venerated as a saint and a national father figure, known by the honorific title Abba Salama, meaning "Father of Peace".23

This historical account is powerfully corroborated by archaeological and numismatic evidence. A clear chronological marker of this religious shift can be seen in the coinage minted during Ezana's reign. Early Aksumite coins feature pagan symbols, most notably the crescent and disc motif associated with South Arabian deities.18 However, coins issued in the latter part of his reign replace these symbols with the Christian cross, providing tangible proof of the king's change in faith.18 Similarly, monumental stone inscriptions erected by Ezana show a parallel evolution. Early inscriptions dedicate his military victories to traditional pagan gods like Mahrem (the Aksumite equivalent of the Greek war god Ares).17 A later, famous inscription celebrating a victory over the Noba people, however, is dedicated not to a pantheon of gods but to the "Lord of Heaven," the "Lord of all," and makes specific references to Christ in its Greek and South Arabian scripts.18

Ezana's conversion was a decision of profound political and cultural consequence. By adopting Christianity, Ezana aligned his kingdom with the Christian Roman Empire, the dominant power in the Mediterranean world. This forged crucial diplomatic and commercial links that were key to Aksum's prosperity and strengthened the king's own status as the earthly deputy of a single, all-powerful God.18

The existence of these two distinct conversion narratives—one apostolic, one royal—is not a contradiction but a source of strength for Ethiopian Christian identity. The story of the eunuch provides a timeless, biblical legitimacy, rooting the church in the sacred, foundational era of the apostles. It makes a powerful claim of spiritual authority that predates and stands independent of other Christian centers. The story of King Ezana, conversely, provides a specific, historical-political legitimacy. It documents the precise moment when Christianity became fused with state power, the monarchy, and the kingdom's geopolitical identity, making a claim of institutional and worldly authority. Together, they work in tandem. The eunuch's tale "Christianizes" Ethiopia's ancient past, while Ezana's conversion historicizes its Christian present and future. This duality provides an exceptionally robust foundation for its identity as both an ancient apostolic church and a divinely sanctioned Christian kingdom.

## **Part IV: The Tewahedo Faith \- A Unique Theological and Liturgical Identity**

Isolated for centuries from the main currents of both Western and Byzantine Christianity, the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church developed a theological and liturgical framework that is unique in Christendom. This distinctiveness is not a collection of disparate quirks but a coherent worldview built upon a foundation of continuity: the continuity of divine revelation through an expanded biblical canon, the continuity of the covenant through the retention of Old Testament practices, and the continuity of Christ's person in its Miaphysite theology.

### **4.1 An Expanded Revelation: The Orthodox Tewahedo Biblical Canon**

The most striking feature of the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church (EOTC) is its biblical canon, which is the largest and most diverse in all of Christianity.25 While most Protestant Bibles contain 66 books and the Catholic Bible contains 73, the EOTC's "broader canon" includes a total of 81 books, preserving a wealth of ancient texts that were lost or excluded by other traditions.25

This expanded canon is most notable for its inclusion of several works from the Second Temple period of Judaism (c. 515 BCE – 70 CE) that are considered apocryphal or pseudepigraphal elsewhere. Two of the most significant of these are the *Book of Enoch* and the *Book of Jubilees*.25

The *Book of Enoch* (or *1 Enoch*) is an ancient Jewish apocalyptic text, composed in stages between approximately 300 BCE and 100 BCE.12 It contains elaborate material on the origins of demons and the Nephilim, the fall of a group of angels, a justification for the Genesis flood, and prophecies concerning a thousand-year messianic reign.12 This book was widely read and highly influential in the period leading up to and during the time of Jesus and the apostles; it is even quoted directly in the New Testament in the Epistle of Jude (Jude 1:14–15).12 Despite its early importance, the

*Book of Enoch* was eventually excluded from the canonical scriptures of both Rabbinic Judaism and mainstream Christianity. For centuries, it was considered a lost work in the West, known only through fragments and references in other texts. It was only through its preservation in Ethiopia—where it is considered fully canonical and is preserved in its entirety in Ge'ez—that the complete text survived.12 Its canonicity is shared only with the Beta Israel Jewish community, highlighting the reception in Ethiopia of a stream of Second Temple Judaism that was extinguished elsewhere.27

Similarly, the *Book of Jubilees*, another Jewish work from the Second Temple period that retells the stories of Genesis and early Exodus from the perspective of an angel, is also considered canonical scripture within the EOTC.25 Other unique texts in the Ethiopian Old Testament include the

*Paralipomena of Jeremiah* (4 Baruch) and the three books of *Meqabyan* (Ethiopian Maccabees), which are distinct from the books of Maccabees found in the Catholic deuterocanon.25

This expansive approach to scripture suggests that the very concept of a fixed and rigidly "closed" canon may be understood differently in the Ethiopian tradition. Some scholars argue that the EOTC has a more fluid and inclusive understanding of sacred literature, one that transcends the idea of a strictly limited list of books.28

### **4.2 The Mosaic Heritage: Synthesis of Old Testament Practices**

A second defining characteristic of the EOTC is its profound and visible integration of Old Testament practices into its Christian life and worship. This "Hebraic" or "Judaic" character is so pronounced that it sets the church apart from all other Christian traditions.29 Many scholars see this synthesis not as a later development but as a direct consequence of Christianity being introduced into a culture already deeply influenced by a pre-existing Judaic population.9

The specific practices that reflect this Mosaic heritage are numerous and deeply embedded in the life of the church:

* **Sabbath Observance:** In a practice unique among major Christian denominations, the EOTC traditionally observes two holy days of rest: Saturday, the Old Testament Sabbath (*Sanbat*), and Sunday, the day of the Resurrection (*Ehud*).31  
* **Dietary Laws:** The church adheres to a set of dietary laws that are surprisingly similar to the *kashrut* of Judaism. The consumption of pork and other animals deemed "unclean" in the book of Leviticus is prohibited.32 In addition, animals must be slaughtered in a specific manner, often invoking the Trinity, a practice that shows clear links to Jewish ritual slaughter (  
  *shechitah*).32  
* **Circumcision:** The practice of male infant circumcision on the eighth day after birth, a cornerstone of the Abrahamic covenant in the Old Testament, is maintained as a cultural and religious tradition.29  
* **Church Architecture:** The traditional layout of an Ethiopian church, often circular or octagonal, is divided into three concentric spaces. The innermost sanctum, the *Qeddeste Qeddusan* ("Holy of Holies"), houses the *tabot* (a replica of the Ark of the Covenant) and can only be entered by priests. This tripartite structure is a direct architectural echo of the layout of the ancient Temple in Jerusalem.34  
* **Fasting:** The EOTC has one of the most rigorous fasting schedules in the Christian world. Devout adherents may fast for up to 250 days a year, with the laity observing around 180 days.31 These fasts typically involve abstaining from all animal products (meat, dairy, eggs) and refraining from food and drink until the afternoon, a discipline with deep roots in Old Testament asceticism.31

Importantly, the EOTC does not view these practices as a contradiction of the New Covenant or a regression to an obsolete law. Instead, its theology posits a continuous and unfolding revelation of God's will. The law is understood in three stages: first, the *Həgga Ləbbunä* ("law of the heart" or conscience), given to all humanity; second, the *Həgga Orit* ("law of Moses" or the Torah), given to Israel; and finally, the *Həgga Wangel* ("law of the Gospel").30 In this framework, the Gospel is not the abrogation of the Torah but its ultimate fulfillment and continuation. Faith in Christ establishes the law rather than abolishing it.30

### **4.3 One United Nature: The Miaphysite Christology of Tewahedo**

The theological core of the Ethiopian Church is encapsulated in its very name: the Ethiopian Orthodox *Tewahedo* Church. The Ge'ez word *Tewahedo* means "being made one" or "unified," and it refers directly to the church's defining Christological position.23 This doctrine is the reason for the EOTC's inclusion in the family of Oriental Orthodox Churches and its historical separation from the Catholic and Eastern Orthodox Churches.

The schism occurred in the 5th century, following the Council of Chalcedon in 451 CE. This council was convened to settle disputes over the nature of Christ. The definition it produced, which became the standard for the Byzantine and Roman churches, declared that Christ is one person existing in *two natures* (*physeis*)—one divine and one human. This position is known as Dyophysitism (from the Greek *dyo*, "two").36

The Church of Alexandria, and by extension its daughter church in Ethiopia, rejected the Chalcedonian formula. They feared that speaking of "two natures" after the Incarnation risked dividing Christ into two separate beings, a view associated with the heresy of Nestorianism.36 Instead, they held fast to a formula championed by the revered Alexandrian patriarch, St. Cyril:

*mia physis tou Theou Logou sesarkōmenē*, meaning "one (mia) incarnate nature of God the Word".34

This position is properly known as **Miaphysitism** (from the Greek *mia*, meaning "one" in a composite sense, and *physis*, "nature").36 It teaches that in the person of Jesus Christ, the divine nature and the human nature are perfectly and mysteriously united to form a single, composite, unified nature. This union is "without separation, without confusion, without alteration, and without mixing".34 The divinity is not diminished, and the humanity is not obliterated; they are brought together in a perfect, inseparable union. Thus, Christ acts as a single, unified being—not sometimes as God and sometimes as man, but always as the one God-Man.36

It is crucial to distinguish Miaphysitism from the heresy of **Monophysitism** (from the Greek *mono*, "single" or "simple"), a term often incorrectly and pejoratively applied to the Oriental Orthodox Churches.36 Monophysitism is properly associated with the 5th-century monk Eutyches, who taught that Christ's human nature was effectively absorbed and dissolved into his divine nature, like a drop of honey in the ocean. The EOTC and all other Oriental Orthodox Churches explicitly condemn this teaching as heretical, insisting that the properties of both humanity and divinity are preserved in the one united nature of Christ.36

The distinct theological pillars of the EOTC—its expanded canon, its retention of Old Testament practices, and its Miaphysite Christology—are not disconnected peculiarities. They form a deeply coherent theological system unified by a powerful, underlying theme: an embrace of continuity and an aversion to sharp ruptures. The expanded biblical canon represents a fuller and more continuous scriptural revelation, preserving the bridge between the Old and New Testaments. The retention of Mosaic Law represents a continuous covenantal practice, where the Gospel fulfills rather than replaces the Torah. Finally, the Miaphysite doctrine of *Tewahedo* represents a continuous and unbroken divine-human person in Christ. This overarching principle of continuity reinforces Ethiopia's unique self-conception as the faithful heir to the *entirety* of God's unfolding plan for salvation.

## **Part V: The Fortress of Faith \- Endurance in an Age of Isolation**

The survival of Ethiopian Christianity as an independent kingdom through the medieval period is a evidence of its remarkable resilience. Faced with the rise of Islam, which reordered the geopolitical map of the ancient world and isolated it from the wider Christian ecumene, the Aksumite kingdom and its successors did not passively wither. Instead, they executed a series of deliberate and dynamic survival strategies—a strategic "inward turn"—that transformed the geographic fortress of the Ethiopian highlands into a spiritual and cultural bastion, ensuring the preservation and flourishing of their unique Christian civilization.

### **5.1 Geopolitical Realities: The Rise of Islam and the Red Sea Shift**

From the 1st to the 7th centuries CE, the Kingdom of Aksum was a formidable power, a major hub in the trade networks connecting the Roman Empire with India and the East.40 Its port city of Adulis on the Red Sea was a bustling cosmopolitan center where goods like ivory, gold, and spices were exchanged for imports from across the known world.43 The kingdom minted its own currency, developed its own script (Ge'ez), and was considered by the 3rd-century Persian prophet Mani as one of the four great powers of the world, alongside Rome, Persia, and China.40

This prosperous, outward-looking era came to an end with the dramatic geopolitical shifts of the 7th century. The rapid expansion of Islam from the Arabian Peninsula fundamentally altered the balance of power. The Arab conquests of Egypt, the Levant, and North Africa severed the centuries-old ties between Christian Ethiopia and the Byzantine Mediterranean world.43 As Arab powers gained control of the Red Sea, Aksum's maritime trade network, the lifeblood of its economy, was choked off.45 The destruction of the vital port of Adulis by Islamic invaders around 710 CE was a catastrophic blow that signaled the end of Aksum's commercial dominance and the beginning of a long period of economic decline and increasing isolation.44

It is important to note that the initial encounter between Aksum and Islam was peaceful. When the early followers of the Prophet Muhammad faced persecution in Mecca in the early 7th century, they sought refuge in the Christian kingdom of Aksum. This event, known as the First Hijra (Migration to Abyssinia), saw the Aksumite king, or *Negus*, grant the Muslim refugees protection, an act of hospitality remembered fondly in Islamic tradition.46 However, this early goodwill could not halt the inexorable geopolitical tide. Over the subsequent centuries, the Christian kingdom found itself increasingly encircled by emerging Muslim sultanates, such as Ifat and Adal, along its eastern and southern frontiers, leading to centuries of rivalry and conflict.5

### **5.2 Strategies for Survival: Southward Expansion and Institutional Resilience**

Faced with the loss of its maritime empire and growing pressure from the north and east, the Ethiopian Christian state initiated a strategic retreat that was simultaneously a project of internal colonization and cultural consolidation. The political center of gravity shifted from the old coastal-facing capital of Aksum inward and southward, into the rugged, defensible highlands of the interior.43 This was not a simple decline but a deliberate reorientation, a turning away from a hostile external world to focus on building a new, more secure heartland. This "inward turn" became the master strategy for survival, a process that spurred immense cultural and religious creativity.

The Zagwe dynasty, an Agaw-speaking line of kings who came to power around 1137 CE, were the architects of this first phase of consolidation. Ruling from the safety of their native Lasta Mountains, the Zagwe ensured the continuity of the Christian state after the fragmentation of Aksumite power.5 They were devout Christians who expressed their faith through one of the wonders of the medieval world: the rock-hewn churches of their capital, Roha, which was later renamed Lalibela in honor of its greatest king.48 These magnificent churches, carved directly out of the living rock, were a monumental effort to construct a "New Jerusalem" on Ethiopian soil. For a kingdom physically cut off from the holy sites of the Levant, Lalibela became a new, accessible center of pilgrimage, a symbolic recreation of the sacred world within their own fortress.

The Solomonic dynasty, which succeeded the Zagwe in 1270, continued and intensified these survival strategies. Armed with the powerful legitimizing ideology of the *Kebra Nagast*—which symbolically transferred the covenant itself to Ethiopia, making it the "New Zion"—the Solomonid emperors launched aggressive military campaigns to consolidate and expand the Christian domain.5 Rulers like Amda Seyon I and Zara Yaqob waged holy wars against the rival Muslim sultanates on the periphery, securing control over vital inland trade routes and incorporating new territories into the Christian empire.5 They used a combination of warfare, sophisticated diplomacy (such as requiring the children of tributary chiefs to be raised at the royal court as hostages and courtiers), and the strategic awarding of land grants (

*gult*) to loyal soldiers and administrators to cement their control over this expanded highland empire.49

The institutional "backbone" of this entire survival strategy was Ethiopian monasticism.50 Christian monasticism, introduced to Ethiopia in the 5th and 6th centuries by missionary monks like the "Nine Saints," became the lifeblood of the church and culture during the long centuries of isolation.51 These saints, who came from various parts of the Byzantine Empire and were deeply influenced by the Egyptian monasticism of St. Pachomius, established monasteries that became the primary centers of Ethiopian civilization.53

These monastic communities served several critical functions:

* **Centers of Education and Literature:** In an age of isolation, monasteries were the sole institutions of higher learning. They preserved literacy in the classical Ge'ez language, meticulously copying and preserving not only the scriptures but also the theological treatises, hagiographies, and poetry that constituted the nation's literary heritage.50  
* **Pillars of the State:** Monasteries were deeply integrated into the political life of the kingdom. Monastic leaders were influential figures, with monks like Tekle Haymanot playing a decisive role in mediating the transfer of power from the Zagwe to the Solomonic dynasty.53 They were often the advance guard of state expansion, establishing Christian outposts in new territories.49  
* **Economic and Ecological Hubs:** Monasteries were self-sustaining economic engines, controlling vast tracts of agricultural land and employing local populations.51 They were also guardians of the natural environment, preserving the ancient "church forests" that surrounded their communities, which served as vital sanctuaries for biodiversity and were seen as sacred extensions of the church itself.55

In this way, the survival of Ethiopian Christianity was not a passive accident of geography. It was the result of a multifaceted and active response to a profound geopolitical crisis. By turning inward, the kingdom transformed itself. It replaced lost external connections with strengthened internal ones, creating a new sacred geography with Lalibela as its Jerusalem and the *Kebra Nagast* as its charter. The geographic fortress of the highlands became a spiritual and cultural fortress, defended by warrior-kings and sustained by a network of resilient monastic communities that preserved the soul of the nation.

## **Conclusion**

The history of the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church in its formative period is a narrative of exceptionalism, a story of a Christian identity forged in a crucible of unique historical and theological forces. As this report has demonstrated, the character of Ethiopian Christianity is not the result of a single event but the product of a dynamic, centuries-long synthesis of five foundational pillars.

First, its national consciousness was built upon the powerful mytho-historical framework of the *Kebra Nagast*. This epic did more than tell a story; it created a nation, legitimizing a royal dynasty by linking it to the biblical lineage of Solomon and David, and redefining the Ethiopian people as the new chosen of God, the inheritors of the Ark of the Covenant. This provided an unparalleled ideological resilience that fused church, state, and national identity into a single, durable whole.

Second, this identity was shaped by a deep and ancient Judaic heritage, embodied in the history of the Beta Israel. Whether as a source of influence on the nascent church or as a rival community, the Judaic presence in Ethiopia is critical to explaining the pronounced Hebraic character of Orthodox Tewahedo practice—from dietary laws and Sabbath observance to its very conception of the divine covenant.

Third, the church's origins were secured by a dual legitimizing narrative. The apostolic story of the Ethiopian Eunuch in Acts provided a timeless, biblical foundation, granting it an antiquity and authority independent of any other Christian see. This was complemented by the historical account of King Ezana's 4th-century conversion, which cemented the faith's inseparable bond with the monarchy and the state, providing it with institutional power and political protection.

Fourth, these historical developments gave rise to a singular theological worldview, *Tewahedo*. This vision is defined by a theme of continuity: a continuity of revelation, seen in its expanded biblical canon that preserves texts like *Enoch* and *Jubilees*; a continuity of practice, seen in its synthesis of Old Testament laws with the Gospel; and a continuity of personhood, seen in its Miaphysite Christology that insists on the one, perfectly unified nature of Christ.

Finally, this unique Christian civilization was preserved through active and sophisticated strategies of endurance. In the face of geopolitical isolation brought on by the rise of Islam, the kingdom did not collapse but executed a strategic "inward turn." It reoriented itself into the defensible highlands, with dynasties like the Zagwe and Solomonids consolidating a Christian empire through warfare, diplomacy, and the propagation of their foundational myths. This survival was made possible by the institutional backbone of monasticism, which served as the centers of education, literature, and economic life that sustained the faith through centuries of isolation.

In conclusion, Ethiopian Christianity is not an isolated relic of the past but a evidence of a dynamic and creative history. It stands as one of the world's most ancient, self-sufficient, and distinctive Christian traditions, a living monument to a faith that was not merely inherited but was actively and brilliantly forged on the anvil of African history.
