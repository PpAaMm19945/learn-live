# §9.1 The Twilight of Axum (c. 7th–9th Centuries AD)

In this section you will learn about the decline of the once-great Axumite Empire, how the rise of Islam shifted trade routes, and why Ethiopian Christianity began a strategic retreat into the highland interior.

Consider the vast Axumite Empire, a beacon of Christian civilization for centuries. Its merchants had sailed the Red Sea and the Indian Ocean, its kings had minted their own currency, and its architects had raised monumental stelae that pierced the heavens. Yet, by the 7th century AD (conventional estimate), this mighty kingdom was facing a profound and irreversible decline, not from a single, dramatic military defeat, but from a slow economic strangulation that would reshape its destiny.

The primary cause was a fundamental shift in global power. The rapid expansion of Islam out of the Arabian Peninsula and across North Africa and the Levant permanently altered the trade routes that had been the lifeblood of Axum. The Red Sea, once a bustling corridor connecting the Christian worlds of Byzantium and Ethiopia, was now controlled by rising Islamic powers. The vital port of **Adulis**, the gateway through which Axum's wealth flowed, was captured around 710 AD (conventional estimate), effectively severing the kingdom's connection to the Mediterranean economy and its lucrative trade with the Byzantine Empire and India.

> **Key Term:** **Axumite Empire** — A powerful trading kingdom centered in the northern Ethiopian highlands from c. 100 to 940 AD (conventional estimate). It was one of the first states in the world to officially adopt Christianity and was a major player in the commerce between the Roman Empire and ancient India.

The consequences were severe. Without access to its maritime trade network, Axum's economy withered. The minting of its distinctive coinage ceased, a clear sign of economic collapse. The construction of grand monuments halted. The once-thriving cosmopolitan centers began to fade. In response to this growing pressure and the settlement of Muslim communities along the eastern lowlands and coastal plains, the heart of Ethiopian Christianity began a strategic withdrawal. The faith retreated from the vulnerable coastal regions, moving southward and inward to the defensible mountain strongholds of **Lasta**, **Tigre**, **Shewa**, and **Amhara**—territories whose rugged terrain provided natural fortifications against invasion.

### The Highland Refuge: A Biblical Pattern

This retreat into the mountains was not merely a pragmatic military strategy; it echoed a pattern found throughout Scripture. When our Lord warned His disciples of Jerusalem's coming destruction, He commanded them to flee: **"Then let those who are in Judea flee to the mountains"** (Matthew 24:16). The Ethiopian Church, surrounded by the encroaching tide of Islamic expansion, followed this same divinely ordained pattern. The highlands became their refuge, their place of preservation in a time of great upheaval.

The Apostle John's vision in the book of Revelation carries an even more profound parallel: **"The woman fled into the wilderness, where she has a place prepared for her by God, in which she is to be nourished for 1,260 days... But the woman was given the two wings of the great eagle so that she might fly from the serpent into the wilderness, to the place where she is to be nourished for a time, and times, and half a time"** (Revelation 12:6, 14). Here we see the Church herself—portrayed as a woman—fleeing into the wilderness to be sustained and protected by divine providence. The Ethiopian Church's highland retreat was not an abandonment of mission or a sign of defeat, but rather a providential preservation. God prepared a place in the mountains where His people could be nourished and sustained until the appointed time.

### Monasteries as Arks: Preserving the Faith

The monasteries became the vessels of this preservation. **Debre Damo**, the ancient cliff-top monastery founded by Abba Aragawi of the Nine Saints a century earlier (see Chapter 7, Map 17), continued to stand as an impregnable fortress of the faith. Perched atop a flat-topped mountain accessible only by rope, it became a living symbol of the Church's withdrawal into inaccessible heights. **Lake Hayq**, with its **Istifanos monastery**, emerged as a vital center for the copying and preservation of sacred manuscripts. Monks labored in its scriptoriums, continuing the tradition begun by Abba Garima (whose Garima Gospels, the world's oldest illuminated Christian manuscripts, dated to c. 500-530 AD—see Chapter 7, Map 17). These highland monasteries became repositories not only of sacred texts but of the Ge'ez language itself, the liturgical tongue that bound the Ethiopian Church to its apostolic heritage.

The author of Hebrews describes the faithful of old as those who **"wandered about in deserts and mountains, and in dens and caves of the earth"** (Hebrews 11:37-38). These words, written to honor the persecuted saints of Israel, found a new fulfillment in the monks and priests of Ethiopia during this "time of concealment." They carried their sacred relics, precious manuscripts, and the very memory of their covenant into caves and mountain retreats, preserving a flame that the world sought to extinguish.

> **Key Term:** **Adulis** — The main port of the Axumite Kingdom, located on the Red Sea coast of modern-day Eritrea. Its capture by Arab forces in the early 8th century (conventional estimate) crippled the empire's trade and marked a key turning point in its decline.

This period is remembered in Ethiopian tradition as a "time of concealment." It was an era where the custodians of the faith—monks, priests, and devout rulers—preserved their unique identity not through worldly power, but through faithful stewardship of Scripture and tradition. The great monastic centers of the highlands—**Debre Damo** in Tigre, the **Lake Hayq monastery** in the eastern highlands, and emerging centers in **Lasta** and **Amhara**—became beacons of learning and piety. Here, the Nine Saints' legacy endured: their Syrian-influenced monastic rules, their commitment to biblical translation, and their vision of Ethiopia as a land where God would be worshiped in spirit and in truth.

This was not an admission of defeat, but a strategy for survival—and more than survival, a divine plan for preservation. By turning inward, the Ethiopian Church prepared for a new chapter in its history, isolated but not extinguished, concealed but not forgotten.

> **Continuity with Map 17 (Chapter 7):** The highland monastic centers that became Ethiopia's refuge—Debre Damo, Lake Hayq, and the Tigray monasteries—were the same foundations established by the Nine Saints (c. 480-520 AD). The Garima Gospels, transcribed by Abba Garima in the 6th century, were preserved and copied in these scriptoriums during the centuries of Islamic encirclement. The "wilderness retreat" of the 7th-9th centuries was not a new strategy, but a continuation of the monastic vision planted by the Syrian refugees who fled Chalcedonian persecution. What began as a spiritual movement became, providentially, a geographic and political strategy for the preservation of Ethiopian Christianity.

### Theological Reflection

The fading of Axum offers a profound lesson on the nature of God's kingdom. Earthly empires, with their coins, monuments, and trade routes, are subject to the tides of history. The loss of Adulis, the silencing of the royal mint, the crumbling of the stelae—all these demonstrated that worldly glory is fleeting. But the Kingdom of God is not built on economic or political power.

During the "time of concealment," the Ethiopian Church demonstrated that the true wealth of faith is not found in coastal ports but in the hearts of its people. It is preserved in the scriptures diligently copied by monks in mountain caves—continuing the work of Abba Garima and his spiritual descendants—and in the traditions passed down through generations. The highlands became Ethiopia's wilderness, and there God nourished His Church, just as He promised.

This period teaches us a vital truth: when the world closes doors, faith finds a new heartland. The Ethiopian Church's retreat to the mountains was not a failure of mission but a fulfillment of biblical prophecy. It proves that the Church is most resilient when it relies not on its own strength—not on international trade or political alliances—but on God's enduring presence. The monasteries, those ark-like refuges in the storm, preserved not just manuscripts and liturgies, but the living faith of a people who believed that **"Ethiopia shall stretch out her hands unto God"** (Psalm 68:31).

The lesson for us is clear: earthly empires rise and fall, but the Word of the Lord endures forever. When God's people are driven into the wilderness, it is not abandonment—it is preparation. The highlands were Ethiopia's appointed place, prepared by God, where His Church would be nourished and protected until the day of her re-emergence.
