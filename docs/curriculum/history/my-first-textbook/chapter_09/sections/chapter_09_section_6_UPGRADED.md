# §9.6 The Coming Storm (c. 15th–Early 16th Centuries)

In this section you will learn about the rising power of the Sultanate of Adal, the foreshadowing of a new era of conflict, and how Ethiopia stood on the precipice of a dramatic re-engagement with the wider world as the medieval period drew to a close.

The reforms of Zara Yaqob had brought the Ethiopian Church and state to a pinnacle of internal unity and intellectual confidence. The kingdom had successfully weathered centuries of isolation, defended its borders, and forged a unique and resilient Christian identity. Yet, as the 15th century drew to a close, the geopolitical forces that had long been held at bay were gathering strength, preparing to unleash a new and more perilous era of conflict.

### The Sultanate of Adal and the Threat of Ahmad Gran

The primary threat came from the **Sultanate of Adal** on the kingdom's eastern frontier. While Amda Seyon had decisively defeated its predecessor, the Ifat Sultanate, a century earlier (c. 1332), Adal had regrouped and was growing in power, fueled by a revitalized military, control of lucrative trade routes, and a fervent commitment to jihad. By the early 16th century, Adal would produce one of the most formidable military leaders in the history of the Horn of Africa: **Ahmad ibn Ibrahim al-Ghazi** (c. 1506–1543), known to the Ethiopians as **"Gragn"** (meaning "the Left-Handed" in Amharic).

Ahmad Gran's campaigns, which would unfold in the decades after this chapter's time frame (covered in a future chapter), would bring the Christian kingdom to the very brink of annihilation. His forces, supported by Ottoman firearms and artillery, would devastate the Ethiopian highlands, destroy churches and monasteries, and force the Christian population into desperate resistance. The storm that was gathering in the late 15th century would break with full fury in the 1530s and 1540s.

> **Key Term:** **Sultanate of Adal** — A powerful Muslim state located in the Horn of Africa that flourished from approximately 1415 to 1577 (conventional estimate). It was the most formidable rival of the Christian Ethiopian Empire and, under the leadership of Ahmad Gran, came close to conquering it in the 16th century.

> **Key Term:** **Ahmad ibn Ibrahim al-Ghazi ("Ahmad Gran")** — (c. 1506–1543) A Muslim military leader of the Sultanate of Adal whose campaigns against Christian Ethiopia (1529–1543) nearly destroyed the kingdom. His nickname "Gragn" means "the Left-Handed" in Amharic. His invasions would mark the end of Ethiopia's medieval isolation and force the kingdom to seek foreign allies.

### The Portuguese Arrival: A New Power on the Horizon

At the same time, a new power was appearing on the horizon. Portuguese explorers, driven by a desire to control the lucrative spice trade and to find the legendary Christian kingdom of Prester John, had successfully rounded the Cape of Good Hope and entered the Indian Ocean. In the **1490s**, Portuguese envoys such as **Pêro da Covilhã** began to make contact with the Ethiopian court, marking the first direct European engagement with the highland kingdom in over a millennium.

Their arrival would permanently alter the political and religious dynamics of the region. They were not seeking a mythical king for a crusade, but a real ally in a global strategic contest that now involved the Portuguese Empire, the Ottoman Empire, and the regional powers of the Horn of Africa. The old bipolar world of Christian Ethiopia versus Muslim sultanates was about to become a multipolar struggle involving European colonial ambitions and Ottoman expansion.

The medieval era of Ethiopia's history was thus drawing to a dramatic close. The long period of isolation—the "time of concealment" that had begun with the loss of Adulis in the 8th century—was about to be shattered. The mountain fortress, which had stood alone for so long, would soon become a theater of a much larger conflict, one that would test the foundations of the hidden kingdom as never before.

### Providence and Preparation

> **Debate Box — The Portuguese Arrival: Salvation or Complication?**
> The arrival of the Portuguese is often seen as the dramatic rescue of Christian Ethiopia from the brink of destruction at the hands of Ahmad Gran. But was it an unmitigated blessing? One perspective is that the Portuguese were instruments of Providence, a long-hoped-for connection to the Christian world that arrived in a moment of dire need, saving the world's second-oldest Christian nation from extinction. The opposing view argues that the Portuguese arrival, while militarily decisive, introduced a new and damaging set of complications. Their aggressive Catholic missionary zeal created deep divisions within the Ethiopian Orthodox Church, and their involvement entangled Ethiopia in the broader, destructive politics of European colonial ambition.

Yet we must not view these developments as mere accidents of history. Our Lord warned His disciples: **"And you will hear of wars and rumors of wars. See that you are not alarmed, for this must take place, but the end is not yet. For nation will rise against nation, and kingdom against kingdom"** (Matthew 24:6-8). The gathering storm of the early 16th century—the rise of Adal, the arrival of the Portuguese, the expansion of the Ottomans—was part of the turbulent movements of history that God ordains for His purposes.

The Psalmist declares: **"God is our refuge and strength, a very present help in trouble. Therefore we will not fear though the earth gives way, though the mountains be moved into the heart of the sea, though its waters roar and foam, though the mountains tremble at its swelling"** (Psalm 46:1-3). Ethiopia stood on the brink of a geopolitical earthquake, but the God who had preserved His Church in the wilderness for seven centuries would not abandon her in the storm.

In retrospect, we can see that the centuries of isolation had been a time of preparation. The monastic movements of Tekle Haymanot and Ewostatewos, the theological debates over the Sabbath, the construction of Lalibela, the Ge'ez manuscripts copied in highland scriptoriums—all of these had forged a resilient Christian identity that would endure the trials to come. God had nourished His Church in the wilderness (Revelation 12:6), and now He would test her in the fire.

### Theological Reflection

The final years of this era serve as a powerful reminder that the story of God's people is never static. Periods of quiet consolidation and internal reflection are often followed by dramatic, world-altering challenges. The looming conflict with Adal and the unexpected arrival of the Portuguese demonstrate that faith is not a fortress meant for permanent isolation. It is a ship built to weather storms.

The Ethiopian Church, having spent centuries cultivating its unique identity in the highlands, was now being called to bear witness to its faith on a new and more complex global stage. This transition teaches us that God prepares His people in seasons of quiet for the seasons of trial and engagement that will inevitably follow.

The prophet Isaiah declares: **"No weapon that is fashioned against you shall succeed, and you shall refute every tongue that rises against you in judgment. This is the heritage of the servants of the LORD and their vindication from me"** (Isaiah 54:17). Ahmad Gran's armies would come with Ottoman firearms, Portuguese soldiers would bring Catholic theology, and the old order would crumble. But the promise of God endures: He who began a good work in Ethiopia—stretching back to the Ethiopian eunuch of Acts 8, the Nine Saints of the 6th century, and the rock-hewn faith of Lalibela—would carry it to completion (Philippians 1:6).

The coming storm was real, but so was the God who commanded the storm. Ethiopia's medieval isolation was ending, but her covenant with the God of her fathers was not.
