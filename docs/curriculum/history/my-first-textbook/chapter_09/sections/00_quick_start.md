### Quick Start
• **Creation** — God’s creative purpose for a unified humanity is expressed in the rich diversity of peoples and nations, including the ancient civilizations of the Horn of Africa.
• **Fall** — The consequences of the Fall are seen in the fracturing of kingdoms, the rise of political and religious conflict, and the isolation of communities from one another.
• **Redemption** — The Church, as the steward of Scripture and sacred tradition, is called to preserve the faith and bear witness to Christ’s redemptive power, even in the midst of profound geopolitical isolation.

**Chapter Summary (≤100 words):** This chapter explores the history of Christian Ethiopia from c. 900–1500 AD. It traces the decline of the Axumite kingdom and the rise of the Zagwe Dynasty, who built the remarkable rock-hewn churches of Lalibela. It then examines the Solomonic Restoration, which legitimized its rule through the epic *Kebra Nagast*. Finally, it details the kingdom's resilience under military pressure, its first contacts with Europe, and the looming threats that would mark the end of its medieval isolation, all while developing a unique and deeply rooted Christian identity.

**Key Terms:**
* **Zagwe Dynasty:** A line of Agaw kings who ruled Ethiopia from c. 900–1270, famed for building the Lalibela churches.
* **Lalibela:** A Zagwe king and the site of eleven monolithic churches carved from living rock, intended as a "New Jerusalem."
* **Yekuno Amlak:** The Amhara noble who overthrew the Zagwe in 1270, restoring the Solomonic line of kings.
* **Abuna:** The head of the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church, traditionally a Coptic bishop appointed by the Patriarch of Alexandria.
* **Prester John:** A legendary Christian king of the East, whose myth fueled European interest in finding the isolated Ethiopian kingdom.
* **Meskal:** A major Ethiopian Christian festival celebrating the finding of the True Cross by the Empress Helena.

**Scripture Thread:** The Psalms provide a language of worship and lament for a people in isolation (Psalm 68:31), while Isaiah's prophecies of a renewed Zion resonate with the Ethiopian conception of their kingdom as a holy nation (Isaiah 60).
