# §9.5 Ethiopia and the Wider Christian World (c. 14th–15th Centuries)

In this section you will learn about the European legend of Prester John and its connection to Ethiopia, the first tentative diplomatic contacts between the highland kingdom and Europe, and the remarkable reign of Emperor Zara Yaqob, a theologian-king who reformed the church and consolidated the faith.

Despite its profound isolation, Ethiopia was not entirely forgotten by the rest of Christendom. In a Europe locked in a centuries-long struggle with Islamic powers, a fascinating legend took root—the story of **Prester John**. This legend told of a mighty Christian king ruling a lost kingdom in the East, a potential ally who could help turn the tide of the crusades. For centuries, Europeans searched for this mythical figure, with many eventually identifying his kingdom with the remote Christian empire of Ethiopia.

> **SIDEBAR: Prester John — Legend vs. Diplomatic Reality**
> The Prester John legend was one of the most powerful and persistent myths of the European Middle Ages. Initially located in Asia, the legend shifted its focus to Africa in the 14th century. It was a projection of European hopes, a desire for a Christian ally on the other side of the Islamic world. While the historical kings of Ethiopia were not the mythical Prester John, the legend had real-world consequences. It spurred European explorers, particularly the Portuguese, to seek out the mysterious African kingdom, eventually leading to direct contact. The legend, therefore, served as a bridge of imagination that eventually led to a bridge of diplomacy, connecting two long-separated branches of the Christian family. ### First Contacts: Jerusalem and Europe

These legends slowly gave way to reality. During the 15th century, the first tentative diplomatic exchanges began to occur. Ethiopian monks established a presence in Jerusalem, where they interacted with their European counterparts, and a few Ethiopian envoys even reached the courts of Europe, including Rome. The most significant of these contacts occurred during the reign of Emperor **Zara Yaqob** (r. 1434–1468 AD), one of Ethiopia's most intellectually and spiritually formidable rulers. He sent a delegation to the **Council of Florence in 1441**, a clear sign of his desire to re-engage with the wider Christian world.

The Council of Florence was convened to heal the schism between the Eastern (Orthodox) and Western (Catholic) churches. While the Ethiopian delegation did not commit to union with Rome, their presence sparked important theological discussions. The Ethiopians held firm to their Miaphysite Christology (inherited from the Coptic Church) and rejected Roman claims of papal supremacy and the *filioque* clause (the Western addition to the Nicene Creed stating that the Holy Spirit proceeds from "the Father and the Son"). This encounter revealed both the shared foundation and the real doctrinal differences between Ethiopian Orthodox and Western Catholic Christianity.

> **Key Term:** **Prester John** — A legendary Christian patriarch, priest, and king who was believed to rule over a lost Christian nation in the East during the Middle Ages. The legend was a popular motif in European chronicles and romance, and was eventually associated with the Christian kingdom of Ethiopia.

### Zara Yaqob: Theologian and Emperor

Zara Yaqob was more than a diplomat; he was a theologian-emperor. He wrote numerous religious treatises, including the ***Mashafa Berhan*** ("Book of Light") and the ***Mashafa Milad*** ("Book of the Nativity"), fiercely debated doctrinal issues, and worked tirelessly to unify and reform the Ethiopian Church. He enforced orthodoxy, settled the long-running dispute over Sabbath observance (ruling in favor of honoring both Saturday and Sunday), and promoted grand public religious festivals, most notably **Meskal**, the Feast of the Finding of the True Cross.

Zara Yaqob was also a capable military leader. In **1445 AD**, he achieved a decisive victory at the **Battle of Gomit** against Muslim forces, demonstrating that his intellectual prowess was matched by strategic brilliance. His reign was the intellectual and cultural apex of the Solomonic Dynasty, a period in which the kingdom, though still isolated, developed a rich and systematic expression of its unique faith.

> **Exegetical Note:** The celebration of the True Cross is rooted in post-biblical church tradition, particularly the story of Empress Helena's pilgrimage to Jerusalem in the 4th century. For Ethiopian Christianity, the festival of Meskal is not just a historical commemoration; it is a profound theological statement about the victory of the cross over the forces of darkness and a central symbol of their national and religious identity.

> **Key Term:** **Zara Yaqob** — A powerful Ethiopian emperor who reigned from 1434 to 1468 AD (conventional estimate). He was a devout Christian, a writer of theological treatises (including the *Mashafa Berhan*), and a major reformer of the church who consolidated its doctrines, settled the Sabbath controversy, achieved military victories (Battle of Gomit, 1445), and promoted important religious festivals like Meskal.

### The Universal and the Local

The story of Ethiopia's re-engagement with the wider Christian world during the 15th century reminds us of a profound biblical truth. Jesus prayed for His disciples: **"That they may all be one, just as you, Father, are in me, and I in you, that they also may be in us, so that the world may believe that you have sent me"** (John 17:21). For centuries, Ethiopian and European Christians were almost completely unaware of each other's existence, separated by geography, politics, and the rise of Islam. Yet both were part of the one body of Christ.

The Apostle Paul writes: **"There is one body and one Spirit—just as you were called to the one hope that belongs to your call—one Lord, one faith, one baptism, one God and Father of all, who is over all and through all and in all"** (Ephesians 4:4-6). The Ethiopian and European churches, though divided by distance and doctrine, shared this fundamental unity: one Lord, one baptism, one Spirit. The Prester John legend, for all its mythical qualities, testified to this underlying hope—the belief that Christians were not alone, that somewhere beyond the horizon, brothers and sisters in the faith endured.

Yet this unity did not erase distinctiveness. Zara Yaqob's reforms demonstrate how faith takes deep root in a specific culture, developing its own unique traditions, debates, and celebrations. The Sabbath controversy, the Meskal festival, the Ge'ez liturgy—these were distinctively Ethiopian expressions of a universal faith.

### Theological Reflection

The story of Prester John and the reign of Zara Yaqob speak to the universal nature and the particular expression of the Christian faith. For centuries, Ethiopian and European Christians were almost completely unaware of each other, yet both were part of the one body of Christ. The legend of Prester John was a evidence of this underlying unity, a hope that they were not alone. Zara Yaqob's reforms, meanwhile, demonstrate how faith takes deep root in a specific culture, developing its own unique traditions, debates, and celebrations.

This dynamic teaches us that the Church is both universal and local. We share one Lord, one faith, one baptism with believers across the globe and throughout the ages, yet we are called to live out that faith in the beautiful particularity of our own time and place. Ethiopian Christianity, with its Ge'ez liturgy, its Sabbath observance, and its rock-hewn churches, is a powerful reminder that orthodoxy (right belief) can be expressed through diverse orthopraxy (right practice).

The re-connection of Ethiopia with the wider Christian world—first through legend, then through tentative diplomacy—was a foretaste of the global church that would emerge in later centuries. It reminds us that God's kingdom transcends national and cultural boundaries, that the Spirit works in ways we cannot see, and that the prayer of Jesus for unity is being answered, even when we are unaware of it.
