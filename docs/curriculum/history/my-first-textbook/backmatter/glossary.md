# Glossary

**Aksum** — Ancient kingdom in the Ethiopian highlands (c. 100–940 AD), centered in modern-day Tigray region. One of the four great powers of the ancient world.

**Aksumite Script** — Ancient South Arabian-derived script used in the Kingdom of Aksum, ancestor to the modern Ge'ez script.

**Amharic** — Modern Semitic language of Ethiopia, descended from Ge'ez, official language of Ethiopia.

**Ark of the Covenant** — The sacred chest containing the tablets of the Ten Commandments; Ethiopian tradition claims it resides in Aksum.

**Bantu Expansion** — The gradual migration of Bantu-speaking peoples from West-Central Africa across sub-Saharan Africa, beginning c. 300 BC (YEC dating) or c. 1500 BC (conventional dating).

**Berber** — Indigenous peoples of North Africa, descendants of the biblical Phut.

**Cataracts** — Series of six major rapids on the Nile River between Aswan and Khartoum, serving as natural boundaries.

**Coptic** — The final stage of the ancient Egyptian language, written in a modified Greek alphabet, used by Egyptian Christians. Also refers to the Coptic Orthodox Church.

**Cush** — Biblical name for the region south of Egypt (Genesis 10:6), encompassing ancient Nubia, Kush, and Ethiopia.

**Donatism** — Fourth-century schism in the North African church over the readmission of those who had lapsed during persecution.

**Ezana** — King of Aksum (c. 320–360 AD) who converted to Christianity and made it the state religion.

**Frumentius** — Syrian missionary who brought Christianity to Aksum; consecrated first Bishop of Aksum by Athanasius of Alexandria (c. 340 AD).

**Garamantes** — Ancient Berber civilization in the Sahara Desert (modern Libya), known for sophisticated irrigation systems.

**Ge'ez** — Ancient Semitic language of Ethiopia, liturgical language of the Ethiopian Orthodox Church. Also called Ethiopic.

**Hamite** — Biblical descendant of Ham, son of Noah (Genesis 10). Traditional classification of African peoples.

**Horn of Africa** — Peninsula in Northeast Africa comprising modern Ethiopia, Eritrea, Djibouti, and Somalia.

**Kebra Nagast** — "Glory of Kings," Ethiopian national epic recounting the visit of the Queen of Sheba to Solomon and the Solomonic dynasty.

**Kerma** — Ancient Nubian civilization and city (c. 2500–1500 BC conventional), precursor to the Kingdom of Kush.

**Kush** — Ancient Nubian kingdom centered at Napata and later Meroë, ruling c. 1070 BC – 350 AD.

**Lalibela** — Medieval Ethiopian king (r. c. 1181–1221 AD) and the rock-hewn churches named after him.

**Meroë** — Capital city of the later Kingdom of Kush (c. 300 BC – 350 AD), south of the Nile's fifth cataract.

**Meroitic** — Writing system of the Kingdom of Kush, still largely undeciphered. Derived from Egyptian hieroglyphics.

**Miaphysite** — Christological position of the Oriental Orthodox churches (including Coptic and Ethiopian), holding that Christ has one united nature.

**Monophysite** — Term often (incorrectly) applied to Miaphysite churches; technically refers to the heretical view that Christ had only a divine nature.

**Napata** — Capital of the Kingdom of Kush during the 25th Dynasty of Egypt and early Kushite period.

**Nine Saints** — Group of Syrian missionaries who established monasticism in Ethiopia (c. 480–500 AD).

**Nok Culture** — Ancient civilization in central Nigeria (c. 1500 BC – 500 AD), known for distinctive terracotta sculptures.

**Nubia** — Region along the Nile between Aswan and Khartoum, encompassing ancient Kush and medieval Christian kingdoms.

**Periplus** — *Periplus of the Erythraean Sea*, first-century Greek navigational guide describing Red Sea and Indian Ocean trade.

**Phut** — Biblical son of Ham (Genesis 10:6), ancestor of the Libyan peoples of North Africa.

**Proto-Bantu** — Reconstructed ancestral language of the Bantu language family.

**Solomonic Dynasty** — Ethiopian royal dynasty claiming descent from Solomon and the Queen of Sheba, restored 1270 AD.

**Stele** — Upright stone slab used as monument or marker; Aksum famous for its massive stone stelae.

**Taharqa** — Kushite pharaoh of Egypt's 25th Dynasty (r. 690–664 BC), mentioned in the Bible (2 Kings 19:9, Isaiah 37:9).

**Tassili n'Ajjer** — Mountain range in the Sahara Desert (Algeria) with extensive prehistoric rock art.

**Tigrinya** — Modern Semitic language of Eritrea and Tigray, closely related to Ge'ez.

**Urewe** — Early Iron Age ceramic tradition associated with early Bantu-speaking peoples in the Great Lakes region.

**Vandals** — Germanic tribe that conquered Roman North Africa (429–534 AD), persecuting Nicene Christians.

**YEC** — Young Earth Creationism; the biblical worldview that the earth is approximately 6,000–10,000 years old, based on biblical genealogies and chronology.

**Zagwe Dynasty** — Ethiopian dynasty (c. 1137–1270 AD) centered at Roha (Lalibela), known for rock-hewn churches.
