## 3.1 After Babel: The Migrations into a Prepared Land (c. 2200–1800 BC, YEC)

Just as the story of Egypt began with Mizraim’s descendants finding a home along the Nile, the story of the vast lands to the west begins with his brother. The great dispersal from Babel, described in Genesis 11, was not merely an act of judgment. It was the sovereign hand of God scattering humanity to fulfill His original command to "fill the earth." While Mizraim’s family traveled southwest, another branch of Ham's descendants, the sons of **Phut** (Genesis 10:6), moved directly west.

They did not enter a barren wasteland. In an act of profound providence, God had prepared the land for them. In the centuries after the Flood, during the post-Flood Ice Age, North Africa’s climate was dramatically different. The region we now know as the Sahara was a vast, well-watered savanna, dotted with lakes and rivers. This "Green Sahara" was a hospitable environment, ready to sustain new populations. It was into this prepared landscape that the descendants of Phut migrated, carrying with them the skills and knowledge of post-Babel civilization.

Archaeological evidence, when interpreted through a biblical lens, affirms this account. What secular archaeologists call the "Capsian Culture" (conventionally dated to 10,000 BC) is better understood as the material footprint of these first post-Babel settlers (c. 2200–1500 BC, YEC). The famous Saharan rock art, depicting herds of cattle and thriving communities, is not a relic from a mythical pre-history, but a vibrant snapshot of the world of Phut's immediate descendants.

From these initial settlements, two broad groups emerged, as detailed in **Map 4: North Africa Regional Overview**. In the east, along the Libyan coast and desert oases, were the peoples the Egyptians would come to know as the **Tehenu** and the **Meshwesh**. In the west, in the rugged Atlas Mountains, the **Berber** (or Amazigh) peoples established their homeland, developing a fierce independence that would characterize their history for millennia. Their story, like all histories, is a evidence of God's purpose, which extends to all nations He scattered from the plains of Shinar.
