## 3.4 The Rise of Carthage: The Last Stand of Canaan (c. 814–146 BC)

Around 814 BC, a new power arrived on the shores of North Africa. Fleeing the growing power of Assyria in the east, Phoenician traders from the city of Tyre founded a colony they called *Qart-Hadasht*, the "New City." We know it as Carthage.

It is critical to understand who these people were. The Phoenicians were the **Canaanites** of the Old Testament (descendants of Ham's son, Canaan, as listed in Genesis 10:15). Even the great church father Augustine, writing over a thousand years later, noted that the Punic-speaking peasants of his day still called themselves *Chanani*. The prophecies of Isaiah 23 against Tyre spoke of its merchants fleeing across the sea; Carthage was the fulfillment of that prophecy.

This Canaanite city grew into a vast maritime empire, controlling the trade of the Western Mediterranean for centuries (see **Map 11: Carthaginian Maritime Empire**). Their mighty navy, the "Ships of Tarshish" mentioned in Scripture, controlled the Straits of Gibraltar and monopolized the rich silver and tin mines of Spain (the biblical **Tarshish**). But along with their wealth and power, they brought the dark heart of their ancestral religion.

> **Archaeological Window: The Tophet of Carthage**
> The chief gods of Carthage were Baal Hammon and his consort Tanit. At a sacred precinct called the Tophet, they practiced the very abomination for which God had commanded the Israelites to drive the Canaanites from the Promised Land: child sacrifice. Archaeologists have unearthed thousands of urns containing the cremated remains of infants, dedicated to Baal and Tanit. This was not Roman propaganda; it was the horrifying reality of Canaanite religion, a practice condemned in the strongest possible terms by the prophet Jeremiah: "They have built the high places of Baal, to burn their sons in the fire as burnt offerings to Baal, which I did not command or speak, nor did it come into My mind" (Jeremiah 19:5).

Carthage's history was defined by its long and brutal struggle with the rising power of Rome—the three Punic Wars. But from a biblical perspective, this was more than a geopolitical conflict. The Punic Wars were the final, decisive confrontation between the legacy of Canaan and the rising Gentile power God would use to prepare the world for the Gospel. When the Roman general Scipio Aemilianus finally destroyed Carthage in 146 BC, razing the city to the ground, it was the end of a 1,500-year story. It was the final, terrible judgment of God on the persistent, idolatrous, and cruel religion of Canaan.
