# Chapter 3: Answer Keys

## Data Task Answer Keys

### Section 1: Data Task: Analyzing Rock Art

1.  **Primary Activities:** The paintings suggest a mixed economy. Painting A (giraffe hunt) points to hunting. Painting B (tending cattle) points to pastoralism (raising livestock).
2.  **Value of Livestock:** The sheer number of cattle depicted and the care shown by the human figures suggest that livestock was a primary measure of wealth, sustenance, and cultural importance.
3.  **Social/Religious Development:** The single, elaborately decorated figure in Painting C, shown in a position of reverence by others, suggests the beginnings of social stratification (a priestly or chiefly class) and organized religious rituals.

### Section 2: Data Task: Names and Identity

1.  **Difference in Perspective:** "Berber" is an exonym (a name given by outsiders) and carries a negative connotation of being "other" or "uncivilized." "Amazigh" is an endonym (a name used by the people themselves) and carries a positive connotation of freedom and nobility. One is a label of contempt; the other is a statement of identity.
2.  **Importance of Self-Naming:** Self-naming is a powerful act of self-determination and preserves cultural dignity. Accepting a name from an outsider, especially a derogatory one, can be a form of cultural submission.
3.  **Other Examples:** Answers will vary. Examples could include Sámi (vs. Lapps), Roma (vs. Gypsies), or Inuit (vs. Eskimo).

### Section 3: Data Task: Biblical Timeline Correlation

1.  **Biblical Events (examples):**
    *   God's covenant with Abram (Genesis 15): c. 1912 BC (biblical chronology)
    *   Birth of Isaac: c. 1896 BC (biblical chronology)
    *   Jacob and Esau born: c. 1836 BC (biblical chronology)
2.  **Meaning for Understanding History:** It reveals that biblical history did not happen in a vacuum. While God was dealing specifically with the patriarchs, other complex civilizations with their own remarkable achievements were flourishing elsewhere, all within God's sovereign purview.
3.  **How it Helps:** It moves other nations from being "background noise" to being active players in a global history that God is overseeing. It helps us take seriously the Bible's claim that God is the Lord of all nations, not just Israel.

### Section 4: Data Task: Cross-Referencing Historical Records

1.  **Historical Significance:** The alignment of the two records provides powerful external corroboration for the historical reliability of the biblical text. It shows that the Bible is recording real-world history, not myth.
2.  **Portrayal:** Sheshonq I would have portrayed the event as a glorious military victory, emphasizing his power and the submission of foreign lands to his might. Public inscriptions are propaganda, designed to glorify the ruler. The biblical narrative is a theological history, designed to show God's judgment on sin.
3.  **God's Operation in the World:** It teaches that God is not limited to working through believers. He can use the ambitions, greed, and political goals of any ruler or nation to bring about His own purposes, including the discipline and preservation of His own people.

### Section 5: Data Task: Analyzing Foundational Myths

1.  **Initial Power Dynamic:** The story suggests that the Phoenicians were not in a position to take the land by force. They were newcomers who had to respect the authority of the local Amazigh rulers who controlled the territory. The power, at least initially, was with the indigenous population.
2.  **Preserving the Story:** Carthage was a commercial empire, not a land-based military one like Rome. A story emphasizing cleverness, negotiation, and business acumen aligns better with their cultural values than a story of brutal conquest. It celebrates their identity as savvy merchants.
3.  **Contrast with Rome:** The Romulus and Remus myth is rooted in violence, fratricide, and military strength from its inception. The Carthaginian myth is rooted in intelligence and negotiation. The myths reflect the core character of the two rival civilizations.

### Section 6: Data Task: Evaluating a General's Legacy

1.  **"Heroic Patriot" Arguments:** (a) He was defending his homeland from the existential threat of Rome, a relentlessly expanding power. (b) His daring invasion of Italy was a brilliant strategic move designed to take the fight to the enemy and relieve pressure on Carthage.
2.  **"Ruthless Conqueror" Arguments:** (a) His ambition led to decades of war and the deaths of hundreds of thousands across the Mediterranean. (b) He was the aggressor in the Second Punic War, choosing to invade Italy rather than seek a peaceful solution.
3.  **Biblical Principles:** Answers will vary. Students should wrestle with the tension. While leadership and protecting one's people are valued, the immense pride, ambition, and staggering loss of life involved in Hannibal's campaign would be condemned by biblical principles that value peace, humility, and the sanctity of every human life made in God's image.

### Section 7: Data Task: The Shifting Alliances of Power Politics

1.  **Key Event:** The destruction of Carthage in 146 BC. Once Numidia's role as a strategic buffer against Carthage was no longer needed, Rome began to see a strong, independent Numidia as a potential future threat.
2.  **"National Interest":** Based on Rome's actions, "national interest" is the principle that a state will make and break alliances based entirely on what benefits its own security and power, without regard for loyalty or past friendships.
3.  **Application:** Numidia was Rome's "friend" only as long as they shared a common enemy (Carthage). Once that enemy was gone, the basis for the friendship disappeared, and the political relationship changed entirely.

### Section 8: Data Task: Geography and Culture

1.  **Coastal vs. Interior:** Coastal regions are hubs for trade, travel, and naval power, making them the natural points of contact and control for a maritime empire like Rome. Mountainous interiors are harder to access, conquer, and control, allowing indigenous cultures to persist with less interference.
2.  **Differing Perspectives:** A Romanized merchant would likely value the stability, trade networks, and legal system Rome provided. An Amazigh chieftain would likely resent Roman taxes, the loss of political autonomy, and the seizure of tribal lands for Roman colonists.
3.  **Modern Parallels:** Answers will vary. Students might point to divides between urban, globally-connected coastal cities and more traditional, rural interior regions in many countries around the world.

### Section 9: Data Task: Synthesizing Historical Threads

1.  **Coexistence of Forces:** A culture can absorb new ideas, technologies, and even people (fusion) while still holding onto its core identity, language, and social structures (resilience). The Amazigh people adopted Punic farming techniques and Roman urbanism but largely retained their own language and identity in the interior.
2.  **Which is More Important?:** Answers will vary. Students should discuss the dangers of stagnation (no fusion) versus the dangers of assimilation (no resilience). A healthy society likely needs a balance of both.
3.  **Christianity as a Model:** The core truths of the Gospel (resilience) are unchanging across all time and cultures. However, the expression of that faith in music, art, language, and church structure is incredibly diverse and adaptable to every culture it enters (fusion).

## Review Question Answer Key

1.  The Garamantes were an Amazigh people who built a powerful kingdom in the Fezzan region of the Sahara. Their key innovation was the *foggara* (or *qanat*), an underground irrigation system that channeled water from aquifers to their settlements, allowing for intensive agriculture in the desert.
2.  The Phoenicians arrived primarily as traders seeking commercial relationships. They established coastal trading posts and integrated with the local Amazigh population, creating a new Punic culture. The Romans arrived as conquerors. Their primary goals were to eliminate a rival (Carthage), secure resources (especially grain), and impose their own legal and political structures.
3.  Sheshonq I was a pharaoh of Libyan (Amazigh) descent who founded Egypt's 22nd Dynasty. He connects the story of the descendants of Phut (by being Libyan) and the story of Egypt (by becoming its pharaoh). He is almost certainly the "Shishak" mentioned in the Bible who invaded Judah and plundered the Temple, thus connecting his story directly to the biblical narrative of Israel.
4.  The destruction of Carthage removed Numidia's primary regional rival, allowing King Masinissa, with Roman support, to consolidate a large, prosperous kingdom (its "golden age"). However, with Carthage gone, Rome no longer needed a strong Numidian ally as a buffer. Rome then began to view Numidia as a potential threat, leading to political interventions that eventually ended the kingdom's independence.
5.  Answers will vary but should touch on the theme of God's universal sovereignty. Israel's story shows God's *special* redemptive plan, while Phut's story shows His *general* providence over all nations. The story of Phut demonstrates that God raises up and puts down empires (like Carthage and Rome) according to His will, often using their political movements to shape the world in which His redemptive plan unfolds (e.g., using the Libyan Shishak to judge Israel, or using the Roman Empire to prepare the way for the Gospel). Together, they show that God is in control of all history, not just "church history."
