# Band 2 Constraint Templates (Ages 6-9)
## Strand 1: Number & Quantity

*Note: The constraint prompt is shown to the parent. The parent can use it to lead the task, or capture it for Async AI, or invoke Live AI.*

---

### D1: Place Value as Grouping
**Prerequisite:** C8 (Composition/Decomposition)

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Physical manipulation |
| **Materials** | 30-50 small objects (beans, sticks), rubber bands or small cups |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Give your child {N} objects. Ask them: 'How can we count these quickly without losing track?' Let them try. If they don't group by 10s, suggest: 'What if we put them in groups of 10?'" |
| **Success** | Child groups objects into 10s and counts the leftover ones. |
| **Failure** | Child insists on counting by 1s and loses track, or cannot form a group of 10. |
| **Reasoning check** | "Why is grouping by 10 faster than counting by 1?" |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Physical to symbolic translation |
| **Materials** | Bundles of 10, single objects, paper |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x5):** Give child a number (e.g., 42). "Build this with your 10-bundles and 1s, then draw it."<br>**Endurance (x2):** "Build 35, but you only have two 10-bundles. What do you do?" (Forces unbundling: 2 tens, 15 ones). |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Tell your child to build the number {number}. Watch if they use the bundles correctly." |
| **Success** | Correctly represents the number with tens and ones. Handles the endurance 'unbundling' task. |
| **Reasoning check** | "If I have 2 bundles of 10 and 15 loose beans, what number is that? How do you know?" |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Error detection |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Show your child this drawing: 3 bundles of 10 and 12 loose sticks. Say: 'Your friend wrote that this is the number 3012. Is that right? What should it be?'" |
| **Success** | Identifies that 10 loose sticks make a new bundle, so it's 4 bundles and 2 sticks = 42. |
| **Failure** | Agrees with 3012, treating tens and ones as separate numbers rather than a combined quantity. |
| **Reasoning check** | "Can you ever have 12 single ones in the 'ones' place? Why not?" |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | System design |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask your child: 'If we were aliens with only 4 fingers on each hand, how would we bundle our sticks? Show me what the number 23 would look like for them.'" |
| **Success** | Child bundles by 8s (or 4s). Realizes that '10' is just a convention, not a magic number. |

### D2: Addition as Combining
**Prerequisite:** C8 (Composition/Decomposition)

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Physical storytelling |
| **Materials** | Two disparate groups of items (e.g., 7 rocks, 5 leaves) |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Present {Group_A} and {Group_B}. Ask: 'If we put all of these into one big pile, how many things do we have total? Don't count from 1. Can you start from the bigger group?'" |
| **Success** | Starts with 7 and counts on: "8, 9, 10, 11, 12". Understands addition is joining. |
| **Reasoning check** | "Does it matter if we start with the rocks or the leaves? Why?" |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Mental / Written execution |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x5):** Standard addition problems bridging 10 (e.g., 8+6).<br>**Endurance (x3):** "Add 24 and 37 mentally, then explain your steps." |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask your child to solve {Eq}. Watch how they do it. Do they use their fingers? Do they group tens?" |
| **Success** | Correct sum, ideally using a strategy like "make a 10" (8+6 -> 8+2+4 -> 10+4 = 14) rather than counting by ones. |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Strategy comparison |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'I need to add 48 + 35. Person A says: add 40+30, then 8+5. Person B says: take 2 from 35, give it to 48 to make 50, then add the left over 33. Which way is better? Why?'" |
| **Success** | Child recognizes BOTH are correct. Can explain how Person B's "compensation" strategy works. |
| **Reasoning check** | "Is one way always better, or does it depend on the numbers?" |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Word problem creation |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Tell your child: 'Write a story problem that results in the equation {Eq}. It cannot be about buying things or eating food.'" |
| **Success** | Creates a logically sound word problem requiring addition. |

### D3: Subtraction as Separating (and Comparing)
**Prerequisite:** D2 (Addition as Combining)

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Physical manipulation |
| **Materials** | Number line (drawn on floor) or physical blocks |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask: 'You have {Start}. You need to get to {Target}. How many steps to get there?' (This introduces subtraction as the difference/distance, not just taking away)." |
| **Success** | Understands subtraction can mean "how much more do I need?" and finds the difference. |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Written/Mental execution |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x5):** Problems involving taking away and finding difference (e.g., 15-8, 52-29).<br>**Endurance (x3):** "Solve 40 - 15 without crossing out or borrowing. How can you think about it as addition?" |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask your child to solve {Eq}. Can they solve it by counting up from the smaller number?" |
| **Success** | Uses strategies like "counting up" (15 + ? = 40) or "compensation" instead of relying solely on the standard algorithm. |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Error detection |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Show your child: 52 - 28. Someone wrote the answer is 36. Ask: 'What mistake did they make?'" |
| **Success** | Identifies that the person subtracted the smaller digit from the larger digit in both columns (5-2=3, 8-2=6) instead of regrouping. |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Teaching / Reversibility |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask your child: 'Can you prove to me that your subtraction answer is right using addition? Teach me how they are opposites.'" |
| **Success** | Explains that if A - B = C, then C + B must equal A, demonstrating structural understanding of inverse operations. |

### D4: Multiplication as Equal Groups
**Prerequisite:** D2 (Addition)

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Visual array |
| **Materials** | Grid paper or a tray of organized objects (e.g., an egg carton) |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Show an array of {Rows} rows and {Cols} columns. Ask: 'How can we count these fast without counting by 1s?'" |
| **Success** | Recognizes they can skip count by rows or columns. |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Model translation |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x5):** "Draw a model that shows 4 x 6."<br>**Endurance (x3):** Area model context: "Draw a rectangle that is 4 units wide and 6 units long. How many squares inside?" |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask your child to draw a picture for {Eq}. Watch to ensure they draw equal groups or an array, not just random dots." |
| **Success** | Accurately translates the expression into equal groups or an area model. |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Structural property analysis |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask: 'If you know 4 x 6 = 24, do you also know 6 x 4? Why? Can you prove it with your drawing?'" |
| **Success** | Rotates the array drawing 90 degrees to prove commutativity. |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Distributive property design |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask: 'We don't know what 7 x 8 is. But we know 5 x 8 and 2 x 8. Can we use those to find 7 x 8? Show me with a drawing.'" |
| **Success** | Breaks a 7x8 array into a 5x8 and a 2x8 array, demonstrating the distributive property conceptually. |

### D5: Division as Equal Partitioning
**Prerequisite:** D4 (Multiplication as Equal Groups)

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Physical manipulation |
| **Materials** | Countable objects (beans, blocks), cups |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Your child needs to divide {N} beans equally into {G} cups. Ask them to explain why the groups are equal." |
| **Success** | Each cup has exactly {result} items AND child uses words like 'same', 'equal', 'fair'. |
| **Failure** | Groups are unequal OR learner cannot explain after 2 prompts. |
| **Reasoning check** | "Are they equal? How do you know?" |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Physical manipulation with recording |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x5):** Give {N} objects and {G} groups. "Draw what you did." Includes cases with remainders.<br>**Endurance (x2):** "Divide 15 beans into 4 groups. What happens with the extra? Is it fair to give it to one group?" |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask your child to divide 13 objects into 4 groups. Ask: 'What about the extra one? Is that fair?'" |
| **Success** | Correct partitioning in 5/5 cases, including articulating remainder as "leftover". |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Error detection + comparison |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Set up a wrong partition (e.g., 15 objects in 4 groups: 4, 4, 4, 3). Ask the child to find the mistake and fix it. Also ask: 'Someone says 12 ÷ 3 = 5. Without counting, can you tell if that's right or wrong? How?'" |
| **Success** | Identifies error, explains reasoning, uses inverse relationship ("3 x 5 = 15, not 12"). |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Design + Teaching |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'Create a sharing problem for your sibling that has a remainder. Make sure it's fair. Explain your problem and why the answer must be what it is.'" |
| **Success** | Creates a valid division problem, predicts the answer, explains the structure. |

### D6: Number Line as Model
**Prerequisite:** C6 (Comparison)

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Physical movement |
| **Materials** | Tape on floor or drawn chalk line |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Draw a line with marks 0 to 10. Ask your child to stand on 3. Then say: 'Walk to 7. How many steps did you take?'" |
| **Success** | Child counts the *steps* (the spaces between numbers), not the tick marks. |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Abstract representation |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x5):** Draw an open number line (no ticks). "Put 50 in the middle. Where does 48 go? Where does 60 go?"<br>**Endurance (x2):** "Put 10 and 20 on the line. Now put 100. Is your spacing right?" |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask your child to place {Num1} and {Num2} on a blank line. Check if the distance makes sense." |
| **Success** | Numbers are in the correct order, and the proportional distance is roughly accurate. |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Error detection |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Show a number line where the distance from 0 to 10 is the same size as the distance from 10 to 12. Ask: 'What is wrong with this picture?'" |
| **Success** | Recognizes the scale is broken (10 steps shouldn't take the same space as 2 steps). |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | System creation |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask: 'Design a number line that only shows multiples of 5 (0, 5, 10...). Use it to show someone how to solve 15 + 10.'" |
| **Success** | Creates a consistent scale stepping by 5s and models an operation on it. |

### D7: Properties of Operations
**Prerequisite:** D2 (Addition)

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Observation |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Show 3 rocks and 4 leaves. Count them (7). Now swap them: show 4 leaves and 3 rocks. Count them. Ask: 'Did the total change? Does the order matter?'" |
| **Success** | Recognizes order doesn't change the sum (Commutative property). |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Relational thinking |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x5):** "Solve 2 + 7+ 8. Which two numbers should you add first to be fast?" (Looks for 2+8=10).<br>**Endurance (x2):** "If 58 + 42 = 100, what is 42 + 58? Don't add it!" |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Give your child a math problem with 3 numbers where 2 make a 10. See if they group the easy numbers first." |
| **Success** | Uses properties (associative/commutative) to make math easier rather than just working left to right. |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Boundary testing |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask: 'You know 5 + 3 is the same as 3 + 5. Is 5 - 3 the same as 3 - 5? Let's check with blocks.'" |
| **Success** | Child discovers that subtraction is NOT commutative. (Crucial realization). |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Generalization |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask: 'Can you write a rule about adding? Something that is ALWAYS true for any numbers you ever find?'" |
| **Success** | States a rule like "You can always switch the numbers when adding and get the same answer." |

### D8: Equivalence
**Prerequisite:** D2 (Addition) / C9 (Pattern Recognition)

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Physical balance |
| **Materials** | A hanger balance or visual balance scale |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Put 4 identical blocks on the left, 4 on the right. It balances. Add 1 block to the left. Ask: 'What do I have to do to the right to make it balance again?'" |
| **Success** | Understands that whatever happens to one side must happen to the other. |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Symbolic balance |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x5):** Balance equations: "5 + 4 = 6 + ___"<br>**Endurance (x3):** "___ + 2 = 5 + 3. The blank is first!" |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Write {Eq} with a blank not at the end. Remind them: '=' means BALANCE, not 'write the answer here'." |
| **Success** | Finds the missing number without simply adding all visible numbers together (a common error). |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Error detection |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Show this: 8 + 4 = 12 + 2. Ask: 'Your friend wrote this. What did they think the '=' sign meant? How would you explain they are wrong?'" |
| **Success** | Understands the friend thought '=' meant 'compute 8+4', ignoring the '+2' completely. Fixes it. |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Design |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask: 'Create the longest balancing equation you can using addition and subtraction. Like 5 + 5 = 12 - 2.'" |
| **Success** | Creates a true, multi-term equation demonstrating deep understanding of equivalence. |

### D9: Estimation & Reasonableness
**Prerequisite:** D2 (Addition)

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Magnitude guessing |
| **Materials** | A clear jar with items (e.g., beans) |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Show the jar. Ask: 'Are there about 10, about 100, or about 1000 in here? Why do you think that?'" |
| **Success** | Picks the right order of magnitude without counting. |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Rounding basics |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x5):** "Is 48 closer to 40 or 50?"<br>**Endurance (x2):** "If you want to buy two toys that cost 18 and 21, will 30 be enough? Just guess by looking!" |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Give a problem where an exact answer isn't needed, just a 'yes' or 'no' based on estimation." |
| **Success** | Uses estimation appropriately instead of insisting on exact calculation. |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Checking calculations |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'Someone added 42 + 59 and got 81. Before adding it yourself, how do you know their answer MUST be wrong?'" |
| **Success** | "Because 40 + 50 is 90, so the answer has to be bigger than 90." |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Problem generation |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask: 'Tell me a story where someone SHOULD estimate instead of counting exactly. Why is it better to estimate there?'" |
| **Success** | Identifies real-world situations (e.g., crowds, distant trees) where estimation is preferred or necessary. |
| **Reasoning check** | "Can you tell me a rule for when estimation is good enough and when you NEED the exact answer?" |

## Formal Milestone Tasks — Strand 1 (All Capacities)

*Per Part VIe of the Spine: every milestone is a real-world situation with no mathematical vocabulary signalling the operation. The child must recognise the mathematical structure themselves.*

---

### D1 Milestone: Place Value
> **Situation:** "Your grandmother asks you to count the chickens on the farm. There are many. She needs to know how many there are so she can tell the vet. How would you count them so you don't lose track and don't have to start over?"
>
> **Success:** Child proposes grouping by 10s or another systematic method. Reasoning: explains why grouping prevents double-counting. Failure: counts by 1s or proposes random methods.

### D2 Milestone: Addition
> **Situation:** "Your family is travelling. In the morning, you drove past 28 road signs. After lunch, you drove past 34 more. Grandmother wants to know the total so she can tell a story about it. Figure it out."
>
> **Success:** Child combines the quantities without being told to "add." Uses a strategy (make-a-ten, decomposition) and explains why the total must be more than 50.

### D3 Milestone: Subtraction
> **Situation:** "There were 43 chapatis on the plate this morning. Now there are 17. Your aunt wants to know what happened. Help her figure it out."
>
> **Success:** Child recognises this is a comparison/difference situation. Finds the gap (26). Reasoning: explains that the missing chapatis are the difference between what was there and what's left.

### D4 Milestone: Multiplication
> **Situation:** "We are planting a garden. We have 6 rows, and each row has space for 8 seedlings. Tendo wants to buy the seedlings at the market. How many should he buy?"
>
> **Success:** Child identifies the array/equal groups structure. Calculates 48. Does not need to be told "multiply."

### D5 Milestone: Division
> **Situation:** "Nala brought 30 groundnuts to share with her 5 friends (not including herself). She wants everyone to have the same amount. What should she do?"
>
> **Success:** Child partitions 30 into 6 equal groups (Nala + 5 friends). Each gets 5. Reasoning: explains why 6 groups, not 5.

### D6 Milestone: Number Line
> **Situation:** "The school is at the 15-kilometre marker on the road. The market is at the 42-kilometre marker. Your uncle wants to know how far it is from school to the market."
>
> **Success:** Child finds the distance (27 km) by subtraction or counting up. Reasoning: explains this is a "how far between" problem, not "what is 15 plus 42."

### D7 Milestone: Properties of Operations
> **Situation:** "You need to count the total legs on 3 tables and 7 chairs. Tables have 4 legs each and chairs have 4 legs each. Amara says you should count the table legs first, then the chair legs. Tendo says just count all the furniture and multiply by 4. Who gets the answer faster? Do they get the same answer?"
>
> **Success:** Child recognises the distributive property/commutativity in action. Both methods work. Explains why.

### D8 Milestone: Equivalence
> **Situation:** "Your mother gave your brother 6 sweets in the morning and 4 at lunch. She gave you some sweets at lunch only. You both have the same total. How many did she give you?"
>
> **Success:** Child sets up the balance (6 + 4 = ?). Finds 10. Key reasoning: the child is not looking for '= means write the answer' — they are solving for balance.

### D9 Milestone: Estimation
> **Situation:** "Aunt Prossy says there are about 200 people at the wedding, but Uncle Joseph says there are 500. You look at the crowd. The chairs have 10 rows of 10. Some people are standing. Who do you think is closer to right? Why?"
>
> **Success:** Child uses the anchor (100 chairs × occupancy) to estimate. Recognises that 200 is more plausible than 500, given the visible reference point. Does NOT try to count every head.

## Retroactive Reasoning Check Addendum

| D2 Own | Own | "Read your story problem back to me. How do you know someone HAS to add to solve it?" |
