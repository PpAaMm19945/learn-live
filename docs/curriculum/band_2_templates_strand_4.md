# Band 2 Constraint Templates (Ages 6-9)
## Strand 4: Data, Probability & Statistics

*Note: The constraint prompt is shown to the parent. The parent can use it to lead the task, or capture it for Async AI, or invoke Live AI.*

---

### P2a: Data Collection
**Prerequisite:** P1b (Counting & Tallying)

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Field observation |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'We need to know what color car is most popular on our street. Let's sit outside for 5 minutes. How can we keep track without writing the word 'white' or 'black' every time?'" |
| **Success** | Child invents or uses a simple recording system, like making a mark for each car. |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Designing a survey |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x4):** "Survey 10 people (family/friends/toys) on their favorite fruit out of 3 choices. Ask them. Record properly."<br>**Endurance (x2):** "Survey people on their favorite food, but don't give them choices. Try to organize the answers." (Shows the messiness of open-ended data). |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask your child to collect data from at least 5 people about {Topic}. Watch to ensure they record each answer exactly once." |
| **Success** | Methodically collects and organizes data (e.g., using tally marks) without double-counting or skipping. |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Identifying bias / Poor methodology |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'I wanted to know if kids like broccoli. I asked your brother right after I promised him ice cream if he said yes. Then I asked your sister when she was crying. Is my data good? Why not?'" |
| **Success** | Recognizes that *how* and *when* you collect data changes the outcome. Starts understanding fairness in data. |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Categorization design |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'Look at all the books on this shelf. I want to collect data on them. Give me three different ways we could sort them to get data (e.g., by color, by size...)'" |
| **Success** | Identifies multiple distinct attributes for the same population, showing an understanding that "data" depends on the question asked. |

### P2b: Bar Graphs & Tally Charts
**Prerequisite:** P2a (Data Collection)

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Physical graphing |
| **Materials** | Sticky notes or identical blocks |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask your child to make a tower of red blocks for every red car we saw, and a blue tower for the blue cars. Stand them side-by-side. 'Which one is taller? What does that mean?'" |
| **Success** | Connects the physical height of the tower to the quantity of items. |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Chart creation and reading |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x5):** Give raw data. "Draw a bar graph. Remember the title and labels."<br>**Endurance (x3):** Show a graph where the scale goes by 2s instead of 1s. Ask a question about a bar that lands halfway between numbers. |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Give your child this table of data: {Data}. Ask them to turn it into a bar graph. Do they label the bottom and the side? Are the bars the same width?" |
| **Success** | Accurately translates a table into a graph with appropriate labels, categories, and scale. |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Missing information detection |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Show a bar graph with bars but NO numbers on the side and NO title. Ask: 'What does this graph tell us? Could it be about elephants? Could it be about ants? Why is this a bad graph?'" |
| **Success** | Identifies that without a labeled Y-axis and title, the visual representation is meaningless. |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Purposeful display |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'You have data showing that 5 kids like summer and 4 like winter. Draw the data in a way that makes it look like SUMMER won by a HUGE amount.'" |
| **Success** | Manipulates the baseline (starting the Y-axis at 3 instead of 0) or stretches the scale, demonstrating early understanding of how data can be drawn to persuade. |

### P2c: Asking Good Questions
**Prerequisite:** P1c (Simple Comparison)

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Question sorting |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask: 'Which is a better question for a survey: 'Do you like pizza?' OR 'What is your favorite food?' Why?'" |
| **Success** | Recognizes the difference between a yes/no question and an open-category question in terms of the data it produces. |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Aligning question to goal |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x4):** "We need to know what games to buy for the party. Write 2 questions we should ask the guests."<br>**Endurance (x2):** "You asked: 'Do you like cake or pie better?' Everyone said cake. But at the party, nobody ate the cake. What question *should* you have asked?" (e.g., 'Are you allergic to cake?'). |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Give a scenario: {Scenario}. Ask them to write down the exact question they would ask people to solve this." |
| **Success** | Writes questions that directly address the informational need without being overly broad or confusing. |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Evaluating answerability |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'I want to know who the nicest person in the world is. Is that a good question for a math graph? Why not? How could we change it so we CAN graph it?'" |
| **Success** | Identifies subjective vs objective criteria. Suggests measurable changes (e.g., "Who helps the most people?"). |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Reverse engineering |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Show a bar graph about pet ownership. Ask: 'What was the exact question the person asked to get this chart?'" |
| **Success** | deduces the survey question strictly from the graph structure and labels. |

### P2d: Mode
**Prerequisite:** P2b (Bar Graphs)

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Visual identification |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Show a lineup of shoes in the house. 'Which size do we have the MOST of? That's called the Mode.'" |
| **Success** | Identifies the most frequent category visually. |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Finding Mode from lists |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x5):** Give lists of numbers/items (e.g., 2, 4, 4, 5, 4, 7). "Find the mode."<br>**Endurance (x3):** List with NO mode (1, 2, 3, 4). List with bimodal data (1, 1, 2, 2, 3). "What happens now?" |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Present this data set: {DataSet}. Ask your child to find the mode. See if they organize the data first." |
| **Success** | Correctly identifies the most frequent value, handling edge cases (no mode, two modes). |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Usefulness of Mode |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'A shoe store sold one Size 14 shoe, and fifty Size 8 shoes. Is it more helpful for the manager to know the Mode (size 8) or the largest size sold? Why?'" |
| **Success** | Connects the statistical measure to real-world utility (stocking inventory based on what is most popular). |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Constructing data to fit a parameter |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'Write a list of 7 numbers where the mode is 5, but 5 is not the biggest number on the list.'" |
| **Success** | Generates a valid data set meeting the constraints (e.g., 1, 5, 5, 5, 8, 9, 10). |

### P2e: Likely vs Unlikely
**Prerequisite:** P1e (Certain vs Impossible)

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Vocabulary placement |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Draw a line. Put 'Impossible' on one side, 'Certain' on the other. Ask: 'Where should we put 'If I drop this cup, it will break'? Where should we put 'It will snow tomorrow'?'" |
| **Success** | Places events intuitively along the continuum of probability. |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Analyzing simple setups |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x5):** Show spinners or bags of marbles with unequal colors. "Is it likely or unlikely to get red?"<br>**Endurance (x2):** "If a bag has 50 red and 49 blue, is red 'certain' or just 'likely'? Why?" |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Show an image (or describe) a bag with {Color1} and {Color2} items. Ask your child to use the words 'likely', 'unlikely', or 'equally likely' to describe pulling a {Color1} item." |
| **Success** | Uses the correct terminology based on the proportion of the parts, differentiating 'likely' from 'certain'. |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Flawed reasoning |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'I bought a lottery ticket. There are two options: I win, or I lose. Since there are two options, does that mean it's equally likely? Do I have a 50/50 chance? Why not?'" |
| **Success** | Understands that "two outcomes" does not automatically mean "equally likely outcomes." |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Designing probability |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'Design a spinner for a game where Player A is LIKELY to win, but Player B is NOT IMPOSSIBLE to win.'" |
| **Success** | Designs a spinner with unequal sectors (e.g., 90% Player A, 10% Player B). |

### P2f: Simple Experiments
**Prerequisite:** P2b (Bar Graphs), P2e (Likely vs Unlikely)

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Prediction vs Reality |
| **Materials** | A coin |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask: 'If I flip this coin 10 times, what do you predict will happen?' Then flip it 10 times and record. 'Did it match your prediction exactly?'" |
| **Success** | Realizes that real-world experiments don't always perfectly match the theoretical expectation in the short term. |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Large sample testing |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x3):** Roll a die 20 times, record results on a tally chart.<br>**Endurance (x1):** Roll a die *50* times. Does the chart look flatter (more equal) than when you rolled it 10 times? |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask your child to run an experiment: {Experiment}. Make sure they record EVERY result accurately without 'cheating' to make it look even." |
| **Success** | Conducts a fair experiment and organizes the results. |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Gambler's fallacy (introductory) |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'I flipped a coin 4 times, and got HEADS every time. What is more likely for the next flip: Heads or Tails? Or are they equal? Why?'" |
| **Success** | Recognizes that the coin has no memory; the chances are still equal. |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Checking fairness |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'Your friend brought a weird, bumpy die. How could we design an experiment to test if it's fair or if it's rigged?'" |
| **Success** | Proposes rolling the die many times and analyzing the graphed results to look for unequal distribution. |

### P2g: Comparing Data Sets
**Prerequisite:** P2b, P2f

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Visual comparison |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Show two bar graphs side-by-side. Graph A (Dogs vs Cats in Class 1). Graph B (Dogs vs Cats in Class 2). Ask: 'Which class likes cats more? How can you tell?'" |
| **Success** | Compares bars across two different graphs visually. |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Feature analysis |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x4):** Give two sets of data (e.g., highs temps for last week vs this week). "Which week was hotter overall? Which week had the highest single day?"<br>**Endurance (x2):** Give two sets where Set A has a higher total, but Set B has the highest mode. |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Show your child Data Set A and Data Set B. Ask them to write down 3 things that are different between the two sets." |
| **Success** | Accurately identifies differences in maximum, minimum, and mode across two data sets. |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Unfair comparisons |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'Graph A says 10 people like blue. Graph B says 20 people like red. So red is twice as popular! BUT Graph A only asked 10 people total, and Graph B asked 100 people. Is the claim fair?'" |
| **Success** | Recognizes that comparing raw numbers between different sized populations is misleading (early intro to fractions/percentages). |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Data narrative |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'Here is data for Group 1. Here is data for Group 2. Write a short news headline that summarizes the biggest difference between them.'" |
| **Success** | Synthesizes raw data comparing two groups into a clear, accurate, high-level summary. |
| **Reasoning check** | "Why did you choose to talk about THAT difference? Why is it the most important thing to know?" |

## Formal Milestone Tasks — Strand 4 (All Capacities)

*Per Part VIe of the Spine: every milestone is a real-world situation with no mathematical vocabulary signalling the operation.*

---

### P2a Milestone: Data Collection
> **Situation:** "We need to buy new shirts for the 10 players on the football team. We only want to buy two colours. How do we figure out which two colours most people will like, without just guessing?"
>
> **Success:** Child proposes asking the players and keeping track of the answers. Reasoning: explains how they will make sure they don't count anyone twice.

### P2b Milestone: Bar Graphs
> **Situation:** "You counted the animals in the village. 12 goats, 5 cows, 18 chickens. Your grandfather wants to see the numbers, but he cannot read words or numbers well. Make a picture that shows him exactly what you found."
>
> **Success:** Child constructs a bar graph (or physical equivalent like blocks) scaled correctly. Reasoning: explains how the height/length of the bars tells the grandfather the story without numbers.

### P2c Milestone: Asking Good Questions
> **Situation:** "The headteacher wants to know if the children are happy with the school lunches. She wrote this question for the survey: 'Is the posho too dry today?' What is wrong with asking it this way if she wants to know about ALL the lunches?"
>
> **Success:** Child identifies the question is too specific (only about today, only about posho). Suggests a broader alternative.

### P2d Milestone: Mode
> **Situation:** "A woman sells shoes in the market. She can only afford to buy one size of shoe from the supplier this week. She looks at her book: last week she sold sizes 6, 8, 8, 7, 8, 9, 8. Which size should she buy more of? Why?"
>
> **Success:** Child identifies 8 as the most common (the mode). Reasoning: connects the most frequent number to real-world business demand, rather than picking the biggest number (9).

### P2e Milestone: Likely vs Unlikely
> **Situation:** "It is the dry season. A man looks at the clear blue sky and says: 'I am certain it will rain today, so I will carry my big umbrella.' What advice do you give him?"
>
> **Success:** Child advises him that rain is 'unlikely' or 'impossible' (depending on exact local context), not 'certain'. Reasoning: justifies the probability based on evidence (dry season, clear sky).

### P2f Milestone: Simple Experiments
> **Situation:** "Two brothers are arguing. Tendo says he is faster. Alex says he is faster. They raced once, and Tendo won. Alex says: 'I slipped on a stone! It wasn't fair!' How do they settle who is *actually* faster?"
>
> **Success:** Child proposes racing multiple times (running an experiment with a larger sample size) to see who wins the most, filtering out random luck.

### P2g Milestone: Comparing Data Sets
> **Situation:** "Farmer A got 5 sacks of maize from his field this year. Last year he got 20. Farmer B got 10 sacks this year. Last year he got 8. Who is having a worse year? Why?"
>
> **Success:** Child identifies Farmer A is having a worse year, despite the numbers looking mixed. Reasoning: compares the *change* over time rather than just current raw totals.
