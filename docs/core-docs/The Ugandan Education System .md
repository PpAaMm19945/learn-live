# **The "Theoretical Adult": Why Our Schools Are Failing to Produce Fruit**

## 

According to the National Planning Authority 9 out of 10 of Ugandan graduates are not "job-ready." It's now the order of the day for degree holders to ride boda bodas not out of an entrepreneurial spirit, but out of desperation. We are producing **"theoretical adults"**, loaded with definitions but stripped of the skills needed to build families and economies.

I wish it was just a problem of employment and skill, but it runs deeper. Immorality and laziness are at an all-time high. The same people looking for work lack the wisdom and character to keep it. Our leaders have failed to champion the nation out of its economic and developmental crisis, largely due to greed.

Jesus gave us a simple, universal metric for evaluating the health of anything—whether a person, a movement, or an institution:

​"*A good tree cannot bear bad fruit, nor can a bad tree bear good fruit.*" (Matthew 7:18)

​While Jesus was speaking of false prophets, the principle holds true for any system: you judge the machine by what it produces. For decades, Ugandan parents have planted their children in the soil of the modern school system, watering it with exorbitant fees and immense sacrifice. We were promised a harvest of disciplined, capable, and employable adults. Instead, we are harvesting dust.

## **The Abandoned Definition**

Whenever an analysis is conducted on education, we usually circle the usual suspects: outdated curricula, theoretical teaching, underpaid staff, and lack of resources. While true, these are merely surface wounds. We must ask the fundamental question: **What is education in the first place?**

Historically, "education" was not the same as "schooling." To educate was to raise a child—shaping habits, character, and faith for future responsibility in the family, the church, and the world. To be educated was not merely to be crowned with the "D's", Diplomas, Degrees, and PhDs.

Over time, we narrowed the definition. Education became classroom instruction, exams, and certificates. Schooling replaced education, but nothing replaced the work of moral and spiritual formation.

**Noah Webster’s 1828 dictionary captures the original intent:**  
*“The bringing up, as of a child... intended to enlighten the understanding, correct the temper, and form the manners and habits of youth, and fit them for usefulness in their future stations.”*

This definition refuses the notion that preparing a child for the future is simply giving them head knowledge. Webster includes instruction, but refuses to separate it from upbringing and character development. Education moves a person from childhood to responsible adulthood. It addresses the temper, the behaviors, and the disposition of the child. It is a comprehensive training for independent adulthood.

## **The Myth of Neutrality**

Why has a shift been widely accepted from focusing on *all* aspects of Education to just a few? Robert L. Dabney argued that education can never be neutral. To teach values, discipline, and judgment is to take a stand on what is right, good, and true.

Moral formation always rests on religious foundations, whether they are acknowledged or not. When a system claims to educate while refusing to name its moral and spiritual commitments, it does not become neutral—it defaults to godlessness and secularism. **This is the primary snare of the Ugandan Education system.**

In an attempt to stay quiet about matters of religion to accommodate everyone, we have lost our way. We have a "national" curriculum body and a "national" examination board that both claim to focus on knowledge and skills, leaving questions of character and faith to families and religious entities. But is this separation practical?

Not only do parents lack the time to fill this crucial gap, but it is also wrong to assume schools are not actively—and often counteractively—shaping a child's character. **Are schools fairly and squarely staying in their box, sticking to Math and Science only?** How do you teach a child for 10+ years without forming their character and disposition?

Education is not "Formation OR Knowledge," nor is it both in random order. **Education is formation and knowledge, in that specific order.** The acquisition of both demands a simultaneous unity under caring, methodical hands.

The Ugandan school environment is far too greedy and immoral to handle this task. If head knowledge takes precedence, character formation becomes haphazard and unmonitored. until we recover a clear sense of education as *first* the formation of the child—their morals, disposition, and faith—Uganda’s education debates will continue to circle the surface, unable to name the true weight of the problem.

## **Order Without God Is Still Disorder**

As we analyze a secular system, one might ask, “But it is secular; how do you expect any good to come from it?” Is the system failing simply because man is sinful? While human depravity is real, we must think deeper.

Consider this: In a home where children are unregenerate, Christian parents will still hold high moral expectations. No parent excuses disorder by saying, "Well, my children were born sinful." Likewise, the state enforces laws on believers and unbelievers alike. While the human heart is depraved, God has ordained structures—family authority, discipline, and law—to restrain sin and maintain order. Even fallen societies can produce disciplined workers and strong economies if they respect these structures.

The Ugandan system fails not just because it is run by sinners. **It fails because it has abandoned the God-ordained structures that restrain chaos.** We have handed formation over to institutions never designed to carry it.

## **The Catastrophic Classroom: Time and Influence**

The failure is visible in our use of time. Authority follows time. While parents and churches get the margins, schools claim the child’s most alert, formative hours.  
*"Fathers, nurture them\!"* This is the biblical mandate to every parent. Yet, our education system has taken the lion's share of the child’s life. We accept that teaching Calculus is complicated, but **isn’t 10 hours a day an overkill?**

If these long hours took place in a natural setting, perhaps it would be bearable. But look at the reality:

1. **Isolation:** We take large herds of children—same age, same immaturity—and isolate them from the rest of the world and adult wisdom from 8am to 6pm.  
2. **The Vacuum:** We leave them with a "caring teacher" who cannot possibly form 50–200 children. They can only manage behavior.  
3. **Peer Rule:** In this vacuum, peer culture rules. Status is gained through rebellion and petty humor. The "wise" student is simply the notorious one who is never caught.

Some parents hope their children will be "missionaries" in these environments. Be warned, dear parents: **These environments wield higher formative power over a rising generation than years of counseling.** Do not be deceived. Bad company corrupts good morals.

## **Why "Christian" Schools Likewise Fail**

Even schools that claim a moral mandate—the so-called "Christian" institutions—often perpetuate this structural failure because they subscribe to the same faulty design.

The vast majority of Christian schools still subscribe to the secular national curriculum and pledge allegiance to its "neutral" examination board. All that changes is a chapel service here and there, or a prayer said right before evolution is taught as fact. **The structure remains misordered.** Time is still institutionalized, and formative authority is still outsourced.

The graduate of such a system is usually fluent in religious language but almost always lacks the discipline and endurance required to live out the faith they profess. You find "church boys" in their thirties refusing responsibility and family leadership. This is not a failure of Christianity, but of a structure designed to mass-produce weak-minded employees rather than raise sons and daughters in obedience to God.

**The system is not evil by intention, but by design.** Misordering what God has clearly designed in His Word always produces broken outcomes.

## **The Fear of the Lord Is the Beginning of Wisdom**

### **Time Reveals Loyalty**

Every day, each parent answers the question: *What deserves the best of my child’s time?* The tug-of-war is between knowledge/skills versus faith/character formation. **Prioritizing schooling over formation is a confession of loyalty.**

Ugandan parents understand that hunger and poverty are real. Knowledge and skills matter. But when moral and spiritual formation are consistently given less time than schooling, a decision has been made. Character has been ranked below competence. Systems have been trusted more than Scripture.

### **No Option for "Preference"**

One of the most dangerous assumptions a parent can make is that God left the question of child formation open to personal preference or cultural convenience. Scripture makes no such allowance.

* **Deuteronomy 6:** God commands parents to shape their children with His words *continually*—when sitting, walking, lying down, and rising. Formation is not a program; it is a way of living.  
* **Ephesians 6:** Fathers are explicitly addressed and held accountable. Children are to be *brought up*, not handed over.

Scripture is striking for what it *never* does. It never assigns this task to schools. It never treats institutions as moral guardians. It never excuses parents because the economy is tough. Parents will stand before God—not with the degrees they gave their children, but with the souls entrusted to them.

## **Wisdom First, Then Labor**

*"The fear of the Lord is the beginning of knowledge."* This is not a cliché; it is the basis for all who seek to educate.

Holistic education builds on this bedrock. When we prioritize formation, we must burn the midnight oil. We must spend time on our knees seeking God's help. **Obedience must come first, then we must labor to teach them everything else.**

After we prioritize formation, we then ask: *How will my child learn math and science? How will they write well?* We do not neglect these things. We pursue them with excellence *because* we serve a God of excellence. But we trust that in obeying God's order is where true blessedness rises.

The hope for Uganda does not begin with better schools, but with parents who fear God more than they fear poverty.

## **The Verdict**

The Ugandan education system is structurally incapable of fulfilling the biblical mandate to educate the rising generation. It attempts to sever the mind from the soul, inevitably causing an epidemic of godlessness and immorality.

We must choose this day whom we will serve. We cannot be wise in our own eyes. We must trust God’s ordering of the family above the state’s ordering of the school. The solution requires a radical reordering of our lives, our finances, and our time. We cannot rest until our children are fully formed—not just as workers for the economy, but as servants of the Most High.
