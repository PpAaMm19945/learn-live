# Band 2 Constraint Templates (Ages 6-9)
## Strand 3: Spatial Reasoning & Geometry

*Note: The constraint prompt is shown to the parent. The parent can use it to lead the task, or capture it for Async AI, or invoke Live AI.*

---

### G2a: 2D Shape Classification
**Prerequisite:** G1a (Shape Recognition)

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Sorting & Attributes |
| **Materials** | Paper cutouts of various polygons (regular, irregular, convex, concave) |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Give your child a pile of shapes. Say: 'Sort these into two piles: shapes with 4 sides (quadrilaterals) and all other shapes. Then pick one 4-sided shape and tell me what makes it special.'" |
| **Success** | Correctly sorts inclusive of irregular shapes. Identifies attributes like "it has 4 straight sides" or "it has 4 corners." |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Property identification |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x5):** "Show me a shape with exactly 3 sides and 1 square corner (right angle)."<br>**Endurance (x2):** "Draw a polygon with 5 sides. Now draw a different one with 5 sides." (Breaking the mental prototype of a regular pentagon). |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask your child to draw or find a shape that matches this rule: {Rule}. Does it have any curved lines?" |
| **Success** | Identifies and draws polygons based solely on verbal attribute lists rather than visual matching. |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Inclusive definitions |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Hold up a square. Say: 'Your friend says this is a square. I say it is a rectangle. Who is right?'" |
| **Success** | Understands hierarchy (a square is a special type of rectangle because it has 4 right angles and is a quadrilateral). |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Creation from constraints |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'Draw a shape that has 4 sides, but only two sides are the same length, and it has no right angles.' Can you name it?" |
| **Success** | Draws an irregular quadrilateral (e.g., a kite or specific trapezoid) fitting the constraints. |

### G2b: 3D Shape Recognition
**Prerequisite:** G2a (2D Shape Classification)

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Physical scavenger hunt |
| **Materials** | Household objects |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'Find 3 objects in the house that are cylinders. Find 1 that is a rectangular prism. How are they different when you roll them?'" |
| **Success** | Finds appropriate objects and articulates that prisms have flat faces and don't roll smoothly in all directions. |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Face, edge, vertex counting |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x5):** Give a 3D block. "Count the faces, edges, and vertices (corners)."<br>**Endurance (x2):** "A pyramid has a square base. How many faces does it have total? Can you figure it out without holding one?" |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Hand your child a {Shape}. Ask them to point to and count the edges. Watch out — do they count the same edge twice?" |
| **Success** | Develops a systematic way to count faces and edges without double-counting (e.g., top, bottom, then sides). |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | 2D to 3D mapping (Nets) |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Show a drawing of an unfolded box (a net of a cube). Ask: 'If I fold this up, will it make a perfect box with a lid, or will it overlap/have a hole?'" |
| **Success** | Mentally folds the 2D representation to verify if it forms a valid 3D shape. |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Construction |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'Use clay and toothpicks (or rolled paper) to build a triangular prism. How many toothpicks did you need? Why?'" |
| **Success** | Builds the skeleton model correctly, connecting the structural properties (9 edges) to the materials used. |

### G2c: Standard Measurement — Length
**Prerequisite:** G1e (Non-Standard Measurement), D1 (Place Value)

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Tools and units |
| **Materials** | A ruler with cm and inches |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Show the ruler. Ask: 'Why are the numbers on this side closer together than the numbers on the other side?'" |
| **Success** | Realizes that units can be different sizes, introducing the need to specify "centimeters" vs "inches". |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Accurate procedural measurement |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x5):** "Measure this book in cm. Measure this pencil."<br>**Endurance (x3):** "The ruler broke! It starts at the number 5 now. Measure this pencil using the broken ruler." |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask your child to measure {Object}. Watch closely: do they align the object with the 0 mark or the edge of the physical ruler?" |
| **Success** | Aligns correctly at zero. Handles the "broken ruler" by counting the intervals (finding the difference), not just reading the end number. |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Error detection & Unit appropriateness |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'Someone measured the height of our house and said it was 10 centimeters. Is that right? What unit should they have used?'" |
| **Success** | Identifies that centimeters are too small for a house, suggesting meters instead. |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Scale and estimation |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'Without using a ruler, find something in this room that you think is exactly 1 meter long. Then let's measure it to see how close you were.'" |
| **Success** | Develops a personal benchmark for a meter (e.g., "about the height of the doorknob") and uses it to estimate. |

### G2d: Standard Measurement — Mass & Capacity
**Prerequisite:** G2c (Measurement — Length)

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Physical comparison |
| **Materials** | Kitchen scale, various grocery items, measuring jug |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Give your child a large empty box and a small rock. 'Which one is bigger? Which one is heavier?'" |
| **Success** | Separates the concept of volume/size from mass/weight. |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Using tools and reading scales |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x4):** "Weigh this bag of rice in grams. Measure 500ml of water."<br>**Endurance (x3):** "The kitchen scale dial is between 400g and 500g, on the third little line. What is the weight?" |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask your child to measure {TargetAmount} of {Substance}. See if they can read the markings between the big numbers." |
| **Success** | Accurately reads analog scales, inferring the value of unlabelled graduation marks (e.g., each mark = 20g). |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Conservation of capacity |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Pour 200ml of water into a tall, skinny glass. Note the height. Pour that exact same water into a wide, flat bowl. Ask: 'Did the amount of water change? Why does it look different?'" |
| **Success** | Understands conservation (the volume doesn't change just because the shape of the container does). |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Problem solving with units |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'A recipe needs 1 kilogram of flour. We have a 400-gram bag and a 500-gram bag. Do we have enough? Prove it.'" |
| **Success** | Mixes numerical calculation with unit awareness (knows 1kg = 1000g, calculates 900g, concludes it's not enough). |

### G2e: Perimeter
**Prerequisite:** G2c (Standard Measurement — Length)

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Physical boundary walking |
| **Materials** | String, large floor shapes or rugs |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask your child to walk exactly on the edge of the living room rug. Say: 'If the rug is a field, you just walked the fence. That fence is the perimeter. How many baby steps long is the fence?'" |
| **Success** | Understands perimeter is the *outside edge*, distinct from the inside space. |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Additive calculation |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x5):** "Find the perimeter of this shape with sides: 4, 5, 4, 5."<br>**Endurance (x3):** "Find the perimeter of this regular pentagon. Only one side is labeled! (It's 6 cm)." |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Give your child a polygon where only some sides are labeled. Do they know how to find the missing sides if the shape is a rectangle or regular?" |
| **Success** | Uses properties of shapes (opposite sides of a rectangle are equal) to find missing side lengths, then adds them all up. |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Constant perimeter, changing shape |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Give them 12 toothpicks. Make a 3x3 square. The perimeter is 12 toothpicks. Ask: 'Can you make a long, skinny rectangle that also has a perimeter of 12?'" |
| **Success** | Realizes perimeter is a fixed length of boundary that can be bent into different shapes (e.g., 5x1 rectangle). |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Minimizing/Maximizing |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'We have 20 pieces of fence for a dog pen. Draw the pen that gives the dog the MOST room inside.' Let them experiment on grid paper." |
| **Success** | Discovers empirically that a square (5x5) gives more area than a long skinny shape (9x1) for the same perimeter. |

### G2f: Area — Conceptual
**Prerequisite:** G2a (2D Shape Classification)

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Tiling / Covering |
| **Materials** | Post-it notes or square tiles |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'Cover the front of this book completely with Post-it notes. No overlapping, no gaps. How many did it take? That's the area.'" |
| **Success** | Covers the surface completely and counts the units. |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Array calculation |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x5):** Give rectangles drawn on grid paper. "Count the squares inside."<br>**Endurance (x2):** "This rectangle is 3 squares wide and 5 squares long, but I didn't draw all the squares inside. Can you still tell me the area?" |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Watch if your child relates area to multiplication (3 rows of 5) instead of counting every single square one-by-one." |
| **Success** | Transitions from counting by 1s to skip counting or multiplying arrays. |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Area vs Perimeter confusion |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Show a 4x4 square (Area 16, Perimeter 16). Ask: 'Are area and perimeter always the exact same number? Let's check a 3x5 rectangle (Area 15, Perimeter 16).' What did you find?" |
| **Success** | Differentiates the properties: area is the inside space, perimeter is the outside edge. The numbers are usually different. |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Composite shapes |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Draw an L-shape on grid paper. Ask: 'How can you find the area of this? It's not a simple rectangle!'" |
| **Success** | Decomposes the L-shape into two rectangles, finds their areas separately, and adds them. |

### G2g: Line Symmetry
**Prerequisite:** G1d (Simple Symmetry)

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Physical folding / Mirrors |
| **Materials** | Paper shapes, small mirror |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Give your child a paper heart, star, and a random scribble shape. Ask: 'Which ones can you fold perfectly in half so the edges match?'" |
| **Success** | Creates a crease that acts as a line of symmetry perfectly. |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Drawing lines of symmetry |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x5):** "Draw all the lines of symmetry on this square. Now this rectangle."<br>**Endurance (x3):** "Here is half a drawing. The line down the middle is a mirror. Draw the other half." |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask your child to finish the 'mirror image' of a drawing on grid paper. Do they get the distances exactly right on the other side?" |
| **Success** | Accurately reflects shapes across a line. Identifies multiple lines of symmetry in regular polygons. |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | False symmetry |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Show a parallelogram. Ask: 'Does this have a line of symmetry? Try folding it.' People often think it folds corner-to-corner. Does it match?" |
| **Success** | Confirms through testing that a standard parallelogram has no lines of symmetry, despite "looking" balanced. |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Symmetrical design |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'Design a butterfly wing pattern. It must have exactly two lines of symmetry: one down the middle, and one straight across.'" |
| **Success** | Creates a complex pattern satisfying vertical and horizontal symmetry. |

### G2h: Directions & Simple Maps
**Prerequisite:** G1c (Positional Reasoning)

#### Cognitive Level 1: Encounter
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Egocentric vs Allocentric |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Stand facing your child. Point to your right arm. Say: 'Raise your right arm. Is it on the same side as mine? Why not?'" |
| **Success** | Realizes that direction is relative to the observer's orientation. |

#### Cognitive Level 2: Execute
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Grid navigation |
| **Repetition Arc** | **Execution (x5):** "On this map (grid), start at the House. Go North 3 blocks, East 2 blocks. Where are you?"<br>**Endurance (x2):** "Write the instructions to get from the School back to the House." |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Ask your child to act as the 'robot'. You give commands: Forward 3, Turn Right, Forward 2. Do they end up in the right spot? Now reverse roles." |
| **Success** | Follows and generates multi-step directional sequences accurately. |

#### Cognitive Level 3: Discern
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Optimizing paths |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Show a simple map. Say: 'Find two different paths from A to B. Is one path shorter? Prove it by counting the blocks.'" |
| **Success** | Compares path lengths and defines the most efficient route. |

#### Cognitive Level 4: Own
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task type** | Map making |
| **Parent Prompt** | "Say: 'Draw a map of your bedroom on grid paper. Make a key so I know what the shapes mean. Then write down how to get from the door to your bed in 'robot' language.'" |
| **Success** | Creates an abstract representation of a physical space, maintaining rough relative scale and position. |
| **Reasoning check** | "How does someone using your map know if they should take 2 steps or 10 steps? What tells them the distance?" |

## Formal Milestone Tasks — Strand 3 (All Capacities)

*Per Part VIe of the Spine: every milestone is a real-world situation with no mathematical vocabulary signalling the operation.*

---

### G2a Milestone: 2D Shape Classification
> **Situation:** "A carpenter is making a table. He tells his helper: 'Cut me a piece of wood with 4 straight sides, and all the corners must be perfectly square.' What shape is he asking for? Can it be more than one thing?"
>
> **Success:** Child identifies rectangle and/or square. Reasoning: explains that a square also has 4 straight sides and 4 square corners, so that also works.

### G2b Milestone: 3D Shape Recognition
> **Situation:** "We need a container to pack fragile glasses in so they arrive safely on the bus. We can use a round container (cylinder) or a box (cube). Which is better for packing glasses inside a larger crate? Why?"
>
> **Success:** Child chooses the box (cube) because it stacks without rolling and doesn't leave gaps. Does not need formal language, just spatial reasoning.

### G2c Milestone: Measurement — Length
> **Situation:** "Nala's mother is sewing her a dress. She needs to know how long the skirt should be. Her younger brother measures it using his handspan, and says it's 10 hands. Nala measures it using a string, and says it's 2 strings. The shopkeeper uses a measuring tape and says it's 50 centimetres. Why do they all have different numbers for the exact same skirt?"
>
> **Success:** Child explains that the tools/units are different sizes (hands vs string vs cm). Recognises the need for a standard unit (cm) so the tailor knows exactly what to do.

### G2d Milestone: Measurement — Mass & Capacity
> **Situation:** "You have a large bag of popped popcorn and a small bag of wet sand. Your grandmother says: 'Carry the heavy one for me.' Which one do you pick up?"
>
> **Success:** Child chooses the small bag of wet sand. Reasoning: understands that size/volume does not equal mass/weight.

### G2e Milestone: Perimeter
> **Situation:** "Amara wants to put a border of shiny ribbon completely around the edge of her rectangular drawing. How does she figure out exactly how much ribbon to cut from the roll before she starts gluing?"
>
> **Success:** Child explains measuring all four sides and adding the distances together (without needing the word 'perimeter').

### G2f Milestone: Area — Conceptual
> **Situation:** "Uncle Joseph has two rooms. One has floor tiles that are 4 tiles wide and 5 tiles long. The other room has tiles that are 3 tiles wide and 6 tiles long. He wants to sleep in the room with the most floor space. Which one should he choose?"
>
> **Success:** Child calculates 4x5=20 and 3x6=18, and recommends the first room. Shows understanding of area vs perimeter (if asked about perimeter, Room 1 is 18, Room 2 is 18 — equal perimeter, different area).

### G2g Milestone: Line Symmetry
> **Situation:** "You are making a paper aeroplane. You want it to fly perfectly straight. How should you fold the two wings?"
>
> **Success:** Child specifies folding them exactly the same so they mirror each other, achieving balance.

### G2h Milestone: Maps
> **Situation:** "Your friend from another neighborhood is coming to visit. She is at the big mango tree at the corner of your street. She has never been to your house. Tell her exactly how to walk to your front door from the tree."
>
> **Success:** Child provides a clear, ordered sequence of directions/turns relative to the friend's starting orientation, estimating distance (e.g., "Walk straight for 5 houses, then turn left").
